## Overview

Islam arrived in West Africa not at the point of a sword but along trade routes, carried by merchants whose commercial success demonstrated the practical benefits of belonging to the vast *umma* (community of believers) that stretched from Spain to Central Asia. Beginning in the eighth century CE, Muslim traders from North Africa and the Berber Sahara established commercial relationships with the peoples of the Sahel, creating a network of exchange that gradually drew West Africans into the Islamic world. Over the following centuries, Islam transformed from a foreign merchants' religion into a central feature of West African political, intellectual, and cultural life — yet this transformation was neither swift nor total. [E]

The story of Islam in West Africa is fundamentally a story of African agency. West African rulers, scholars, and merchants adopted Islam on their own terms, adapting it to local contexts while simultaneously reshaping their societies in response to Islamic ideals. The Ghana Empire maintained a sophisticated religious pluralism, hosting a thriving Muslim merchant quarter while the king practiced indigenous religion. The Mali Empire produced Mansa Musa, whose 1324 pilgrimage to Mecca announced West African Islam to the wider world with a display of wealth and piety that astonished Cairo. The Songhai Empire under Askia Muhammad I attempted the most systematic implementation of Islamic governance in the region, consulting with North African jurists and establishing Sharia courts. Each polity represented a different approach to the question of how African societies could engage with Islam while maintaining their own identities and institutions. [E]

Yet Islam's spread was never uniform or complete. Throughout this period, most West Africans — particularly those in rural areas, in the forest zones, and among the common population — continued to practice indigenous religions or blended Islamic observance with traditional beliefs and practices. The "Islamization" of West Africa was a centuries-long process that produced not a monolithic Muslim society but a complex religious landscape where Islam coexisted, competed, and merged with indigenous traditions. The tension between "scholars' Islam" (the rigorous practice of the learned elite) and "popular Islam" (the syncretic practices of ordinary believers) would remain a defining feature of West African religious life. Understanding this complexity — rejecting both the colonial-era dismissal of African Islam as "superficial" and the simplistic narrative of a single "conversion" event — is essential to grasping the actual history. [P]

## Arrival and Early Spread (~700-1000 CE)

### The Trans-Saharan Connection

Islam reached West Africa through the same routes that carried gold northward and salt southward: the trans-Saharan caravan trails that connected the Mediterranean world with the Sahel. These routes, traversing one of Earth's most hostile environments, had existed for centuries before Islam, but the Arab conquest of North Africa (completed by the early eighth century) transformed their religious significance. North African merchants and their Berber partners now carried not only trade goods but also a new faith. [E]

The chronology of Islam's earliest arrival in West Africa is imprecise. Muslim geographers and travelers begin mentioning the western Sudan in the ninth and tenth centuries, describing commercial relationships that must have existed for some time before being documented. The earliest mosques in the Sahel that can be dated with confidence appear in the ninth century, though earlier structures may have existed. What is clear is that by around 800-900 CE, Muslim merchant communities had established themselves at major trading centers throughout the region. [P]

These early Muslims in West Africa were primarily:

**North African Merchants:** Arab and Berber traders from the Maghreb who traveled south on commercial expeditions, some settling permanently in the Sahel trading towns. They brought goods, capital, and commercial expertise — as well as their faith.

**Berber Intermediaries:** The Sanhaja and other Berber peoples of the Sahara controlled the desert routes and the crucial oasis way-stations. Their gradual Islamization (accelerating in the tenth and eleventh centuries) meant that Islam became embedded in the very infrastructure of trans-Saharan commerce.

**Wangara/Dyula Merchants:** Mande-speaking traders, themselves converts to Islam, became the primary African intermediaries in the gold trade. The Wangara (Soninke) and later the Dyula (Juula) created extensive commercial networks that carried Islam far beyond the Sahel into the forest zones. [P]

### The Character of Early Islam

The Islam that arrived in West Africa during this early period was predominantly:

**Merchant Islam:** The first Muslims in the Sahel were traders, not missionaries. Their primary purpose was commercial, and their religious practice, while genuine, was not primarily directed toward converting others. The mosque functioned as a community center for Muslim merchants, a place of prayer and socialization for those far from home.

**Maliki Legal Tradition:** The Maliki school of Islamic jurisprudence, dominant in North Africa and Spain, became the standard throughout West Africa. This legal tradition, emphasizing the practices of the early Medinan community and allowing considerable flexibility in local customs that did not contradict Islamic principles, proved adaptable to West African contexts.

**Literate and Skilled:** Muslims brought literacy in Arabic, which proved useful for commercial record-keeping, diplomatic correspondence, and administrative tasks. African rulers who had no intention of converting nonetheless found Muslim scribes, interpreters, and treasurers valuable. [P]

### African Responses: Early Conversions

The earliest African conversions to Islam occurred primarily among:

**Traders and Commercial Elites:** Those who worked closely with Muslim merchants had both the opportunity and the incentive to convert. Membership in the *umma* facilitated commercial relationships across vast distances, providing access to networks of trust that transcended ethnic and linguistic boundaries.

**Urban Populations:** The trading towns of the Sahel — Gao, Tadmekka, Djenne, and later Timbuktu — became centers of Islamic practice. Urban life, with its cosmopolitan contacts and commercial orientation, was more conducive to religious change than rural villages.

**Royal Courts (Selectively):** Some rulers and court elites converted to Islam, though many more adopted a position of tolerance and accommodation without personal conversion. The kings of Gao and the rulers of Kanem were among the earliest royal converts. [P]

### The Ghana Model: Religious Pluralism

The Ghana Empire under rulers like Tunka Manin (r. c. 1062-1076) exemplified one African response to Islam: accommodation without conversion. As al-Bakri documented in 1068:

**The King's Religion:** The Ghana king practiced indigenous Soninke religion, performing the rituals and maintaining the sacred spaces that legitimized his rule among his own people. He could not convert without undermining the religious foundations of his authority.

**The Muslim Quarter:** At the same time, Ghana hosted a thriving Muslim community in a separate town near the royal capital, complete with twelve mosques and its own legal and religious infrastructure.

**Muslims in Government:** The king employed Muslim scribes and interpreters, using their skills without adopting their faith. The treasurer and the interpreter at the Ghana court were Muslims.

**The Logic of Coexistence:** This arrangement served everyone's interests: the king maintained traditional legitimacy; Muslim merchants had the institutions they needed; trade flourished because both communities could operate within their own frameworks. [E]

This "two-city" model — Islamic and indigenous spheres coexisting under one political authority — represented a stable equilibrium that could persist for generations. It was neither a failed conversion nor a rejection of Islam, but a sophisticated arrangement for managing religious diversity in a commercial state. [P]

## Patterns of Islamization

### Royal Conversion vs. Popular Islam

Throughout West African history, Islamization typically proceeded from the top down: rulers and urban elites converted first, while rural populations and commoners maintained indigenous practices for much longer. This pattern produced a characteristic social distribution of religious practice:

**Royal Courts:** Rulers who converted (the kings of Kanem, later the mansas of Mali, the askias of Songhai) used Islam as a legitimizing framework, patronizing scholars, building mosques, and making the pilgrimage to Mecca. But even Muslim rulers often maintained elements of indigenous royal ritual, and their subjects were not forced to convert.

**Urban Centers:** Trading towns like Timbuktu, Gao, Djenne, and Kano developed substantial Muslim populations, with mosques, schools, and legal institutions. Urban Islam was relatively rigorous and connected to the wider Islamic world.

**Rural Areas:** The majority of West Africans lived in villages, practiced agriculture or pastoralism, and remained largely outside the formal Islamic sphere. Indigenous religions persisted in rural areas throughout the period, often long after nominal royal conversion.

**The "Conversion" Question:** When historians speak of a kingdom "converting" to Islam, they typically mean that the ruler and court elite adopted Islamic practice. Popular conversion, if it occurred at all, was a much slower process extending over centuries. [P]

### Gradual Accommodation

Islamization in West Africa rarely involved a single, dramatic conversion event. Instead, the process typically unfolded through:

**Generation 1:** Initial contact through trade. Muslims present as foreign merchants; local populations observe their practices with curiosity but little adoption.

**Generation 2-3:** Muslims become established residents. Some local traders and urban dwellers convert for commercial advantage. Muslim skills (literacy, administration) prove useful.

**Generation 4-5:** Royal or elite conversion, often for political or diplomatic reasons. Islam becomes associated with prestige and connection to the wider world. But indigenous practices continue alongside Islamic observance.

**Generation 6+:** Gradual expansion of Islamic practice beyond the elite, but never achieving uniformity. Syncretic practices emerge as Islam merges with indigenous traditions. [S]

### The "Mixed Islam" Question

Arabic sources frequently criticized West African Muslims for practices they considered un-Islamic. Ibn Battuta, visiting Mali in 1352-53, was scandalized by:

- Women appearing unveiled before men
- Court rituals involving masked dancers
- Practices at royal audiences that seemed to mix Islamic and indigenous elements
- The general "mixing" (*ikhtilat*) of Islamic and non-Islamic customs

These observations have led some scholars (particularly in the colonial era) to characterize West African Islam as "superficial" or incomplete. This framing reflects external standards rather than historical understanding. A decolonizing approach recognizes that:

**Islam is always localized:** Every Muslim society adapts Islamic practice to local cultural contexts. Arabian, Persian, Turkish, South Asian, and Southeast Asian forms of Islam all differ; West African Islam is not uniquely "syncretic."

**The scholarly/popular divide exists everywhere:** The gap between learned, rigorous religious practice and popular, accommodationist practice appears in every religious tradition. West African Muslims were not unusually lax; they were typical.

**African agency in adaptation:** West Africans deliberately chose which elements of Islam to adopt, adapt, or reject. This was not passive corruption but active cultural creativity. [P]

## Islam in the Ghana Empire (~800-1200 CE)

### The Two-City System

Al-Bakri's 1068 account of the Ghana capital describes a sophisticated arrangement for religious coexistence:

**The Royal City (El-Ghaba):**
- Where the king held court and lived
- Named for the sacred grove surrounding it
- Center of indigenous religious practice
- Site of royal ancestral tombs and shrine complexes
- Restricted religious space closed to Muslims

**The Muslim Town (approximately 10 km distant):**
- Where Muslim merchants and residents lived
- Twelve mosques, including one for Friday prayers
- Imams, muezzins, and Islamic jurists (*fuqaha*) in residence
- Stone and acacia-wood construction
- A complete Islamic community with its own legal infrastructure

This spatial separation was not segregation in the modern sense but a deliberate architecture that allowed both communities to maintain their religious integrity while participating in a common political and economic system. [E]

### Muslims at Court

Despite the king's personal adherence to indigenous religion, Muslims played important roles at the Ghana court:

**The Interpreter:** Al-Bakri specifically mentions that the king's interpreter was Muslim. This official managed communication with Arabic-speaking merchants, diplomats, and travelers — a crucial function for a state dependent on trans-Saharan trade.

**The Treasurer:** Also Muslim, according to al-Bakri. The management of Ghana's gold revenues required literacy and numeracy; Muslim administrative skills made them natural choices for financial offices.

**Diplomatic Functions:** When Ghana needed to communicate with North African states or Arab merchants, Muslims served as intermediaries who could bridge the cultural and linguistic gap.

This arrangement demonstrates that Islamic skills could serve a non-Islamic state without threatening its religious foundation. The king used Muslims; he did not need to become one. [E]

### The Almoravid Factor

The Almoravid movement, arising among Sanhaja Berbers of the western Sahara in the mid-eleventh century, introduced a more militant, reform-oriented Islam to the region:

**The Almoravid Program:** Under the jurist Ibn Yasin, the Almoravids sought to purify Islamic practice and extend Muslim rule. They captured key Saharan trading towns (Audaghost in 1054) and eventually expanded into Morocco and Spain.

**Pressure on Ghana:** Whether the Almoravids militarily conquered the Ghana capital (traditionally dated to 1076) is [Debated]. What is clear is that Almoravid control of northern trade routes and Saharan oases disrupted Ghana's commercial position and intensified Islamic pressure on the region.

**The Question of Forced Conversion:** Some sources suggest that Almoravid pressure led to royal conversion or submission. The evidence is thin, and modern scholarship tends toward a more complex picture: Ghana was weakened by Almoravid disruption without necessarily experiencing a single conquest event.

**Long-term Impact:** Whether or not a military conquest occurred, the Almoravid era marked an acceleration of Islamization in the western Sahel. By the twelfth century, the region was more thoroughly integrated into the Islamic world than it had been in al-Bakri's time. [D]

## Islam in the Mali Empire (~1235-1500 CE)

### Sundiata and the Ambiguity of Origins

The Mali Empire's founding presents a characteristic ambiguity regarding Islam. Sundiata Keita, who defeated Sumanguru Kante at the Battle of Kirina (c. 1235) and established Mali's dominance, appears in oral tradition in various guises:

**In Some Versions:** Sundiata is a Muslim hero whose victory represents Islam's triumph over Sumanguru's sorcery. He is depicted as pious, perhaps even as having received divine assistance.

**In Other Versions:** Sundiata defeats Sumanguru through mastery of indigenous *nyama* (spiritual power), using the magical weapons and protective charms that his griot and mother provided. His victory is achieved through Mande spiritual tradition, not Islamic piety.

**Historical Assessment:** The oral tradition's ambiguity probably reflects Sundiata's actual position: a ruler who may have been nominally Muslim (or whose lineage had Muslim connections) but who operated primarily within indigenous Mande religious frameworks. The founding epic of a "Muslim" empire is rooted in pre-Islamic cosmology. [T]

This ambiguity was not necessarily a contradiction. A West African ruler could participate in both Islamic and indigenous religious systems, drawing legitimacy from multiple sources. The later characterization of the Mali dynasty as thoroughly Muslim may represent a retrospective Islamization of the founding narrative. [P]

### Mansa Musa: Islam as Imperial Ideology

Mansa Musa's reign (c. 1312-1337) marked the full emergence of Islam as Mali's official imperial ideology. His 1324-1325 pilgrimage to Mecca announced Mali's wealth and piety to the wider Islamic world:

**The Hajj as Political Act:** Musa's pilgrimage was not merely personal devotion but state policy. He traveled with thousands of followers, enormous quantities of gold, and the full regalia of a great sovereign. The hajj demonstrated that Mali was a wealthy, powerful, pious Muslim state deserving recognition in the Islamic world.

**The Cairo Encounter:** Musa's passage through Egypt left a profound impression. Al-Umari's informants, interviewed within about fifteen years of the event, described Musa's generosity, piety, and sovereign dignity. He refused to prostrate fully before the Mamluk sultan, insisting on his own royal status.

**The Gold Distribution:** Musa gave away so much gold in Cairo that he reportedly depressed the gold price for over a decade — evidence of both Mali's wealth and Musa's religious generosity (charitable giving is a pillar of Islam).

**Bringing Back Scholars:** Musa returned to Mali with scholars, architects (notably Abu Ishaq al-Sahili), and books. He was not merely displaying his existing piety but actively importing Islamic learning and expertise. [E]

### The Building of Timbuktu

Under Mansa Musa's patronage, Timbuktu was transformed from a trading post into a center of Islamic learning:

**Mosque Construction:** The Djinguereber Mosque, traditionally attributed to al-Sahili's design, became one of Timbuktu's three great mosque-universities. Musa invested in creating the physical infrastructure for Islamic scholarship.

**Scholarly Patronage:** Musa invited scholars from North Africa and the wider Islamic world to Mali, offering them positions, salaries, and respect. The scholarly community that would later produce figures like Ahmad Baba had its foundations in Musa's patronage.

**Manuscript Culture:** The book trade flourished under Musa's reign and continued thereafter. Timbuktu became a center not only for Islamic learning but for manuscript production and circulation. Hundreds of thousands of manuscripts from this tradition survive today.

**The Sankore Tradition:** The Sankore mosque (which may predate Musa but flourished during and after his reign) became particularly associated with advanced Islamic scholarship, drawing students from across West Africa. [E]

### Ibn Battuta's Critique

Ibn Battuta's visit to Mali in 1352-53 (during the reign of Mansa Sulayman, Musa's successor) provides an eyewitness account of Malian Islam — though filtered through the traveler's own expectations:

**What Ibn Battuta Praised:**
- Malians' devotion to prayer and regular attendance at mosques
- Their commitment to memorizing the Quran (children might be chained until they completed their memorization)
- The maintenance of public order and safety
- The elaborate Islamic ceremonies at court

**What Ibn Battuta Criticized:**
- Women's freedom of association with men (not veiling, mixing freely)
- Female slaves and servants appearing naked before the sultan
- Court ceremonies that included masked dancers performing before the ruler
- The mixing of Islamic and non-Islamic practices at royal audiences

Ibn Battuta's account reveals the tension between scholarly expectations of "proper" Islam and Malian cultural realities. His criticisms reflect his own Maghrebi standards, not necessarily deficiencies in Malian piety. His praise, often overlooked in favor of his complaints, demonstrates that Malian Islam was genuine and substantial even if it did not conform to foreign norms. [E]

### Mali's Religious Complexity

The Mali Empire under the Keita dynasty was Muslim but not monolithically so:

**Court Islam:** The mansas were publicly Muslim, making the hajj, building mosques, patronizing scholars, and conducting Islamic ceremonies. Islam was the official religion of the court and the legitimizing framework for imperial authority.

**Continued Indigenous Practice:** Elements of pre-Islamic Mande religion persisted at court. Royal rituals, succession ceremonies, and certain court practices drew on indigenous traditions. The balance varied by ruler; some were more rigorously Islamic than others.

**Popular Religion:** The majority of Mali's subjects, particularly in rural areas, continued indigenous practices. Islam was the religion of the elite and the cities, not (yet) of the entire population.

**Regional Variation:** Mali's vast extent meant that Islamization varied by region. The Niger Bend cities were more thoroughly Islamized than peripheral areas. Forest-zone tributaries maintained indigenous religions.

This complexity was not failure but adaptation — an African approach to integrating Islam into existing social and political structures. [P]

## Islam in the Songhai Empire (~1464-1591 CE)

### Sunni Ali: The Religious Controversy

Sunni Ali (r. 1464-1492) built the Songhai Empire through military conquest — capturing Timbuktu (1468), conquering Djenne after a lengthy siege (traditionally seven years), and extending Songhai control across the Middle Niger. His relationship with Islam became one of the most contested questions in West African historiography:

**The Chronicles' Portrayal:** The *Tarikh al-Sudan* and *Tarikh al-Fattash*, written by Timbuktu scholars, portray Sunni Ali as hostile to Islam, a persecutor of the ulama, and a practitioner of indigenous religion. They describe him as cruel, impious, and deserving of condemnation.

**The Revisionist View:** Modern historians note that these chronicles were written by scholars whom Sunni Ali may have politically marginalized. The "persecution" they describe may reflect political conflict (the king limiting scholarly autonomy) rather than religious hostility. Sunni Ali was almost certainly a Muslim; the question is what kind of Muslim.

**The Likely Reality:** Sunni Ali probably practiced both Islam and indigenous Songhai religion, as many West African rulers did. He may have clashed with the Timbuktu scholarly establishment over political authority rather than theological principle. His "impiety" consisted of not deferring to the scholars' vision of proper Islamic governance.

**Confidence Level:** [D] — The chronicles' bias is clear, but some kernel of genuine religious tension likely underlies their account. [D]

### Askia Muhammad's Coup: Islam as Revolution

Askia Muhammad I's seizure of power in 1493 was explicitly framed as a religious act:

**The Justification:** When Sunni Baru, Sunni Ali's son, succeeded his father and reportedly refused to make a clear profession of Islam, Muhammad Ture (a general of Soninke origin) raised rebellion in the name of true religion. The coup at the Battle of Anfao was presented as a religious revolution, not a mere power grab.

**The Political Function:** Whether Muhammad's motives were purely religious is unknowable. What is clear is that claiming Islamic legitimacy served his political needs. As a non-royal usurper, he needed a legitimizing framework that transcended hereditary succession. Islam provided it.

**The Scholarly Alliance:** Muhammad allied with the Timbuktu scholarly establishment that had opposed Sunni Ali. In return for their support (and for writing favorable accounts of his seizure of power), he would patronize their institutions and defer to their authority on religious matters.

The coup established a pattern: Islamic legitimacy could justify the overthrow of rulers deemed insufficiently pious, and scholarly support was worth cultivating. [E]

### The Hajj and Caliphal Investiture (1496-1497)

Only three years after seizing power, Askia Muhammad made the pilgrimage to Mecca:

**Political Calculation:** The hajj addressed Muhammad's legitimacy problem. As a usurper without royal blood, he needed religious sanction. Walking to Mecca and returning with caliphal blessing would transform him from a successful general into God's appointed ruler.

**The Abbasid Connection:** In Cairo, Muhammad sought and obtained an audience with the Abbasid caliph (the shadow caliphate maintained by the Mamluks). The caliph invested him with authority over the Sudan, granting him the title "Caliph of Takrur" or "Caliph of the Sudan."

**The Return Transformed:** Muhammad returned to Songhai with religious credentials that even his detractors could not easily dismiss. He was now a hajji (pilgrim), invested with caliphal authority, the legitimate Islamic sovereign of West Africa.

Whether the Abbasid caliph's investiture carried real authority in Islamic legal theory mattered less than its political effect. Muhammad had successfully converted religious performance into political legitimacy. [E]

### Al-Maghili's Influence

Muhammad al-Maghili, the North African jurist who had earlier advised Muhammad Rumfa in Kano, provided Askia Muhammad with detailed guidance on Islamic governance:

**The Questions Muhammad Asked:**
- How should religious syncretists (those mixing Islam with indigenous practice) be treated?
- What is the proper relationship between ruler and scholars?
- How should markets and commerce be regulated according to Islamic law?
- When is jihad (religious war) obligatory?

**Al-Maghili's Answers:** The jurist provided rigorous, sometimes uncompromising responses rooted in Maliki jurisprudence. He urged Muhammad to enforce Islamic law strictly, suppress syncretic practices, and establish proper qadi courts.

**Implementation:** How fully Muhammad implemented al-Maghili's recommendations is uncertain. The correspondence represents ideals that the Askia aspired to; the realities of governing a diverse empire likely required more flexibility than the jurist's treatises envisioned.

**Long-term Influence:** Al-Maghili's treatises circulated for centuries, shaping West African political thought. Reformers in later centuries (including, well after our period, the Sokoto jihad leaders) cited his authority. [E]

### Timbuktu's Scholarly Peak

Under the Askia dynasty, Timbuktu reached its apogee as a center of Islamic learning:

**The Three Mosques:** Djinguereber, Sankore, and Sidi Yahia functioned as mosque-universities where scholars taught the full range of Islamic sciences: Quranic exegesis, hadith (prophetic traditions), fiqh (jurisprudence), Arabic grammar, logic, and more.

**The Scholarly Families:** Certain lineages (the Aqit family, the ancestors of Ahmad Baba, and others) dominated Timbuktu's intellectual life, passing knowledge and libraries from generation to generation. These families formed a scholarly aristocracy with substantial social prestige.

**The Student Population:** Young men came from across West Africa to study with Timbuktu's masters. Completion of study with a recognized scholar earned an ijaza (certificate) that validated one's own authority to teach.

**The Manuscript Libraries:** Private libraries accumulated thousands of manuscripts. Books became valuable commodities, copied, bought, sold, and treasured. The survival of hundreds of thousands of manuscripts from this tradition represents one of Africa's great intellectual heritages.

**Ahmad Baba al-Timbukti (1556-1627):** The most celebrated Timbuktu scholar, whose career extended beyond our period, represented the culmination of this tradition. His exile to Morocco following the 1591 Moroccan conquest symbolized the destruction of Songhai's intellectual achievement. [E]

## Islam in the Hausa States (~1000-1600 CE)

### Early Spread

Islam reached Hausaland through trans-Saharan trade routes, probably by the eleventh century if not earlier:

**Trade Connections:** The Hausa city-states (Kano, Katsina, Zaria, Gobir, and others) lay at the eastern end of the Sahel trading network, connected by caravan routes to Tripoli, the Fezzan, and ultimately the Mediterranean.

**Merchant Islam:** As in the western Sahel, the earliest Muslims in Hausaland were primarily traders. Muslim merchant quarters developed in the major cities, providing the nucleus for later Islamization.

**Royal Conversion:** The Kano Chronicle, our principal source for early Hausa history, records gradual Islamization of the Kano royal house, traditionally beginning with Yaji (r. c. 1349-1385), though earlier contacts existed. Other Hausa states followed their own timelines. [T]

### Muhammad Rumfa's Reforms

Muhammad Rumfa (r. c. 1463-1499) of Kano undertook the most systematic pre-sixteenth-century reform of Hausa governance along Islamic lines:

**The Kano Chronicle's List:** The Chronicle credits Rumfa with twelve major innovations (*gina gari*), including:
- Establishment of the Kurmi market (a major commercial center)
- Building of the city walls
- Appointment of eunuch officials
- Consultation with the jurist al-Maghili
- Various other administrative and ceremonial reforms

**Al-Maghili's Guidance:** Muhammad al-Maghili visited Kano during Rumfa's reign and composed the *Taj al-Din fima Yajib ala al-Muluk* ("Crown of Religion Concerning the Obligations of Princes"), a treatise on Islamic governance tailored to Rumfa's questions.

**The Questions Rumfa Asked:**
- What qualifies a legitimate Muslim ruler?
- How should officials be appointed?
- How should religious syncretists be treated?
- What constitutes legitimate taxation?
- When is jihad obligatory?

**Implementation:** Rumfa reportedly established Sharia courts, appointed Islamic judges, and reformed state institutions according to al-Maghili's guidance. The *Taj al-Din* became a foundational text for political thought in Hausaland. [P]

### Kano as Islamic Center

By the sixteenth century, Kano had developed into a major center of Islamic learning:

**Mosques and Schools:** The city boasted substantial mosques and a scholarly community, though never rivaling Timbuktu in intellectual prestige.

**Commercial Islam:** Kano's identity remained closely tied to commerce. Islam facilitated the long-distance trade networks that sustained the city's prosperity.

**The Hausa-Borno Connection:** Kano and other Hausa states maintained complex relationships with Kanem-Bornu to the east, sometimes paying tribute, sometimes independent. Islamic scholarship flowed through these connections.

**Queen Amina of Zazzau:** The legendary warrior queen Amina (traditionally dated to the late sixteenth century), though not primarily a religious figure, ruled a Hausa state that was at least nominally Islamic. Her campaigns of expansion occurred within an Islamic political framework. [P]

## Islam in Kanem-Bornu (~1000-1600 CE)

### Early Conversion

Kanem, centered on the Lake Chad basin, has one of the longest histories of royal Islam in West Africa:

**The Traditional Date:** Oral tradition credits Mai Hume (or Hawwa), a queen or queen mother, with the introduction of Islam to the Kanem ruling house, possibly as early as the late eleventh century. The chronology is uncertain, but royal Islamization in Kanem predates that of most West African states.

**The Sefuwa Dynasty:** The ruling dynasty of Kanem (and later Bornu) maintained Islamic identity as a central feature of their legitimacy for centuries. The mais (kings) styled themselves as Muslim sovereigns, made pilgrimages, and patronized Islamic scholarship.

**Eastern Connections:** Kanem's location gave it strong connections to eastern trans-Saharan routes leading to Tripoli, Egypt, and ultimately Mecca. These connections shaped Kanem's distinctive Islamic identity. [P]

### Mai Dunama Dibbalemi

Mai Dunama Dibbalemi (r. c. 1210-1248) exemplified the Kanem monarchs' Islamic commitment:

**Pilgrimage:** Dunama made the hajj to Mecca, establishing a pattern that many of his successors would follow. Kanem's rulers were notable for their pilgrimage tradition.

**Diplomatic Connections:** Dunama reportedly established a hostel in Cairo for Kanem pilgrims, demonstrating the kingdom's integration into the wider Islamic world.

**The Mune Symbol:** Dunama allegedly opened the sacred *mune* (a traditional Kanem royal symbol), an act that later tradition blamed for subsequent misfortunes. This story suggests tension between Islamic and indigenous legitimizing frameworks. [T]

### The Bulala Wars and the Move to Bornu

The fourteenth and fifteenth centuries saw Kanem weakened by conflicts with the Bulala people, eventually forcing the Sefuwa dynasty to relocate westward to Bornu (around Lake Chad's western shores):

**Continuity of Islam:** Despite political turmoil, Islamic identity remained central to Sefuwa legitimacy. The move to Bornu did not represent a religious rupture.

**New Capital:** Ngazargamu became the Bornu capital, developing into a significant urban center with mosques and scholarly activity.

**Integration with Hausaland:** In Bornu, the Sefuwa increasingly interacted with the Hausa states to the west, sometimes exerting overlordship, sometimes competing. [P]

### Idris Alooma's Reign

Idris Alooma (r. c. 1564-1596) brought Kanem-Bornu to its height and implemented substantial Islamic reforms:

**Military Innovation:** Idris imported firearms (arquebuses) from North Africa and employed Turkish military advisors, making Bornu one of the first sub-Saharan states to use gunpowder weapons.

**Pilgrimage:** Like his predecessors, Idris made the hajj, reinforcing Bornu's Islamic identity and connections to the wider Muslim world.

**Islamic Reforms:**
- Established Sharia courts
- Appointed qadis (Islamic judges)
- Patronized mosque construction
- Attempted to enforce stricter Islamic practice

**The *Masufawa*:** Our knowledge of Idris Alooma's reign comes partly from a chronicle written by his imam, Ahmad ibn Fartua. This court chronicle presents Idris as an ideal Islamic ruler, though with the biases inherent in court-sponsored literature.

**Confidence Level:** [E] for the reign's basic facts; the chronicle's portrayal of religious fervor should be read with awareness of its purpose. [P]

## Schools, Traditions, and Scholarly Networks

### Maliki Dominance

Throughout West Africa, the Maliki school of Islamic jurisprudence was virtually unchallenged:

**Origins:** The Maliki school, founded on the teachings of Malik ibn Anas (d. 795), emphasizes the practice of the early Medinan community and the *amal* (customary practice) of Medina as sources of law alongside the Quran and hadith.

**Why Maliki?** The school's dominance in North Africa and Spain meant that Muslims entering West Africa through trans-Saharan networks were predominantly Maliki. No competing legal tradition gained significant foothold.

**Practical Implications:** Maliki jurisprudence shaped the specifics of Islamic practice: prayer, fasting, marriage, inheritance, commercial transactions, and criminal law were all interpreted through Maliki categories. A West African Muslim seeking legal guidance would receive Maliki answers.

**Flexibility:** The Maliki tradition's emphasis on *amal* (customary practice) allowed some accommodation of local customs that did not directly contradict Islamic principles, though the extent of permissible accommodation was debated. [E]

### Sufi Orders: The Qadiriyya

Sufi Islam — the mystical, devotional tradition emphasizing direct experience of the divine — entered West Africa primarily through the Qadiriyya order:

**Origins:** The Qadiriyya, named for the Baghdad mystic Abd al-Qadir al-Jilani (d. 1166), spread across the Islamic world, reaching the Maghreb and thence to West Africa.

**Character:** Qadiriyya Sufism emphasized:
- The veneration of saints (*awliya*) as friends of God
- The importance of a spiritual guide (sheikh) for the seeker
- Dhikr (remembrance of God) through repetitive prayer formulas
- The possibility of direct divine connection beyond mere legal observance

**Spread in West Africa:** By the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, Qadiriyya affiliations were present among West African scholars, though the order's formal institutional structures were less developed than they would become later.

**Accommodation with Local Religion:** Sufi emphasis on saints and spiritual power may have facilitated accommodation between Islam and indigenous religious frameworks. The concept of holy men with special spiritual access resonated with African traditions of religious specialists.

**Tijaniyya:** The Tijaniyya order, which would become extremely important in West Africa, arrived later, largely after 1600, and is thus mostly outside this project's scope. [P]

### Scholarly Networks

West African Islamic scholarship was embedded in networks that transcended political boundaries:

**Chains of Transmission (*Isnad*):** Scholars documented their intellectual lineages — who they had studied with, and who their teachers had studied with — creating chains of authority reaching back to the Prophet and his companions.

**The Ijaza System:** A student who completed study with a recognized master received an *ijaza* (certificate) authorizing them to teach that material to others. This system created an informal but powerful accreditation structure.

**Timbuktu as Hub:** Timbuktu functioned as the central node in West African scholarly networks. Scholars from across the region studied there, and Timbuktu-trained scholars dispersed to teach elsewhere.

**North African Connections:** West African scholars maintained connections with the Maghreb, Egypt, and the Hijaz. Some studied abroad; others corresponded with foreign scholars; still others received visitors like al-Maghili.

**The Book Trade:** Manuscripts circulated through commercial networks. Books were valuable commodities, copied by professional scribes, traded alongside other goods, and treasured in family libraries. [E]

## Coexistence with Indigenous Religion

### The Pattern of Syncretism

Throughout the period, Islam coexisted with indigenous religious practices in complex ways:

**Elite vs. Popular Practice:** The rigorous Islam of scholars and rulers differed from the "mixed" Islam of ordinary people, who might combine Quranic recitation with consultation of indigenous diviners, or wear Quranic amulets alongside traditional protective charms.

**Urban vs. Rural:** Cities were more thoroughly Islamized than villages. Rural populations maintained indigenous practices longer and more openly.

**Public vs. Private:** Even in Islamized contexts, some indigenous practices continued in private or domestic settings while public religious life was Islamic.

**The "Two Registers" Model:** Many West Africans operated in two religious registers simultaneously, participating in Islamic observances (Friday prayer, Ramadan fasting, the use of Arabic blessings) while also maintaining indigenous religious practices (ancestor veneration, consultation of diviners, participation in traditional ceremonies).

This was not hypocrisy or superficiality but a pragmatic approach to religious multiplicity. Different religious frameworks addressed different needs; combining them maximized access to spiritual resources. [P]

### Specific Syncretic Practices

Ibn Battuta and other observers noted practices that blended Islamic and indigenous elements:

**Quranic Amulets:** Wearing or carrying passages from the Quran as protective amulets was (and remains) widespread. This practice has Islamic precedent but also resonated with indigenous traditions of protective charms.

**Marabouts and Diviners:** The marabout (the West African Muslim religious specialist) combined Islamic learning with functions similar to traditional diviners and healers. Some marabouts incorporated clearly non-Islamic practices into their repertoire.

**Royal Ceremonies:** Even Muslim courts often maintained elements of indigenous royal ritual. The masked dancers at the Mali court that scandalized Ibn Battuta probably represented some form of this integration.

**Ancestral Veneration:** The Islamic principle that the dead cannot help the living stood in tension with widespread African practices of ancestor veneration and offerings. In practice, many compromises emerged.

**Spirit Possession:** In some areas, spirit possession cults (with roots in indigenous religion) coexisted with Islamic practice, sometimes incorporating Islamic terminology or concepts. [P]

### The Scholars' Critique

West African scholars were well aware of syncretic practices and debated how to respond:

**The Al-Maghili Position:** Jurists like al-Maghili advocated strict distinction between Islam and non-Islam. Those who mixed Islamic and indigenous practices should be treated as apostates or unbelievers, called to repentance or subjected to harsher measures.

**The Accommodationist Position:** Other scholars adopted more flexible approaches, focusing on maintaining core Islamic obligations while tolerating practices that did not directly contradict them.

**Political Realities:** Rulers who wished to implement strict Islamic standards faced the practical problem of governing populations that were not ready to abandon indigenous practices. Even Askia Muhammad, who consulted al-Maghili, could not fully implement the jurist's rigorous recommendations.

**The Ongoing Tension:** The tension between purist and accommodationist approaches to popular religion was never resolved during this period (or since). It would drive later reform movements, including, centuries later, the jihads of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. [P]

## The Timbuktu Scholarly Tradition

### The City as Intellectual Center

By the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, Timbuktu had developed into one of the great centers of Islamic learning in the world:

**The Physical Setting:** The city's location at the Niger Bend, where the river approaches closest to the Saharan caravan routes, made it a natural commercial and cultural crossroads. Its sandy streets, mud-brick buildings, and three great mosques became synonymous with African Islamic civilization.

**The Three Mosques:**
- **Djinguereber:** Built under Mansa Musa's patronage (traditionally attributed to al-Sahili), the oldest of the three great mosques, associated with congregational Friday prayer.
- **Sankore:** Particularly associated with advanced scholarship, functioning as a university where the highest levels of Islamic learning were pursued.
- **Sidi Yahia:** The third of the great mosque-universities, completing the triad that made Timbuktu a comprehensive center of learning.

**The Student Population:** Estimates of students in Timbuktu vary wildly (some contemporary sources give figures of 25,000 or more), but the city unquestionably attracted substantial numbers of young men seeking Islamic education. They came from across West Africa and occasionally beyond.

**The Curriculum:** Students studied the Quran, hadith, Maliki jurisprudence, Arabic grammar and rhetoric, logic, theology, and sometimes supplementary subjects like astronomy and medicine. The curriculum was similar to that of Islamic institutions worldwide, rooted in the same classical texts. [E]

### Ahmad Baba al-Timbukti

Ahmad Baba (1556-1627) represented the culmination of the Timbuktu scholarly tradition. Though his career extended beyond the conventional end date of this project, he merits mention:

**Background:** Born into a scholarly family (the Aqit), Ahmad Baba received a thorough education in Islamic sciences and emerged as Timbuktu's most celebrated scholar.

**Works:** Ahmad Baba authored numerous works on Islamic law, biography, and other subjects. His biographical dictionary of Maliki scholars (*Nayl al-Ibtihaj*) remains an important reference.

**The Moroccan Exile:** Following the Moroccan conquest of Songhai in 1591, Ahmad Baba was arrested and exiled to Marrakesh, where he spent years in captivity and semi-captivity before eventually returning to Timbuktu. His exile symbolized the destruction of Songhai's intellectual achievement.

**His Judgment on Slavery:** Ahmad Baba wrote an important treatise (*Mi'raj al-Su'ud*) on the Islamic law of slavery, arguing against the enslavement of Muslims and raising questions about the legality of enslaving various African peoples. This work demonstrates the engagement of West African scholars with pressing ethical questions.

**Legacy:** Ahmad Baba became a symbol of Timbuktu's intellectual tradition, revered by later generations and invoked as evidence of African scholarly achievement. [E]

### The Manuscript Heritage

The Timbuktu scholarly tradition produced an enormous body of manuscripts:

**Scope:** Hundreds of thousands of manuscripts from this tradition survive today, preserved in institutional collections and private family libraries in Timbuktu and across Mali.

**Contents:** The manuscripts cover the full range of Islamic sciences — Quranic commentary, hadith, jurisprudence, theology, Sufism, grammar, poetry, history — as well as practical subjects like medicine, astronomy, and mathematics.

**The Family Libraries:** Many manuscripts remained in family hands, passed from generation to generation as treasured heirlooms. These private collections, often poorly catalogued and vulnerable to environmental damage, represent an enormous and still partially unexplored archive.

**Modern Preservation:** Since the late twentieth century, various projects have worked to catalogue, preserve, and digitize the Timbuktu manuscripts. This work continues despite security challenges.

**Historical Significance:** The manuscripts demonstrate that West Africa produced a genuine intellectual tradition, not a provincial echo of Middle Eastern learning but a creative engagement with Islamic scholarship that addressed local questions and made original contributions. [E]

## Architecture: Mosques and the Sahelian Tradition

### The Sahelian Style

West African mosque architecture developed a distinctive style adapted to local materials and climate:

**Mud-Brick Construction:** The scarcity of stone and the abundance of clay dictated construction in mud brick (*banco* or adobe). Buildings required regular maintenance (replastering) to prevent erosion but proved well-suited to the climate.

**Wooden Armature:** Permanent wooden beams (*toron*) projected from walls, serving as scaffolding for maintenance and as visual decoration. These projecting beams became a signature element of Sahelian architecture.

**Organic Forms:** Without the sharp edges of stone construction, Sahelian buildings developed flowing, organic shapes — curved walls, rounded corners, tapered towers. The effect was of buildings that seemed to grow from the earth.

**Climatic Adaptation:** Thick mud walls provided thermal mass, keeping interiors cool during hot days and retaining warmth during cool nights. The architecture was exquisitely adapted to the Sahel's harsh climate.

**Height and Towers:** Mosques featured distinctive towers (minarets) and elevated structures, rising above the surrounding buildings to announce Islamic presence and provide vantage for the call to prayer. [E]

### Al-Sahili and the Question of Innovation

Abu Ishaq al-Sahili, the Andalusian architect whom Mansa Musa brought back from his hajj, is traditionally credited with introducing architectural innovations to West Africa:

**The Traditional Claim:** Al-Sahili supposedly introduced fired-brick construction, new architectural techniques, and a more sophisticated aesthetic to West African building.

**The Scholarly Debate:** Modern scholars debate how much al-Sahili actually innovated versus how much he adapted existing Sahelian traditions. Fired brick existed in Africa before al-Sahili; mud-brick construction had its own sophisticated techniques.

**A Balanced View:** Al-Sahili probably brought design sensibilities and perhaps some technical refinements from the Islamic Mediterranean, but he worked within and built upon existing West African traditions. The great mosques of Timbuktu, Djenne, and Gao represent a fusion of influences, not an imposition of foreign forms.

**The Djenne Mosque:** The Great Mosque of Djenne (the current structure dates from 1907, rebuilt on an earlier site) exemplifies the mature Sahelian style — the massive mud-brick walls, the projecting torons, the organic curves, the soaring towers. Whether or not al-Sahili influenced the original, the mosque demonstrates the power and beauty of the indigenous architectural tradition. [D]

### Mosques as Social Centers

West African mosques functioned not merely as places of prayer but as comprehensive social institutions:

**Education:** Mosques housed the schools where children learned Quranic recitation and where advanced students studied Islamic sciences. The mosque courtyard, cool and shaded, was the classroom.

**Law Courts:** Qadis (Islamic judges) typically conducted their business at or near the mosque, making it a center of legal administration.

**Community Gathering:** The mosque courtyard served as a meeting place, a site for announcements, a venue for community decisions.

**Commercial Proximity:** Markets often developed near major mosques, making them economic as well as religious centers.

**Charitable Distribution:** Mosques served as distribution points for zakat (alms) and other charitable giving.

The mosque was thus the heart of the Islamic urban community — not separate from daily life but integrated into all its aspects. [P]

## For Fiction Writing

### Prayer and Daily Practice

**Fiction Potential: HIGH** — The rhythms of Islamic practice provide natural structure for narrative, marking time and revealing character through devotional observance.

**The Five Daily Prayers (Salat):**
- **Fajr (Dawn):** Before sunrise. The muezzin's call breaks the darkness; the faithful rise from sleep.
- **Dhuhr (Noon):** When the sun passes its zenith. Markets pause; work stops.
- **Asr (Afternoon):** Mid-afternoon, when shadows lengthen.
- **Maghrib (Sunset):** Immediately after the sun sets. The most precisely timed prayer.
- **Isha (Night):** After darkness falls.

**The Call to Prayer (Adhan):** The muezzin's voice echoing across the city — "Allahu Akbar" (God is greatest), "Hayya 'ala al-salah" (Come to prayer) — is one of the most evocative sounds of the Islamic world. From Timbuktu's three minarets, the calls would overlap and echo.

**Ablution (Wudu):** Before prayer, Muslims wash hands, face, arms, and feet in a prescribed ritual of purification. The act of cleansing prepares body and mind for approaching the divine.

**The Physical Prayer:** Standing, bowing, prostrating, sitting — the prayer movements are precise and universal. A West African praying alongside a visitor from Egypt or Arabia would perform the same movements, recite the same Arabic words.

**Character Revelation:** How characters perform (or neglect) their prayers reveals their piety, their social position, their internal struggles. A man who rushes through prayers differs from one who prays with deliberate care; one who avoids the mosque differs from one who arrives early.

### Religious Festivals

**Ramadan:** The holy month of fasting. From dawn to sunset, Muslims abstain from food, drink, and other appetites. The month's rhythm — hungry days, celebratory evenings, communal meals after sunset (*iftar*) — provides rich atmospheric material.

**Eid al-Fitr:** The festival marking Ramadan's end. Celebration, feasting, gift-giving, new clothes, communal prayer. A city transformed from the austerity of fasting to the joy of completion.

**Eid al-Adha:** The festival of sacrifice, commemorating Abraham's willingness to sacrifice his son. Animals are slaughtered; meat is distributed to family, friends, and the poor. Occurs during the hajj season, connecting West Africans to the pilgrims in Mecca.

**Mawlid (Prophet's Birthday):** Celebrated with varying degrees of enthusiasm. Poetry praising the Prophet, processions, gatherings. Some scholars considered it an innovation; others embraced it.

### Scholarly Life

**The Madrasa:** The school, often in a mosque courtyard, where students sat cross-legged around their teacher, listening, memorizing, discussing. The scratch of reed pens on paper; the drone of students reciting in unison.

**The Scholar's Day:** Morning teaching, study, composition; afternoon teaching, consultations, writing; evening prayer, family, further study. A life organized around the pursuit and transmission of knowledge.

**Books as Treasures:** Manuscripts were valuable objects — beautifully copied, bound in leather, passed down in families. A scholar's library was his legacy and his tool. The loss of books (through fire, theft, or the disasters of war) was mourned like the loss of kin.

**Scholarly Debates:** Jurists disagreed; scholars argued. The tradition was not monolithic but constantly contested. A narrative could depict scholars in passionate disputation, defending their interpretations, challenging their rivals.

### Pilgrimage

**The Hajj Journey:** Leaving home for a journey of months or years, crossing the Sahara, arriving in the holy cities, performing the ancient rituals, returning transformed. The pilgrim who departed was not the same person who returned.

**The Saharan Crossing:** The terrifying desert, the oases like paradise, the threat of raiders, the discipline of the caravan. The physical ordeal that tested pilgrims before they reached the sacred.

**Mecca:** The Kaaba draped in black, the circling pilgrims (*tawaf*), the rush between the hills (*sa'i*), the standing at Arafat. The pilgrims from every nation, all equal before God in their white ihram garments.

**The Returned Pilgrim (Hajji):** The title, the prestige, the white cloth worn as a sign. A hajji was respected; their advice carried weight; their witness to the holy places gave them authority.

### Religious Debates and Tensions

**Scholars vs. Rulers:** The tension between religious authority and political power — scholars insisting on their autonomy, rulers demanding obedience. Al-Maghili advising Askia Muhammad; Ahmad Baba resisting Moroccan authority.

**Purists vs. Accommodationists:** The tension between those who demanded strict Islam and those who tolerated popular practices. A character might struggle between the rigorous demands of their scholarly training and the everyday realities of the society around them.

**Indigenous Religion's Persistence:** The old ways did not simply disappear. Characters might be torn between Islamic practice and the pull of ancestral traditions — the diviner their grandmother trusted, the sacred grove where their family had always made offerings.

### Sensory Details of Islamic West Africa

**Sound:**
- The adhan (call to prayer) echoing across the city, from multiple minarets in overlapping succession
- Students chanting Quranic verses in unison
- The Friday sermon (khutba), the preacher's voice carrying across the assembled congregation
- The drums that might still mark royal ceremonies, mixing with Islamic soundscapes

**Sight:**
- The great mosques rising above mud-brick cities, their towers studded with projecting wooden beams
- Scholars in flowing white or blue robes, turbans wound elaborately
- Manuscripts open on low writing desks, the angular beauty of Arabic script
- Prayer beads (misbaha) threaded through fingers during contemplation

**Smell:**
- Incense at formal gatherings, the smoke rising toward high ceilings
- The leather binding of old manuscripts
- The special foods of religious festivals

**Texture:**
- The cool interior of a mud-brick mosque, the hard earth floor beneath kneeling worshippers
- The rough weave of the prayer mat
- Paper and pen, the flow of ink across the page

## Scholarly Debates

### Depth of Islamization

**The Central Question:** How "Muslim" was West Africa during this period?

**Position A (Superficial Islam):** Older scholarship, particularly from the colonial era, characterized West African Islam as superficial, incomplete, or corrupted by indigenous practices. This view held that true Islamization came only later (or not at all).

**Position B (Genuine Islamization):** Modern scholarship rejects the "superficial" characterization, recognizing that:
- Islam is always adapted to local contexts everywhere
- The gap between scholarly and popular practice is universal in religious traditions
- West African Islam was genuine, substantial, and creative

**Current Consensus:** [D] — The question is poorly framed. West Africa was genuinely Islamized, but "Islamization" should not be understood as total uniformity or elimination of all non-Islamic elements. The appropriate comparison is not with some abstract ideal but with actual Muslim societies elsewhere, which also exhibit variation and syncretism.

### "Pagan Survivals"

**The Question:** How should indigenous religious elements in West African Islam be characterized?

**Colonial-Era Framing:** Older scholarship spoke of "pagan survivals" — indigenous practices that had not yet been eliminated by Islam, implying a teleology toward pure Islam.

**Decolonizing Reframing:** Modern scholarship rejects this framing, preferring terms like "religious synthesis," "accommodation," or "African Islam." The persistence of indigenous elements was not failure but creative adaptation.

**Practical Debate:** Among Muslims themselves (both in the period and since), the question of which practices are acceptably Islamic and which are impermissible innovations or syncretism was and remains actively debated. Al-Maghili and his intellectual descendants took strict positions; others were more accommodating.

### Elite vs. Popular Religion

**The Pattern:** Across West Africa, Islam was typically more rigorous among elites (rulers, scholars, merchants) than among common people, and more established in cities than in rural areas.

**Scholarly Debate:** Some scholars emphasize the elite character of pre-modern West African Islam, arguing that popular religion remained essentially indigenous with an Islamic veneer. Others argue that this distinction is overdrawn, that Islam penetrated more deeply than elite-focused sources suggest, and that the boundary between "elite" and "popular" religion was more porous than often assumed.

**Current Consensus:** [P] — The elite/popular distinction was real but not absolute. Islam was more rigorous at the top, but it was not absent at the bottom. The process of popular Islamization was ongoing throughout the period. [P]
