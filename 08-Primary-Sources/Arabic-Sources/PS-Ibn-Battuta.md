## Overview

Ibn Battuta's *Rihla* stands as the most important eyewitness source for medieval Africa and one of the most remarkable travel accounts in world literature. Over nearly thirty years (1325-1354), this Moroccan legal scholar traveled an estimated 75,000 miles across the Islamic world and beyond, from Morocco to China, from the Russian steppe to sub-Saharan Africa. His account, dictated after his return to Morocco and shaped into literary form by the court scholar Ibn Juzayy, provides irreplaceable testimony about societies, customs, rulers, and landscapes across three continents. For African history specifically, Ibn Battuta's descriptions of the Mali Empire and the Swahili Coast are foundational texts — without them, our understanding of these civilizations would be impoverished beyond measure. [E]

Ibn Battuta visited Africa twice during his travels. In 1331, during his journey from Arabia toward India, he traveled down the East African coast, visiting Mogadishu, Mombasa, and Kilwa on the Swahili Coast. His descriptions of these cities — particularly his account of Kilwa as "one of the most beautiful and well-constructed towns in the world" — provide the earliest detailed eyewitness accounts of Swahili Coast urban civilization. Twenty years later, in 1352-1353, Ibn Battuta undertook his final major journey: crossing the Sahara Desert to visit the Mali Empire, the greatest power in medieval West Africa. His eight-month sojourn at the court of Mansa Sulayman produced the most detailed account we possess of any medieval African kingdom — descriptions of court ceremonies, religious practices, social customs, and daily life that have no parallel in any other source. [E]

The *Rihla* must be approached with both appreciation and critical awareness. Ibn Battuta was a trained legal scholar, an observant traveler, and a vivid writer, but he was also a product of his time and culture. His judgments reflect the standards of a Moroccan Muslim jurist: he praised what accorded with his understanding of proper Islamic practice and criticized what deviated from it. His famous complaints about practices at the Mali court — women appearing unveiled, the persistence of indigenous religious customs, what he perceived as inadequate generosity — reveal as much about his expectations as about Mali society. Modern scholarship reads Ibn Battuta critically, recognizing his biases while valuing his irreplaceable testimony. [E]

The *Rihla* also presents textual complexities. The work was not written by Ibn Battuta himself but dictated to Ibn Juzayy, who shaped the material into literary form and may have added embellishments. Some sections of the *Rihla* appear to borrow from other travel accounts, raising questions about what Ibn Battuta actually saw versus what he incorporated from other sources. The African sections, however, are among the most securely attributed to genuine personal experience — Ibn Battuta's descriptions of the Sahara crossing, his encounters at the Mali court, and his observations of Swahili cities bear the marks of authentic eyewitness testimony. [P]

## The Author

### Biography

Abu Abdullah Muhammad ibn Battuta was born in Tangier, Morocco, on February 24, 1304 (17 Rajab 703 AH), into a family of legal scholars. His family belonged to the Luwata Berber tribe and had produced qadis (Islamic judges) for several generations. Ibn Battuta received a traditional Islamic education, studying the Quran, Arabic grammar, and the Maliki school of Islamic jurisprudence that predominated in the Maghreb. By the standards of his society, he was being prepared for a career as a legal scholar and judge — a respectable but not extraordinary future. [E]

In June 1325, at the age of twenty-one, Ibn Battuta departed Tangier on hajj — the pilgrimage to Mecca that every Muslim is obligated to perform if able. This departure, unremarkable in itself (thousands of Moroccans made the hajj each year), marked the beginning of an extraordinary odyssey that would last nearly thirty years and take Ibn Battuta across virtually the entire Islamic world and beyond. [E]

What began as a pilgrimage became a life of continuous travel. After performing the hajj in 1326, Ibn Battuta did not return home but continued traveling — to Iraq, Persia, the Arabian Peninsula, the East African coast, Anatolia, the Golden Horde in southern Russia, Constantinople, Central Asia, India, the Maldives, Ceylon, Southeast Asia, and China. His motivations appear to have been a combination of restlessness, curiosity, ambition, and the opportunities that the integrated Islamic world offered to a trained legal scholar. Wherever he went, he could find employment as a qadi or as an advisor to Muslim rulers; his expertise in Maliki jurisprudence was valued from Delhi to the Maldives. [E]

Ibn Battuta's longest sojourn was in India, where he served as qadi of Delhi under Sultan Muhammad ibn Tughluq from 1334 to 1341. This position brought him wealth, status, and danger — the sultan was notoriously unpredictable, and Ibn Battuta witnessed (and sometimes barely escaped) the executions of officials who displeased him. His departure from India as ambassador to the Mongol court of China was partly an escape from this volatile environment. [E]

After visiting China (the extent and authenticity of his Chinese travels are debated), Ibn Battuta finally returned to Morocco in 1349. He then made a brief journey to al-Andalus (Muslim Spain) and, in 1352, undertook his final major expedition: the crossing of the Sahara Desert to the Mali Empire. He returned to Morocco in late 1353 or early 1354, concluding nearly three decades of travel. [E]

The last fifteen years of Ibn Battuta's life were spent in Morocco, where he served as a qadi in various towns. The Marinid sultan Abu Inan commissioned him to dictate an account of his travels, producing the *Rihla* that would become his legacy. Ibn Battuta died in Morocco around 1368-1369, leaving behind the most extensive travel account of the medieval period. [E]

### Intellectual Formation

Ibn Battuta's education and intellectual outlook shaped everything he observed and recorded:

**Maliki Jurisprudence:** Ibn Battuta was trained in the Maliki school of Sunni Islamic law, the dominant legal tradition in the Maghreb and West Africa. This training gave him expertise that was valued across the Islamic world — he could serve as a judge wherever Maliki law was followed. It also provided the lens through which he evaluated societies: he consistently judged places and peoples by their adherence to what he considered proper Islamic practice. [E]

**The Rihla Tradition:** Ibn Battuta was not the first Muslim traveler to write about his journeys. The *rihla* (travel account) was an established literary genre, and Ibn Battuta would have been familiar with earlier examples. His own account both follows and exceeds the conventions of the genre. [P]

**Sufi Influences:** Ibn Battuta frequently visited Sufi saints and participated in Sufi practices during his travels. He venerated holy men, recorded miracles attributed to them, and sought their blessings. This Sufi orientation shapes his account, which includes many descriptions of shrines, saints, and mystical experiences. [E]

**Curiosity and Observation:** Whatever his formal training, Ibn Battuta possessed genuine curiosity about the peoples and places he visited. He recorded details — of food, clothing, customs, ceremonies — that had no legal or religious significance but that provide invaluable ethnographic information. This observational capacity makes his account far more valuable than a purely legalistic or religious text would have been. [E]

## The Composition of the *Rihla*

### Dictation and Authorship

The *Rihla* as we have it is not a straightforward autobiographical account written by Ibn Battuta himself. The text was produced through a collaborative process that raises important questions about authorship:

**The Commission:** In 1354, the Marinid sultan Abu Inan ordered Ibn Battuta to dictate an account of his travels. The sultan assigned Ibn Juzayy (Muhammad ibn Juzayy al-Kalbi, c. 1321-1356), a court scholar and accomplished littlateur, to record and edit the account. [E]

**Ibn Juzayy's Role:** Ibn Juzayy was more than a scribe. He shaped Ibn Battuta's oral recollections into polished literary Arabic, organized the material, added poetic quotations and learned references, and provided a preface and conclusion. The extent of his intervention is debated — did he merely polish Ibn Battuta's words, or did he substantially rewrite and supplement them? [D]

**The Preface:** Ibn Juzayy's preface explicitly states that he "wrote out [Ibn Battuta's account] in such a way as would make its purpose clear and facilitate understanding, with due attention to the principles of style and elegance of expression." This acknowledgment of literary intervention should make readers cautious about treating every turn of phrase as Ibn Battuta's own words. [E]

**Completion:** Ibn Juzayy died in 1356, apparently having completed the work. The *Rihla* survives in a single basic manuscript tradition, though with some textual variations among copies. [E]

### The Question of Authenticity

The *Rihla* presents a significant scholarly challenge: how much of it can be trusted as genuine eyewitness testimony?

**Sections Under Suspicion:**

Several portions of the *Rihla* appear to borrow from other sources rather than recording Ibn Battuta's own observations:

1. **China:** The Chinese sections of the *Rihla* contain geographical errors and descriptions that closely parallel earlier accounts. Many scholars believe Ibn Battuta did not actually visit China, or that he visited only briefly and supplemented his account with material from other sources. [D]

2. **Certain Middle Eastern Sections:** Some descriptions of places in Syria and elsewhere appear to derive from earlier geographers rather than personal observation. [D]

3. **The Bulgar Journey:** Ibn Battuta's claimed visit to Bulgar (on the Volga River) in winter 1333 is geographically and chronologically problematic, leading some scholars to question whether this journey occurred as described. [D]

**Sections Generally Accepted:**

Other portions of the *Rihla* bear the unmistakable marks of genuine personal experience:

1. **The African Sections:** Ibn Battuta's descriptions of the Sahara crossing and the Mali court contain vivid personal details (his annoyance at his reception, his interactions with specific individuals, his complaints about local customs) that are difficult to attribute to literary borrowing. Similarly, his Swahili Coast descriptions, while brief, contain specific observations that suggest direct experience. [P]

2. **India:** His lengthy account of India, including his service under Sultan Muhammad ibn Tughluq, is detailed, internally consistent, and corroborated by other sources. [E]

3. **Arabia and the Hajj:** His descriptions of Mecca, Medina, and the pilgrimage routes are detailed and credible. [E]

### The *Rihla* as Literature

The *Rihla* should be understood not just as a historical source but as a work of literature shaped by generic conventions:

**The Rihla Genre:** Muslim travel literature (*rihla*) was an established genre with its own conventions. Travelers were expected to record certain types of information: descriptions of cities and rulers, accounts of holy men and saints, observations of customs and marvels. Ibn Juzayy would have shaped Ibn Battuta's material to meet these expectations. [P]

**The "Wonders" Convention:** The full title of the work emphasizes "wonders" (*'aja'ib*) — marvels that amazed and entertained readers. This emphasis may have led to exaggeration or the inclusion of hearsay as witnessed fact. Readers were expected to enjoy tales of exotic marvels, and the text accommodates this expectation. [P]

**Self-Presentation:** Ibn Battuta presents himself consistently as a pious, learned Muslim who is welcomed by rulers, sought out by scholars, and blessed by saints. This self-presentation is partly autobiographical but also partly conventional — travelers in the *rihla* tradition typically emphasized their piety and the respect they received. [P]

## African Journeys

### The Swahili Coast (1331)

In 1331, during his journey from Arabia toward India, Ibn Battuta traveled down the East African coast, visiting the major trading cities of the Swahili littoral. This journey, though brief, produced the earliest detailed eyewitness account of Swahili urban civilization.

**Route and Chronology:**

Ibn Battuta sailed from Aden (in modern Yemen) down the East African coast, visiting:

1. **Zeila** (in modern Somalia) — briefly noted as a town with an unpleasant smell from fish and camels [E]
2. **Mogadishu** — described in considerable detail as a wealthy trading city [E]
3. **Mombasa** — noted as a large island with extensive gardens; the people described as "Shafi'is in religion and pious, upright, and virtuous" [E]
4. **Kilwa** — described as "one of the most beautiful and well-constructed towns in the world" [E]

The visit occurred during the sailing season dictated by the monsoon winds — the northeast monsoon (November-March) carried ships from Arabia down the African coast, while the southwest monsoon (April-September) would return them northward. Ibn Battuta's visit fits this pattern. [E]

**Duration:**

Ibn Battuta's time on the Swahili Coast was limited. He was not undertaking an extended study but passing through en route to India. His observations, while valuable, reflect brief visits of days or at most weeks to each city. [P]

### The Mali Empire Journey (1352-1353)

Ibn Battuta's journey to Mali was his final major expedition and produced his most extensive African testimony.

**Motivation:**

The *Rihla* does not explain why Ibn Battuta, now in his late forties and finally settled in Morocco after decades of travel, chose to undertake the dangerous Sahara crossing. Possible motivations include:

- **Sultan Abu Inan's Interests:** The Marinid sultan may have wanted intelligence about Mali, with which Morocco had diplomatic relations
- **Scholarly Curiosity:** Having traveled virtually everywhere else in the Islamic world, Mali was one of the few major Muslim lands Ibn Battuta had not visited
- **Professional Opportunity:** Mali's wealth and reputation might have offered opportunities for a scholar of Ibn Battuta's experience
- **Restlessness:** Perhaps Ibn Battuta simply could not stop traveling [S]

**Route:**

Ibn Battuta's route to Mali followed the established trans-Saharan caravan tracks:

1. **Sijilmasa** (February 1352) — The departure point in southern Morocco, the northern terminus of the western trans-Saharan routes. Ibn Battuta spent several weeks here preparing for the journey. [E]

2. **Taghaza** (25 days from Sijilmasa) — The salt mines in the central Sahara, where rock salt was quarried and loaded onto camels. Ibn Battuta describes Taghaza as a village "with nothing good about it" whose buildings were constructed entirely of rock salt and whose inhabitants were enslaved people who mined salt. [E]

3. **Walata** (Oualata) — A Mali border town and trading center. Ibn Battuta arrived after the arduous desert crossing and was deeply disappointed by the hospitality he received, which he considered beneath his dignity. [E]

4. **The Mali Capital** — After resting at Walata, Ibn Battuta traveled to the Mali capital (probably in the Niani region, though the exact location is [D] Debated). Here he spent approximately eight months at the court of Mansa Sulayman. [E]

5. **Timbuktu** — Ibn Battuta visited Timbuktu on his return journey, though his description of the city is brief (suggesting it had not yet achieved the prominence it would under Songhai rule). [E]

6. **Return Journey** — Ibn Battuta crossed the Sahara back to Morocco, arriving in late 1353 or early 1354. [E]

**Duration:**

The entire Mali journey lasted approximately two years (February 1352 to late 1353/early 1354). Of this, perhaps eight months were spent at the Mali capital, with the remainder consumed by travel to and from the empire. [E]

## Mali Empire Content

Ibn Battuta's account of the Mali Empire is the most detailed eyewitness description of any medieval West African kingdom. His observations cover the court, religion, society, and daily life of Mali under Mansa Sulayman.

### Mansa Sulayman's Court

Ibn Battuta provides extensive descriptions of court ceremonies and the mansa's public appearances:

**The Royal Audience (Bembé):**

The mansa held regular public audiences that Ibn Battuta describes in vivid detail:

> "The sultan has a raised cupola (*qubba*) which is entered from inside his house. He sits in it a great part of the time... There is a door from it to the place of audience, which has three wooden arches covered with plates of silver and under these three others covered with plates of gold... The sultan comes out of a door in the corner of the palace, bow in hand, his quiver on his back, on his head a cap of gold bound with a band of gold that has narrow ends like knives, more than a span in length. He frequently wears a robe of red velvet made by European factories (*mutanfas*). In front of him march singers carrying gold and silver *qanabir* [a type of instrument] and behind him come about three hundred armed slaves." [E]

This description captures the ceremonial grandeur of the Mali court — the gold and silver ornamentation, the imported European textiles, the military escort, the musical accompaniment.

**The Dust-Sprinkling Custom:**

Ibn Battuta was particularly struck by the Mande practice of sprinkling dust upon one's head as a sign of respect to the mansa:

> "If one of them [the subjects] addresses the sultan, he removes his clothes and puts on worn-out garments, takes off his turban and puts on a dirty skullcap, and goes in raising his clothes and trousers half-way up his shin-bones. He advances with humility and abjection, knocks the ground with his elbows vigorously, and stands, like one who is performing a *rak'a* [a prayer position], listening to the sultan's words. If one of them speaks to the sultan and the latter replies, he takes his garments off his back and sprinkles dust on his head and back, like one washing himself with water." [E]

This custom, which Ibn Battuta found bewildering, was a standard element of Mande political culture — a ritual demonstration of submission that had nothing to do with actual hygiene or dress. Ibn Battuta's puzzlement reveals the cultural gulf between his expectations and Mande practices.

**The Dugha's Performance:**

Perhaps the most famous passage in Ibn Battuta's Mali account describes the dugha (master of ceremonies and court musician):

> "The interpreter Dugha comes with his four wives and about a hundred slave girls with them, wearing beautiful robes, on their heads bands of gold and silver with gold and silver apples as pendants. A chair is placed for Dugha and he plays on an instrument made of reeds with some small calabashes at its lower end, and chants a poem in praise of the sultan, recalling his battles and deeds of valor. The women and girls sing along with him and play with bows. Accompanying them are about thirty youths, wearing red woolen tunics and white skull-caps; each of them has his drum slung from his shoulder and beats it. Afterwards come his boy pupils who play and turn wheels in the air like the natives of Sind. They show a marvelous nimbleness and agility in this exercise and play most cleverly with swords." [E]

This description is invaluable for understanding Mande court culture — the centrality of music and praise-singing, the elaborate ceremonial, the combination of religious occasion with entertainment and display.

### Ibn Battuta's Reception

Ibn Battuta's account of his own reception at Mali is notable for his complaints:

**The Initial Disappointment:**

After introducing himself to the mansa through the proper channels, Ibn Battuta received his first gift from the royal court:

> "After a little while he [the mansa] had a gift sent to me, consisting of a load of bread and meat fried in *shea butter* and a gourd with yogurt... I thought he was making fun of me when I saw this petty hospitality, for when I had been in the countries of the infidels I received more hospitality than this." [E]

This complaint is characteristic of Ibn Battuta's accounts — he expected lavish treatment befitting his scholarly status and was mortified to receive what he considered paltry hospitality.

**The Complaint and Resolution:**

Ibn Battuta eventually complained about his treatment through the court interpreter:

> "I stayed [waiting] fifty-five days, then the interpreter took me to the audience... I addressed the sultan: 'I have traveled to the countries of the world and met their kings. I have been in your country four months without your giving me a reception-gift or anything else. What shall I say of you before other sultans?' He replied: 'I have not seen you nor heard of you.' The qadi and the preacher [who were present] said to him: 'He has greeted you and you have sent him food.' He thereupon ordered that a house be allotted to me and an allowance be fixed for me." [E]

This episode reveals both Ibn Battuta's sense of entitlement and the functioning of Mali's court bureaucracy — visitors were processed through intermediaries, and the mansa did not personally receive every foreign scholar.

### Social Observations

Beyond the court, Ibn Battuta recorded observations about Mali society more broadly:

**Praise for Justice and Security:**

Despite his complaints about hospitality, Ibn Battuta praised Mali's legal system and public security:

> "The Negroes possess some admirable qualities. They are seldom unjust, and have a greater abhorrence of injustice than any other people. Their sultan shows no mercy to anyone who is guilty of the least act of it. There is complete security in their country. Neither traveller nor inhabitant in it has anything to fear from robbers or men of violence." [E]

This praise is particularly significant because it comes from a critic. Ibn Battuta disapproved of many aspects of Mali society, but he could not deny its effective governance.

**Criticism of Customs:**

Ibn Battuta was scandalized by several Mali customs:

> "Among their bad customs are the following: Their women servants, slave-girls, and young daughters appear before people naked, with their pudenda uncovered... Another of their bad customs is that their women enter in the presence of the sultan naked and unveiled, and that his daughters too go naked... Another [bad custom] is that many of them eat carrion [unclean meat] and dogs and donkeys." [E]

These criticisms reflect Ibn Battuta's own cultural standards rather than objective deficiencies. The practices he condemned were normal within Mande culture; his shock reveals the limits of his cultural relativism.

**Religious Practices:**

Ibn Battuta provides detailed observations of Islamic practice at Mali:

> "They are careful to observe the hours of prayer, and assiduous in attending them in congregations, and in bringing up their children to them. On Fridays, if a man does not go early to the mosque, he cannot find a corner to pray in, on account of the crowd... They are extremely careful about their prayers, and about memorizing the Quran. If their children fall short of learning it by heart they impose shackles on them and do not remove them until they have learned it." [E]

This description shows a society that took Islamic practice seriously, at least among the urban elite. The dedication to Quranic education impressed even the critical Ibn Battuta.

### Geographic Information

Ibn Battuta provides geographic information about his journey through Mali:

**The Niger River:**

Ibn Battuta describes the great river, though he (like most contemporary observers) believed it to be a branch of the Nile:

> "I saw the Niger, which goes to Timbuktu, at Kabara, which is close to Timbuktu. I saw it also at Muli and also at Kawkaw [Gao]. The Nile descends from there to Nubi [Nubia] and Egypt." [E]

This geographical error — connecting the Niger to the Nile — persisted in European geography for centuries.

**Cities and Towns:**

Ibn Battuta mentions several towns and cities, including:

- **Walata** — the border town where he first entered Mali
- **The Capital** — whose exact location remains debated
- **Timbuktu** — described briefly on his return journey
- **Gao** — the Songhai town that would later eclipse Mali

His descriptions suggest a functioning urban network connected by the Niger River and overland routes.

## Swahili Coast Content

Ibn Battuta's descriptions of the Swahili Coast, though briefer than his Mali account, are equally important — they provide the earliest detailed eyewitness testimony about these East African trading cities.

### Mogadishu

Ibn Battuta describes Mogadishu as a large and wealthy city:

> "Mogadishu is a town of vast size. The people are merchants rich in wealth. They possess many camels, slaughtering a hundred or more every day, and many sheep. The people [of Mogadishu] are corpulent, and every one of them eats as much as a whole group of other people would, for this is their custom... When a vessel arrives [in harbor], it is boarded from the sanbuq [sailing boat] by young men of the town, each one of whom brings a covered dish containing food, and presents it to one of the merchants on the ship, saying: 'This is my guest.' And each merchant, when he disembarks, goes only to the house of his host among the young men." [E]

This description captures the organized hospitality system by which Mogadishu merchants secured trading partners — a sophisticated commercial arrangement that facilitated Indian Ocean trade.

### Mombasa

Mombasa receives a briefer description:

> "We then sailed to Mombasa, a large island with two days' journey by sea between it and the Swahili country. The island has no mainland [territory]. It has fruit trees and its produce is bananas. They do not engage in agriculture but import grain from the Swahili. The greater part of their food is bananas and fish. They are Shafi'is in religion, and pious, upright, and virtuous. Their mosques are made of wood, skilfully constructed." [E]

This description, though brief, captures Mombasa's island character, its dependence on food imports, its Islamic piety, and its wooden architecture.

### Kilwa

Ibn Battuta's most enthusiastic description is of Kilwa, which he praises as:

> "Kilwa is one of the most beautiful and well-constructed towns in the world. The whole of it is elegantly built. The roofs are built with mangrove poles." [E]

**The Sultan:**

Ibn Battuta describes the sultan of Kilwa (probably Sultan al-Hasan ibn Sulaiman or his successor Abu al-Mawahib):

> "Its sultan at the time of my entry was Abu al-Muzaffar Hasan, who was noted for his gifts and generosity. He used to engage frequently in expeditions to the land of the Zanj [the African interior], raiding them and taking booty... He is a man of great humility; he sits with poor brethren, and eats with them, and greatly honors men of religion and of noble descent." [E]

This portrait emphasizes the sultan's piety, generosity, and military vigor — the qualities expected of a good Muslim ruler.

**The Gold Trade:**

Ibn Battuta notes Kilwa's economic foundation:

> "Kilwa is the principal town on the coast, the greater part of whose inhabitants are Zanj of very black complexion... The city of Sufala [Sofala] is a half-month's journey from Kilwa, and between Sufala and Yufi [the Zimbabwe Plateau?] in the country of the Limiyyin [?] is a month's journey. From Yufi gold dust is brought to Sufala." [E]

This passage documents the gold trade that made Kilwa wealthy — gold from the interior (the Zimbabwe Plateau) reaching the coast at Sofala and then flowing to Kilwa for export across the Indian Ocean.

## Key Passages

### Most Significant Descriptions

Certain passages from Ibn Battuta's African accounts are cited repeatedly by scholars and deserve special attention:

**Passage 1: The Mali Court Ceremony (Festival)**

> "On the festival day, after Dugha has played his instrument, the poets come. They are called jula [griots]. Each of them is inside a figure like a thrush, made of feathers, with a wooden head made like a bird's head. They stand in front of the sultan in this ridiculous manner and recite their poems. I was told that their poetry is a kind of sermonizing in which they say to the sultan: 'This bemba [throne] upon which you are sitting was sat upon by such and such a king who did good deeds; so do good deeds whose [memory] will survive you.' Similar advice, with mentions of many kings, is recited to him... Then these poets mount to the bemba and lay their heads on the sultan's lap, then mount to his right shoulder and then his left, speaking into his ear. Then they descend." [E]

This passage documents the role of griots in Mande political culture — professional praise-singers who not only flattered the ruler but also reminded him of his responsibilities and the examples of his predecessors. This is the earliest detailed description of griot performance.

**Passage 2: The Dust-Sprinkling Ritual**

(Quoted in full above)

This passage is the primary evidence for the Mande custom of self-abasement before rulers — a practice that continued for centuries and that shaped European perceptions of West African political culture.

**Passage 3: Kilwa's Beauty**

> "Kilwa is one of the most beautiful and well-constructed towns in the world. The whole of it is elegantly built." [E]

Brief but powerful, this statement from a traveler who had seen Cairo, Damascus, Delhi, and countless other cities carries enormous weight. It establishes that Kilwa, at its peak, was comparable to the great cities of the Islamic world.

**Passage 4: Mali's Justice System**

> "The Negroes possess some admirable qualities. They are seldom unjust, and have a greater abhorrence of injustice than any other people... There is complete security in their country." [E]

This praise, coming from a cultural critic, testifies to Mali's effective governance. It is among the most frequently cited passages in West African historiography.

**Passage 5: The Sahara Crossing**

> "We used to go ahead of the caravan, and when we found a place suitable for pasturage we would graze our beasts. We went on doing this until one of our party was lost in the desert; after that I neither went ahead nor lagged behind. We passed a caravan on the way and they told us that some of their party had become separated from them. We found one of them dead under a shrub, of the sort that grows in the sand, with his clothes on and a whip in his hand. The water was only about a mile away from him." [E]

This passage captures the dangers of the Sahara crossing — the ever-present risk of losing one's way, the death that awaited those who strayed from the caravan route. It provides essential context for understanding why trans-Saharan trade, while profitable, was also perilous.

### Passages Requiring Critical Reading

Certain passages in Ibn Battuta's account require particularly careful interpretation:

**The Criticism of Mali Customs:**

Ibn Battuta's complaints about Mali — the nudity of women, the inadequate hospitality, the persistence of non-Islamic practices — must be read as cultural commentary rather than objective description. He was measuring Mali against his own standards, not evaluating it on its own terms. Modern scholars use these complaints as evidence for Mali's cultural complexity rather than as evidence of deficiency.

**The Geographic Confusions:**

Ibn Battuta's identification of the Niger as a branch of the Nile reflects the geographical understanding of his time, not actual observation. Similarly, his vague descriptions of regions he did not personally visit should be treated with appropriate skepticism.

**The "Mansa Musa" Reference:**

Ibn Battuta never met Mansa Musa, who had died before Ibn Battuta's visit. His references to Musa are based on what people told him, not personal observation. The contrast between the legendary Musa and the less generous Sulayman may have been emphasized by Ibn Battuta's informants (or by Ibn Battuta himself) for narrative effect.

## Source Reliability

### Strengths

**Eyewitness Testimony:**

The fundamental value of the *Rihla* is that Ibn Battuta was actually there. He crossed the Sahara, attended Mansa Sulayman's court, walked the streets of Kilwa. His descriptions of what he saw and experienced provide information that cannot be obtained from secondhand accounts. [E]

**Trained Observer:**

Ibn Battuta was an educated man trained to observe and evaluate. His legal training gave him frameworks for analyzing social organization and governance. He noticed details — the materials of buildings, the instruments of musicians, the protocols of court ceremony — that a less observant traveler might have ignored. [E]

**Extended Stay in Mali:**

Unlike travelers who passed through quickly, Ibn Battuta spent approximately eight months at the Mali court. This extended residence allowed him to observe seasonal variations, attend multiple ceremonies, and develop some understanding of how Mali society functioned. [E]

**Cross-Cultural Perspective:**

Having traveled across the Islamic world, Ibn Battuta could place Africa in comparative perspective. His observations that certain practices were similar to or different from what he had seen elsewhere provide valuable contextual information. [P]

**Candid Criticism:**

Ibn Battuta's willingness to criticize what he found (the hospitality, the customs, the standards of Islamic practice) actually enhances his credibility as a source. A purely flattering account would be less trustworthy. His criticisms, while culturally biased, suggest he was recording his genuine reactions rather than merely praising his hosts. [P]

### Weaknesses

**Cultural Bias:**

Ibn Battuta evaluated everything through the lens of Moroccan Maliki Islam. Practices that deviated from his expectations were automatically "bad customs." His judgments reveal his assumptions as much as they reveal the societies he visited. [E]

**Memory and Time:**

The *Rihla* was dictated after Ibn Battuta returned to Morocco, based on recollections of travels that had occurred years (in some cases decades) earlier. Memory is fallible, and details may have shifted or blurred over time. [P]

**Literary Mediation:**

Ibn Juzayy's role in shaping the text means we cannot be certain which words are Ibn Battuta's and which are his editor's. The literary conventions of the *rihla* genre may have influenced how material was presented. [P]

**Exaggeration:**

Like many medieval travelers, Ibn Battuta may have exaggerated to impress his audience. Claims about distances, populations, and especially about his own status and reception should be treated with caution. [P]

**Gaps:**

Ibn Battuta's account is selective. He describes what interested him and omits what did not. Important aspects of the societies he visited — economic arrangements, political structure, daily life of ordinary people — receive less attention than court ceremonies and religious practices. [P]

**Brevity on the Swahili Coast:**

Ibn Battuta's Swahili Coast visits were brief, and his descriptions, while valuable, lack the depth of his Mali account. He spent months in Mali but only days or weeks on the Swahili Coast. [P]

## Scholarly Controversies

### The Itinerary Debates

Scholars have questioned aspects of Ibn Battuta's claimed itinerary:

**The Mali Capital Location:**

Ibn Battuta visited "the mansa's city" but does not provide clear geographic coordinates. The traditional identification with Niani (in modern Guinea) is based on later sources and archaeological work, but it has been questioned. Some scholars suggest the capital may have moved or that there were multiple royal residences. [D]

**What Did He Actually See?**

Given the borrowing evident in other sections of the *Rihla*, scholars have examined the African sections for similar problems. The consensus is that the African material is largely authentic — it contains too many personal details and local specifics to be fabricated from other sources. However, some geographic descriptions (particularly of regions Ibn Battuta did not personally visit) may derive from informants rather than observation. [P]

**Timbuktu's Significance:**

Ibn Battuta's relatively brief description of Timbuktu has puzzled scholars, given the city's later fame. This brevity may indicate that Timbuktu had not yet achieved the prominence it would attain under Songhai rule, or it may simply reflect Ibn Battuta's limited time there. [D]

### The "Borrowed" Sections Problem

The presence of clearly borrowed material in some sections of the *Rihla* (particularly the China account) raises questions about methodology:

**How to Identify Borrowing:**

Scholars identify borrowed material through:
- Comparison with earlier texts that contain identical or very similar passages
- Geographic and chronological impossibilities
- Absence of the personal details and complaints that characterize Ibn Battuta's authentic observations
- Presence of material that closely follows standard geographical compilations

**African Sections Generally Pass These Tests:**

The African sections of the *Rihla* generally pass scrutiny:
- No earlier text provides a model from which Ibn Battuta could have borrowed
- The itinerary is geographically and chronologically plausible
- Personal details abound (the complaints about hospitality, the interactions with specific individuals)
- The descriptions do not match standard geographical compilations [P]

### Interpretation of Ibn Battuta's Criticisms

Modern scholars debate how to interpret Ibn Battuta's negative comments about African societies:

**The Traditional Reading:**

Older scholarship sometimes took Ibn Battuta's criticisms at face value, treating his complaints as evidence of Mali's cultural deficiencies or incomplete Islamization.

**The Revisionist Reading:**

Contemporary scholarship recognizes that Ibn Battuta's criticisms reveal his own biases more than objective conditions. The practices he condemned (unveiled women, indigenous rituals, different hospitality norms) were normal within Mande culture. His shock reflects cultural difference, not cultural deficiency.

**The Decolonizing Reading:**

This project adopts a decolonizing approach that:
- Centers African agency rather than treating Africa as an object of outsider evaluation
- Recognizes that Ibn Battuta's standards were culturally specific, not universal
- Uses his criticisms as evidence for African cultural complexity rather than African failure
- Values his observations while questioning his judgments [E]

## For Fiction Writing

Ibn Battuta's *Rihla* is exceptionally valuable for fiction writers because of its vivid sensory details and personal observations. The following sections extract fiction-relevant material from his African accounts.

### Sensory Details from Mali

**Sight:**

- The mansa in his red velvet European robe (*mutanfas*), gold cap, bow in hand, quiver on his back
- The gold and silver platforms of the audience hall
- The great umbrella with its golden bird atop
- Women in beautiful robes with gold and silver headbands and pendant apples
- Youths in red woolen tunics and white skullcaps, drums slung from shoulders
- The dust-covered petitioners in worn garments, sprinkling sand on their heads
- The Niger River, brown and broad, boats with cargo
- The flat Sahel landscape, sparse trees, the horizon shimmering with heat
- Timbuktu and Walata: mud-brick buildings, narrow streets, the minarets of mosques

**Sound:**

- The dugha's instrument: "reeds with some small calabashes at its lower end"
- Drums beaten by thirty youths
- Women and slave girls singing praise songs
- The muezzin's call to prayer echoing across the city
- The griot poets reciting the deeds of past kings
- Market sounds: haggling, animals, the bustle of commerce
- The creak and groan of camel caravans

**Taste:**

- The famous modest gift: "a load of bread and meat fried in shea butter and a gourd with yogurt"
- Millet porridge (the staple grain)
- Fish from the Niger
- Dates from the Saharan oases
- Water from wells, precious in the dry season

**Smell:**

- Shea butter used in cooking
- Camels and their caravans
- Dust, everywhere dust
- Incense at religious ceremonies
- The Niger: mud, fish, vegetation
- Cooking fires

**Touch/Feel:**

- The oppressive heat of the Sahel
- Sand underfoot, in clothing, in food
- The relief of shade
- The texture of imported textiles: velvet, silk, fine cotton
- The weight of gold dust in the hand

### Social Customs from Mali

**Court Protocol:**

- Visitors processed through intermediaries; the mansa did not personally receive every guest
- Different categories of petitioners approached in a set order
- The dust-sprinkling gesture of submission
- Worn, dirty clothing donned as a mark of humility
- The dugha managing ceremony and precedence
- Praise-singing as a form of political communication

**Religious Practice:**

- Friday congregational prayer so crowded that latecomers found no space
- Children in shackles until they memorized the Quran (Ibn Battuta's observation)
- Festival ceremonies combining Islamic prayer with elaborate musical and poetic performances
- Scholars honored but also critical of rulers when they saw un-Islamic practices

**Gender and Society:**

- Women appearing without veils (to Ibn Battuta's shock)
- Female slaves and servants appearing nude (again, to his shock)
- The senior wife (Qasa) receiving public honors comparable to the mansa's
- The dugha's wives and slave girls participating in court ceremonies

**Daily Life:**

- Wells providing water; water carriers distributing it
- Markets for local and imported goods
- Hospitality obligations: hosts expected to provide food and lodging for guests
- Travel between towns by foot, donkey, camel, or boat depending on region

### Scene Opportunities from the *Rihla*

Ibn Battuta's account suggests several vivid scenes:

**The Royal Audience:**

The full ceremonial of the mansa emerging from his palace, the gold and silver standards, the musicians, the armed escort, the petitioners approaching with their dust-sprinkling gestures. A scene of elaborate protocol and performed power.

**The Dugha's Performance:**

The festival celebration with music, singing, acrobatics, and sword-play. The griot poets in their thrush costumes delivering advice through praise. A unique combination of entertainment and political communication.

**Ibn Battuta's Complaint:**

The frustrated scholar, accustomed to honor at other courts, finally working up the courage to complain about his treatment. The mansa's surprise ("I have not seen you nor heard of you"), the intervention of the qadi and preacher, the resolution. A scene of cross-cultural misunderstanding and negotiation.

**The Sahara Crossing:**

The caravan setting out, the endless dunes, the terror of the lost traveler, the discovery of the dead man "under a shrub... with his clothes on and a whip in his hand. The water was only about a mile away." The thin line between survival and death in the desert.

**Kilwa's Harbor:**

Ibn Battuta arriving by dhow, seeing the city rise from the turquoise water, the white stone buildings, the elegant construction. The sultan receiving him with hospitality and honor.

### Character Notes

**Ibn Battuta as Character:**

If Ibn Battuta himself appears in fiction, his character is well-documented:

- **Proud and Status-Conscious:** Expected treatment befitting a distinguished scholar; easily offended when he felt slighted
- **Pious but Practical:** Devout Muslim who nevertheless adapted to circumstances and accepted the hospitality of dubious patrons
- **Curious and Observant:** Genuinely interested in what he saw, even when he disapproved
- **Sociable:** Made friends and connections wherever he went; his network of contacts spanned the Islamic world
- **Resilient:** Survived decades of travel, including multiple dangerous situations
- **Judgmental:** Quick to criticize what failed to meet his standards

**Using Ibn Battuta's Observations for Other Characters:**

Ibn Battuta's descriptions can inform characters he observed:

- **Mansa Sulayman:** Powerful but perhaps more austere than his famous brother Musa; formal in court settings; willing to acknowledge errors and correct them
- **The Dugha:** Master performer and ceremonial manager; central to court life; perhaps more influential than his nominal role suggests
- **The Scholars:** Serious about Islamic learning; willing to criticize the ruler; maintaining standards despite political pressure
- **Ordinary Mali People:** Devout, just, secure in their persons and property; following customs that seemed natural to them regardless of what foreign visitors thought

### Worldview and Values

Ibn Battuta's account reveals the worldview of a 14th-century Moroccan Muslim jurist:

**Islam as Universal Standard:**

Ibn Battuta evaluated every society by its adherence to Islamic practice as he understood it. Good Muslims prayed, observed Ramadan, educated their children in the Quran, and followed shari'a. Societies that did these things earned his praise; those that did not earned his criticism.

**Honor and Hospitality:**

Ibn Battuta expected hosts to honor visitors according to their status. His complaints about Mali's hospitality reveal assumptions about what scholars deserved and how rulers should demonstrate respect.

**The Islamic World as Home:**

Ibn Battuta traveled across three continents but always within a shared Islamic cultural framework. He could find employment, companionship, and familiar religious practice wherever Muslims lived. This sense of a shared *ummah* (community) enabled his extraordinary journeys.

**Curiosity and Tolerance (Within Limits):**

Despite his judgments, Ibn Battuta was genuinely curious about the peoples he visited and could appreciate customs very different from his own — as long as they did not violate what he considered fundamental Islamic principles.

## Sources and Translations

### Arabic Text

The standard Arabic edition is:

**Al-Tazi, Abd al-Hadi (ed.)**
- *Rihlat Ibn Battuta*, 5 volumes (Rabat: Academy of the Kingdom of Morocco, 1997)
- The most complete modern Arabic edition with extensive notes

### English Translations

**H.A.R. Gibb (trans.)**
- *The Travels of Ibn Battuta, A.D. 1325-1354*, 4 volumes (London: Hakluyt Society, 1958-1994)
- Volume 4 completed by C.F. Beckingham
- The standard scholarly English translation
- Extensive notes and commentary
- Volume IV contains the African sections
- **Reliability: HIGH** — definitive scholarly translation

**Hamdun, Said & Noel King (trans.)**
- *Ibn Battuta in Black Africa* (London: Rex Collings, 1975; revised edition Princeton: Markus Wiener, 1994)
- Focuses specifically on the African sections of the *Rihla*
- Includes the Swahili Coast and Mali material with introduction and notes
- More accessible than Gibb for those interested specifically in Africa
- **Reliability: HIGH** — specialized translation for African content

**Mackintosh-Smith, Tim (trans.)**
- *The Travels of Ibn Battutah* (London: Picador, 2002)
- Abridged translation for general readers
- Engaging introduction; less complete than Gibb
- **Reliability: MEDIUM** — abridged but accessible

### Secondary Literature on Ibn Battuta

**Dunn, Ross E.**
- *The Adventures of Ibn Battuta: A Muslim Traveler of the Fourteenth Century* (Berkeley: University of California Press, 1986; revised 2005)
- The best modern biography and analysis
- Places Ibn Battuta in historical context; addresses authenticity questions
- **Reliability: HIGH** — the standard secondary work

**Waines, David**
- *The Odyssey of Ibn Battuta: Uncommon Tales of a Medieval Adventurer* (London: I.B. Tauris, 2010)
- Accessible narrative biography
- **Reliability: HIGH** — scholarly but readable

**Elad, Amikam (ed.)**
- *The Voyage of Ibn Battuta: Studies in Memory of H.A.R. Gibb* (Special issue of *Jerusalem Studies in Arabic and Islam*, 2000)
- Scholarly articles on various aspects of the *Rihla*
- **Reliability: HIGH** — specialized scholarship

### African Context

**Levtzion, Nehemia & J.F.P. Hopkins (eds.)**
- *Corpus of Early Arabic Sources for West African History* (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1981; reprinted Princeton: Markus Wiener, 2000)
- Includes the West African sections of Ibn Battuta's *Rihla* alongside other Arabic sources
- Essential for placing Ibn Battuta in the context of other Arabic writing about Africa
- **Reliability: ESSENTIAL** — the standard source compilation

**Gomez, Michael A.**
- *African Dominion: A New History of Empire in Early and Medieval West Africa* (Princeton: Princeton University Press, 2018)
- Uses Ibn Battuta extensively; contextualizes his observations
- **Reliability: HIGH** — current scholarship
