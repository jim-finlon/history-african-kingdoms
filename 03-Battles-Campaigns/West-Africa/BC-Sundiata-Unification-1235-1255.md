## Overview

The Unification Campaigns represent the military and political process by which Sundiata Keita, having destroyed Sumanguru Kante's Sosso kingdom at the Battle of Kirina (c. 1235), extended his authority across the western Sahel to create the Mali Empire. While Kirina was the dramatic climax — the moment when the sorcerer-king fell and Mande liberation was achieved — the work of empire-building had only begun. Over the following two decades, Sundiata and his generals conducted a series of campaigns that brought diverse peoples under Mali's hegemony, established control over the vital gold-producing regions and trans-Saharan trade routes, absorbed the remnants of the old Ghana Empire, and created the political and social structures that would govern the Mande world for generations.

The oral tradition preserves these campaigns in heroic and episodic form, centering on the exploits of Sundiata's celebrated generals: [[West-Africa/Mali-Empire/HF-Tiramaghan-Traore|Tiramaghan Traore]], who led the conquest of the western regions reaching to the Gambia River and perhaps the Atlantic coast; [[West-Africa/Mali-Empire/HF-Fakoli-Koroma|Fakoli Koroma]], the defector from Sosso whose blacksmith-sorcery and warrior prowess served the new cause as effectively as it had once served Sumanguru; and other commanders whose names echo through the griot performances. The tradition also preserves the memory of the Kouroukan Fouga — the great constitutional assembly at Kurukan Fuga where Sundiata, having won the empire, organized its governance through a charter that defined clan relationships, social categories, and political principles.

For historians, the Unification Campaigns present challenges typical of this project's subject matter. No contemporary written sources document them. The Arabic chronicles that provide external attestation of Mali's existence (Ibn Khaldun, al-Umari, Ibn Battuta) describe the empire in its maturity, not its creation. What we know of the campaigns comes almost entirely from the Sundiata epic and related oral traditions — sources that preserve genuine historical memory but also serve narrative and political purposes, blending factual kernel with legendary elaboration. The challenge is not to dismiss these traditions but to read them with appropriate source criticism, extracting the historical patterns they encode while acknowledging what cannot be independently verified.

What emerges from careful analysis is the picture of an ambitious state-building project: military conquest to establish authority, political negotiation to integrate allies and subordinate rivals, economic reorganization to control the gold-salt trade that generated Mali's wealth, and ideological legitimation through the Kouroukan Fouga to create a social order that could outlast any individual ruler. The Mali that Sundiata's successors inherited — the Mali of Mansa Musa's legendary wealth — was built in these campaigns. The Mande world that persists today, with its clan structures, joking relationships, and social categories, traces its organization to the assembly that concluded them.

## Background and Causes

### Political Context: The Western Sahel After Kirina

The Battle of Kirina (c. 1235) destroyed Sumanguru Kante's Sosso kingdom and eliminated the most powerful rival to Mande unification. But Kirina was a beginning, not an ending. Sumanguru's defeat created a power vacuum across the western Sahel that required filling. Various groups competed for position:

**Sosso remnants:** Sumanguru's kingdom did not vanish completely with his defeat (or disappearance — the tradition is ambiguous about his fate). Sosso loyalists, members of the Kante clan, and peoples who had been integrated into the Sosso system needed to be dealt with. Some would submit to Mali; others would resist; still others would need to be pursued and destroyed. [T]

**Mande clans not yet committed:** Not all Mande-speaking peoples had joined Sundiata's coalition before Kirina. Some had remained neutral, waiting to see which side would prevail. Others had been too distant or too weak to participate. These groups now faced pressure to accept Mali's authority. [T]

**Non-Mande peoples:** The western Sahel included populations who were not ethnically or linguistically Mande — Fulani pastoralists, Wolof and Serer communities in Senegambia, Soninke remnants of the old Ghana tradition, Tuareg nomads in the northern reaches. Mali's expansion would involve negotiating, coercing, or conquering these diverse peoples. [P]

**The Ghana remnant:** The old Ghana Empire (Wagadu), which had dominated the western Sahel for centuries before its decline in the 11th-12th centuries, persisted as a reduced polity. Its Soninke population and its merchants (the Wangara, who controlled the gold trade networks) remained significant. Mali would need to absorb this remnant — completing the transfer of Sahelian hegemony that had begun under Sosso. [P]

**Trade route control:** The trans-Saharan gold-salt trade was the economic foundation of Sahelian power. Whoever controlled the exchange points where gold from the southern forests met salt from the Saharan mines could tax the most lucrative commerce in the region. This trade had enriched Ghana, then Sosso; now Mali sought to capture it. [P]

### Immediate Cause: The Victory at Kirina

The immediate cause of the Unification Campaigns was the opportunity created by Kirina. Sundiata had assembled a coalition of allied clans, united by opposition to Sosso oppression and bound by oaths of mutual support. That coalition had defeated Sumanguru. But coalitions are fragile; the bonds of shared struggle can dissolve once the common enemy is gone. Sundiata faced a choice: allow his alliance to fragment and see the western Sahel return to political fragmentation, or use the momentum of victory to build something lasting.

The tradition is clear that he chose the latter. The campaigns that followed Kirina were not improvised reactions to threats but a deliberate program of expansion and consolidation. Whether this program was fully conceived before Kirina or emerged gradually cannot be determined, but the scope and systematization of the expansion suggest strategic planning rather than mere opportunism. [T]

### Strategic Objectives

The Unification Campaigns pursued several interlocking objectives:

**1. Elimination of Sosso remnants:** Any surviving centers of Sosso power needed to be neutralized to prevent a revival. This included military operations against Kante loyalists and political integration of Sosso populations who accepted the new order. [T]

**2. Extension to the goldfields:** The gold-producing regions of Bambuk (upper Senegal River) and Bure (upper Niger) were the economic prizes. Controlling these regions — or at least controlling the trade that flowed from them — would give Mali the wealth to sustain an empire. [P]

**3. Control of trade routes:** The trans-Saharan routes required security from raiders and predictable taxation rather than extortion. Mali needed to establish authority along the entire north-south trade corridor, from the Saharan edge to the goldfields. [P]

**4. Expansion to the west:** The Atlantic-facing regions of Senegambia offered agricultural resources, additional populations, and control of the westernmost extent of the Sahelian trade system. [[West-Africa/Mali-Empire/HF-Tiramaghan-Traore|Tiramaghan Traore's]] western campaigns served this objective. [T]

**5. Absorption of Ghana:** The old Wagadu polity, though weakened, still existed. Its incorporation would complete Mali's succession to Sahelian hegemony and bring the valuable Soninke merchant networks (the Wangara) under Mali's umbrella. [P]

**6. Political legitimation:** Military conquest alone would not create a stable empire. The diverse populations brought under Mali's authority needed a framework for coexistence — a political order that defined relationships, settled disputes, and provided predictability. The Kouroukan Fouga was designed to serve this purpose. [T]

## The Belligerents

### Mali Coalition Forces

**Supreme Commander:** [[West-Africa/Mali-Empire/HF-Sundiata-Keita|Sundiata Keita]], Mansa of Mali

**Principal Generals:**

**[[West-Africa/Mali-Empire/HF-Tiramaghan-Traore|Tiramaghan Traore]]** — The most trusted military commander for independent operations, Tiramaghan led the western campaigns that extended Mali's reach to the Gambia region and perhaps to the Atlantic. A member of the Traore warrior clan and a master of the hunter-warrior (*donso*) tradition, he combined physical prowess with the spiritual mastery expected of a great Mande warrior. His campaigns were the largest independent military operation in the Unification era. [T]

**[[West-Africa/Mali-Empire/HF-Fakoli-Koroma|Fakoli Koroma]]** — The defector from Sosso, Fakoli brought to Sundiata's coalition the blacksmith-sorcerer power of his lineage, military forces loyal to him personally, and intimate knowledge of Sosso's strengths and weaknesses. His role in the post-Kirina campaigns is less clearly defined in the tradition than his dramatic defection, but his continued importance is indicated by his prominence in the Kouroukan Fouga arrangements. [T]

**Kamandjan** — A Mande prince mentioned in various traditions as one of Sundiata's commanders. His specific campaigns are less clearly detailed than Tiramaghan's western operations. [T]

**Faonyinke Konde** — Another general mentioned in some versions of the tradition. [T]

**Forces:**

The Mali coalition army was drawn from multiple sources:

- **Keita clan warriors:** Sundiata's own lineage provided a core force loyal to him personally.
- **Allied clan contingents:** Each clan chief who joined the coalition brought warriors from his own people. These were not permanent professional soldiers but freemen mobilized for campaign.
- **Hunter-warriors (*donsow*):** The hunter societies provided elite fighters with spiritual power and bush-warfare expertise.
- **Cavalry:** The western Sahel was cavalry country; Mali's forces included horse-mounted warriors armed with lances, swords, and bows. Horses were a prestige good imported via the trans-Saharan trade; cavalry forces were thus tied to wealth and status.
- **Infantry:** The bulk of the army consisted of foot soldiers armed with iron-tipped spears, bows (sometimes with poisoned arrows), and leather or quilted cotton shields.

**Numbers:** The tradition provides no reliable figures for Mali's armies. Estimates based on later Arabic accounts and the logistical capacity of Sahelian states suggest that major campaign armies might have numbered in the thousands to low tens of thousands, but any specific figure is [Speculative]. [S]

**Strengths:**
- Coalition brought together diverse military capabilities
- High morale from Kirina victory
- Spiritual legitimation from defeating Sumanguru's sorcery
- Superior cavalry (in open savanna warfare)
- Unified command under Sundiata's charismatic leadership

**Weaknesses:**
- Coalition armies were inherently fragile; allied chiefs retained autonomy
- Extended campaigns strained logistics and clan patience
- Integration of conquered territories required ongoing negotiation
- Distance and terrain limited how rapidly the empire could expand

### Opposing Forces

The Unification Campaigns were not a single war against a single enemy but a series of operations against diverse opponents:

**Sosso remnants:** Following Kirina, loyalist elements of Sumanguru's Sosso kingdom continued to resist. The Kante clan and allied groups needed to be suppressed or integrated. These forces were fragmented after their king's defeat, lacking unified command but capable of localized resistance. [T]

**Local Mande polities:** Not all Mande clans had joined Sundiata before Kirina. Some resisted incorporation into the new empire, whether from rivalry, autonomy concerns, or existing alignments. These were typically small-scale opponents, individually weak but cumulatively requiring significant military attention. [T]

**Non-Mande peoples:** The western expansion brought Mali forces into contact with Wolof, Serer, Fulani, and other groups who had their own political structures and military capabilities. The Gambia region in particular was populated by peoples who would be conquered by [[West-Africa/Mali-Empire/HF-Tiramaghan-Traore|Tiramaghan's]] forces. These opponents varied widely in their capacity to resist. [T]

**Ghana/Wagadu remnant:** The residual Soninke state that had once dominated the region possessed sophisticated political traditions and valuable merchant networks. Its military capacity was diminished from its imperial peak, but it was not negligible. Its absorption (c. 1240) required military pressure and/or political negotiation. [P]

**Strengths (collectively):**
- Local knowledge of terrain
- Defensive advantages in forest and river environments (western campaigns)
- Some possessed fortified settlements
- Guerrilla resistance could continue after formal conquest

**Weaknesses (collectively):**
- Fragmentation — no unified resistance to Mali's expansion
- Inferior military organization compared to Mali's coalition
- The momentum of Mali's success encouraged submission
- Economic incentives to join the winning side (trade access)

## The Campaigns

### Overview of Operations

The Unification Campaigns were not a single continuous operation but a series of campaigns over approximately two decades. The tradition preserves them in episodic and sometimes contradictory form, making precise reconstruction impossible. What follows is a synthesis of the general patterns, acknowledging that specific sequences and dates cannot be established with confidence.

### Phase 1: Post-Kirina Consolidation (c. 1235-1237)

**Elimination of Sosso remnants:**

Immediately following Kirina, Sundiata's forces pursued the collapse of Sumanguru's power structure. The tradition describes Sumanguru fleeing the battlefield and either dying, vanishing into the mountains, or being transformed (the accounts vary). But his disappearance did not end Sosso as a political reality. The Kante clan had accumulated power over decades; its networks of subordinates and dependents did not evaporate overnight.

The initial post-Kirina phase involved:

- **Pursuit operations:** Hunting down Sumanguru himself (if he survived) and his immediate supporters. The tradition places his final disappearance at the Koulikoro mountains, suggesting pursuit into the escarpment country east of the battlefield. [T]

- **Submission acceptance:** Many of Sumanguru's former subordinates would have sought to make terms with the victors. Sundiata's coalition needed to decide whom to accept, on what terms, and whom to pursue further. [T]

- **Reorganization of the coalition:** The allies who had fought together at Kirina needed to clarify their relationships in the post-war order. Who would hold what territories? How would tribute flow? What were the obligations of alliance? [T]

**Establishment at Niani:**

The tradition places Sundiata's capital at Niani, on the upper Niger River in modern Guinea. The precise location remains debated, but the establishment of a fixed capital represented the transition from wartime coalition to territorial state. From Niani (or wherever the capital was actually located), Sundiata could coordinate further expansion while beginning the administrative organization of conquered territories. [P]

### Phase 2: The Western Campaigns (c. 1237-1250)

**[[West-Africa/Mali-Empire/HF-Tiramaghan-Traore|Tiramaghan Traore's]] Conquest of the Gambia Region:**

The most extensively remembered military operation of the Unification period was Tiramaghan's conquest of the western territories, extending Mali's reach from the Manden heartland to the Gambia River valley and perhaps to the Atlantic coast. This campaign (or series of campaigns) brought significant new territories under Mali's authority and established Traore lineages that persist in the Gambia region today.

**The march west:**

Tiramaghan led a substantial force westward from the Manden heartland. The distance involved — hundreds of kilometers through varied terrain — implies a campaign of months or years rather than a single swift operation. The tradition emphasizes the scope of his conquests without providing tactical detail: [T]

- Movement through the savanna zone toward the Atlantic
- Encounters with local polities, some of which resisted while others submitted
- Focus on the Gambia River valley as the principal objective
- Possible extension to the Atlantic coast itself

**Conquest and settlement:**

Tiramaghan's western campaigns were followed by settlement — the establishment of Mande political structures in the conquered regions:

- **Traore lineages:** Families in the Gambia region who bear the Traore name trace their ancestry to this period. Whether they descend directly from Tiramaghan's warriors or adopted the name through later affiliation, these genealogies connect contemporary communities to the founding era. [T]

- **Political structures:** The imposition of Mali-style governance — appointed chiefs, tribute relationships, integration into the social framework later codified at Kouroukan Fuga — created the administrative infrastructure of imperial control. [T]

- **Cultural transmission:** Mande language, customs, and identity spread into the western regions through the conquest and settlement process. The Mandinka population of modern Gambia represents this cultural expansion. [T]

**Local perspectives:**

The Gambia region also preserves traditions that remember Tiramaghan as a conqueror rather than a liberator. These local traditions include:

- Memories of resistance to Mande conquest
- Recognition of subjugation and tribute extraction
- Acknowledgment that the conquest involved violence and disruption

This dual perspective — Tiramaghan as hero in the Mande heartland, Tiramaghan as conqueror in the western regions — illustrates how the same historical process is remembered differently by those who benefited and those who were subjected. Both perspectives may preserve genuine historical memory. [T]

### Phase 3: Control of the Goldfields (c. 1235-1250)

**Bambuk and Bure:**

The gold-producing regions of Bambuk (on the upper Senegal River) and Bure (on the upper Niger) were the economic foundation of Mali's wealth. The tradition does not describe dramatic battles for control of these regions; the process was probably more complex than simple military conquest:

**The nature of gold production:**

Gold in the Bambuk and Bure regions was not mined by large-scale operations that could be seized and operated by conquerors. It was produced by small-scale alluvial mining and panning by local populations who possessed the knowledge and labor to extract it. Controlling the gold trade meant controlling the exchange points where gold was traded for Saharan salt and North African goods, not operating the mines directly. [P]

**The Wangara network:**

The gold trade was managed by Wangara (Soninke/Mande) merchants who operated as intermediaries between the gold-producing areas and the trans-Saharan trading system. These merchant communities — Muslim, mobile, and commercially sophisticated — were the sinews of the trade. Mali's control of the gold trade involved establishing authority over the Wangara networks and the trading towns where exchange occurred. [P]

**Military operations:**

Military action in the goldfield regions probably involved:

- Establishing Mali's authority over trading towns and routes
- Suppressing any local rulers who attempted to control the trade independently
- Protecting the trade from raiders and ensuring secure passage
- Creating the conditions of peace and predictability that allowed commerce to flourish

The tradition does not name specific battles or campaigns for the goldfields, suggesting either that the process was relatively peaceful (submission rather than conquest) or that the military operations were not dramatic enough to enter the heroic narrative. [T]

### Phase 4: Absorption of Ghana (c. 1240)

**The Ghana remnant:**

The old Ghana Empire (Wagadu), which had dominated the western Sahel from roughly the 4th through 11th centuries, persisted as a reduced polity in the upper Senegal region. Its Soninke population retained cultural identity, political organization, and — crucially — the Wangara merchant networks that controlled the gold trade.

**The incorporation:**

Sometime around 1240 (the date is traditional and approximate), Mali absorbed the remnants of the Ghana polity. The process may have involved:

- **Military pressure:** A show of force or actual campaign to compel submission
- **Negotiated integration:** Recognition that the Soninke population and Wangara merchants were more valuable as cooperative subjects than as conquered enemies
- **Political accommodation:** Allowing Soninke elites to retain some status within the new Mali order

The incorporation of Ghana completed the transfer of Sahelian hegemony. Mali was now the successor state to the empire that had defined the region for centuries. The Soninke, once rulers, became subjects — though the Wangara merchants retained their commercial importance and would continue to play a crucial role in West African trade for centuries. [P]

### Phase 5: Northern and Eastern Expansion (c. 1240-1255)

**Control of trade routes:**

Mali's northern expansion aimed to secure the trans-Saharan trade routes that connected the Sahel to North Africa. This involved:

- **Establishing authority at Walata (Oualata):** This trading city at the southern edge of the Sahara served as a major entrepot where caravans from the north met merchants from the south. Control of Walata gave Mali leverage over the trans-Saharan trade.

- **Relations with Saharan nomads:** The Tuareg and other Saharan peoples controlled the desert routes that caravans had to traverse. Mali's relationship with these groups was complex — sometimes conflict, sometimes alliance, always negotiation of access and taxation.

- **Extension toward Timbuktu and Gao:** The Niger Bend region, which would become so important under Mali's later rulers (especially Mansa Musa), began to come under Mali's influence during the Unification period, though its full integration may have occurred later.

**The tradition's silence:**

The oral tradition focuses heavily on the western campaigns (Tiramaghan) and the dramatic founding narrative (Kirina, Kouroukan Fuga). The northern and eastern expansion receives less attention in the epic tradition, suggesting either that these operations were less dramatic or that they were conducted more by trade and diplomacy than by warfare. [T]

### Phase 6: The Kouroukan Fouga (c. 1236 or later)

**The Constitutional Assembly:**

The Kouroukan Fouga (also spelled Kurukan Fuga, Manden Charter) represents the tradition's memory of a founding constitutional moment when Sundiata, having won the empire by conquest, organized it by law. According to the tradition, Sundiata convened a great assembly at a clearing called Kurukan Fuga (identified with a site in modern Mali near the Guinea border) where representatives of all the clans gathered to establish the principles that would govern Mande society.

**Content of the Charter:**

The Kouroukan Fouga, as preserved in oral tradition and compiled in written form in the 20th century, addresses:

**1. Social organization:**

The division of Mande society into social categories:

- **Horon** (freeborn, including warrior-nobles and farmers)
- **Nyamakala** (occupational castes: griots/djeli, blacksmiths/numu, leatherworkers/garanke, and others)
- **Jon** (enslaved and formerly enslaved peoples)

Each category had defined rights, obligations, and relationships to others. The nyamakala, in particular, occupied a distinctive position — essential specialists whose work with powerful forces (words, fire/metal, animal skins) gave them spiritual potency that was both valued and feared. [T]

**2. Clan relationships:**

The Kouroukan Fouga established (or codified) the system of *sanankuya* (joking relationships) between specific clans. These relationships allowed certain clans to mock each other freely — violations of normal politeness — while also obligating mutual hospitality and aid. The sanankuya created cross-cutting ties that bound Mande society together, defusing potential conflicts through institutionalized humor and reciprocal obligation.

Examples:
- Traore and Kone clans have a joking relationship
- Keita and Konate clans have specific defined relationships
- The patterns varied by region but the principle was consistent

**3. Governance principles:**

The charter established (or articulated) principles of legitimate rule:

- The role of the mansa (emperor) and subordinate chiefs
- Mechanisms for resolving disputes between clans
- The responsibilities of rulers toward their subjects
- The right of subjects to counsel rulers and, implicitly, the limits of tyrannical rule

**4. Rights and protections:**

Some versions of the Kouroukan Fouga include provisions that sound remarkably modern:

- Protections for women in marriage and inheritance
- Regulations on the treatment of slaves
- Environmental provisions about hunting and land use
- Statements about the sanctity of human life

Scholars debate how much of this content reflects authentic 13th-century provisions versus later elaboration. The 20th-century compilations of the charter may include material added over centuries of transmission. [D]

**Historical assessment:**

The existence of a foundational assembly where Sundiata organized Mande society is [Traditional/Oral]. Some form of political settlement following Kirina is historically plausible — new empires typically establish their organizational principles early. However, the specific content of the Kouroukan Fouga as recorded in modern times cannot be confidently attributed to a single 13th-century moment.

What is clear is that Mande political culture credited Sundiata with establishing the social order that governed their society for centuries. Whether this attribution reflects historical memory of an actual founding moment or retroactive legitimation of accumulated custom cannot be determined from available evidence. [D]

In 2009, UNESCO inscribed the Manden Charter on the Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity, recognizing its importance to Mande peoples regardless of its precise historical status.

## Aftermath

### Immediate Consequences

**Territorial extent:**

By Sundiata's death (c. 1255), the Mali Empire encompassed:

- The Manden heartland (upper Niger and Senegal river regions)
- The goldfields of Bambuk and Bure
- The former Ghana territories
- The western regions to the Gambia River (and possibly the Atlantic coast)
- Significant influence over the trans-Saharan trade routes

This was the largest state West Africa had yet seen, exceeding even the old Ghana Empire at its peak. [P]

**Political structure:**

The empire that emerged from the Unification Campaigns was organized around:

- **The Keita dynasty:** Sundiata's lineage held the throne, with succession (in principle, though often contested in practice) passing within the Keita clan.
- **Provincial governors:** Subordinate rulers (*farba* and other titles) administered territories on behalf of the mansa.
- **Allied clan chiefs:** The coalition structure persisted; allied clans retained internal autonomy while acknowledging Mali's supremacy.
- **Tributary relationships:** Conquered peoples paid tribute and provided military support but often retained local governance.
- **The Wangara network:** The Soninke merchant communities operated the trade that generated the empire's wealth, benefiting from Mali's protection while contributing taxes.

**Social organization:**

The social categories attributed to the Kouroukan Fouga — horon, nyamakala, jon — became the framework of Mande society. Whatever their precise historical origin, these categories were operational by the time external observers (Arab travelers, later European visitors) began documenting Mali. [P]

### Long-Term Impact

**The Mali Empire:**

The state Sundiata founded endured for over two centuries in significant form and persisted in diminished form into the 17th century. His successors — most notably Mansa Musa (r. 1312-1337) — built on the territorial and institutional foundations he established to create an empire of legendary wealth and cosmopolitan culture. The Mali that Mansa Musa took on hajj in 1324, astonishing the Mediterranean world with his gold, was the direct heir of Sundiata's Unification Campaigns. [E]

**Mande political culture:**

The institutions attributed to Sundiata — the clan structure, the sanankuya relationships, the social categories — shaped Mande society long after Mali's political decline. Even today, Mande peoples in Mali, Guinea, Gambia, Senegal, and beyond organize social life according to principles that tradition traces to the Kouroukan Fouga. The Keita name still carries prestige; the joking relationships still operate; the griot lineages still preserve and transmit history. [E]

**The epic tradition:**

The Sundiata epic itself emerged from this period — the story that griots would tell for eight centuries about the founding of their world. The epic preserves not merely information about the Unification Campaigns but the values, worldview, and self-understanding of Mande civilization. It is simultaneously a historical source and a cultural achievement, one of the great works of world oral literature. [E]

**Trans-Saharan trade:**

Mali's control of the gold trade reshaped the economy of the medieval world. West African gold, flowing through Mali to the Saharan routes and thence to North Africa, the Mediterranean, and beyond, became the foundation of international commerce. The very coins used in medieval Europe often contained West African gold that had passed through the territory Sundiata conquered. [E]

### Treatment of the Defeated

The tradition says relatively little about what happened to those who resisted Mali's expansion. What can be inferred:

**Sosso remnants:** The Kante clan and Sumanguru's former supporters were either killed, enslaved, integrated into the new order, or driven into exile. The tradition's ambiguity about Sumanguru's fate (did he die? disappear? transform?) suggests that complete closure was never achieved — perhaps survivors remained who could not be acknowledged as destroyed. [T]

**Resistant populations:** Conquest involved violence. Those who fought and lost faced the usual consequences: death in battle, enslavement, loss of property, subjugation. The tradition does not dwell on these outcomes, focusing instead on the heroism of the conquerors. [T]

**Submitting populations:** Those who accepted Mali's authority without prolonged resistance fared better. The imperial structure accommodated local elites who acknowledged Keita supremacy, paid tribute, and contributed military forces. The empire was not an attempt at ethnic cleansing or cultural erasure but a tributary system that allowed diversity within a hierarchical framework. [T]

**Ghana's Soninke population:** The incorporation of the Ghana remnant seems to have been relatively non-violent, with Soninke elites finding places within the new order and the Wangara merchants continuing their commercial activities. The Soninke language and culture persisted under Mali's rule. [P]

## Military Analysis

### Tactics and Strategy

**Coalition warfare:**

The Unification Campaigns were conducted not by a centralized standing army but by a coalition of allied forces. This had tactical implications:

- **Coordination challenges:** Different contingents had different capabilities, loyalty levels, and interests. Sundiata's success as a commander lay partly in his ability to hold the coalition together and deploy its diverse elements effectively.
- **Delegated command:** Major operations (like Tiramaghan's western campaigns) were conducted by subordinate commanders operating with considerable independence. This was both necessity (communication over distance was slow) and strategy (trusting capable generals with significant responsibility).
- **Political warfare:** The expansion involved as much negotiation as fighting. Potential enemies who saw the coalition's strength often chose submission over resistance. The demonstration of military power enabled political expansion. [T]

**Cavalry-based tactics:**

The western Sahel was cavalry country. Mali's military advantage rested substantially on mounted warriors:

- Cavalry could move rapidly across the savanna, outmaneuvering foot-bound opponents
- Mounted archers and lancers could conduct hit-and-run attacks, harassing enemies before closing for decisive engagement
- Cavalry charges against infantry could be devastating if the infantry lacked discipline and defensive formations
- The prestige of mounted warfare meant that cavalry service attracted elite warriors, creating a qualitative as well as numerical advantage

**Hunter-warrior (*donso*) traditions:**

The hunter societies contributed specialized capabilities:

- Bush warfare skills — tracking, ambush, navigation in difficult terrain
- Spiritual power — the *nyama* accumulated through hunting gave donso warriors an edge in the metaphysical dimension of combat
- Elite status — donso warriors were respected and feared, their presence boosting morale
- Independent initiative — hunters were accustomed to operating alone or in small groups, useful for reconnaissance, raids, and pursuit

**Siege warfare:**

The tradition does not describe elaborate siege operations during the Unification Campaigns. This may reflect either the limited development of fortification in the region or the preference for rapid conquest over protracted sieges. Later conflicts (e.g., Sunni Ali's seven-year siege of Djenne) suggest that siege warfare could occur, but the Unification period may have been characterized more by field operations and negotiated surrenders. [T]

### Technology

**Weapons:**

- **Spears:** The primary infantry weapon, with iron tips produced by the nyamakala blacksmith caste
- **Bows:** Both short bows for cavalry and longer bows for infantry; arrows sometimes poisoned
- **Swords and daggers:** Iron bladed weapons for close combat
- **Clubs and axes:** Secondary weapons for close quarters

**Armor and protection:**

- **Leather shields:** The standard defensive equipment, made by the garanke (leatherworker) caste
- **Quilted cotton armor:** Thick padded garments that provided some protection against arrows and slashing attacks
- **Helmets:** Leather or quilted cotton protection for the head

**Horses:**

- Cavalry horses were a prestige good, imported via the trans-Saharan trade
- Maintaining cavalry required access to horses, fodder, and the wealth to support expensive mounts
- The horse was a status symbol as well as a military instrument

**Spiritual technology:**

Mande warfare operated on spiritual as well as physical levels:

- **Amulets and charms:** Warriors carried protective objects prepared by specialists (including both Islamic and indigenous practitioners)
- **Ritual preparation:** Pre-battle rituals invoked spiritual protection and power
- **Sorcerous attack:** Great warriors (like Sumanguru, like Sundiata himself) were believed capable of deploying *nyama* as a weapon
- **The donso tradition:** Hunter-warrior initiation provided access to occult knowledge and power

### Terrain and Environment

**The savanna:**

Most of the Unification Campaigns occurred in the Sahelian savanna — a landscape of grassland, scattered trees (baobabs, acacias, shea), seasonal rivers, and relatively flat terrain. This environment favored:

- Cavalry operations (open ground for mounted maneuver)
- Rapid movement (few major obstacles)
- Visibility (making ambush difficult but reconnaissance easier)

**Seasonal factors:**

West African climate imposed constraints:

- **Wet season (June-September):** Rivers swell, making crossing difficult; some areas become impassable; malaria and other diseases intensify
- **Dry season (October-May):** Better conditions for campaigning; rivers fordable; drier and healthier (relatively speaking)
- **Harmattan (November-February):** Dry Saharan winds bring dust and reduced visibility

Military operations were probably concentrated in the dry season when conditions favored movement and combat.

**The western expansion:**

Tiramaghan's campaigns to the Gambia region involved different terrain:

- River valleys (the Gambia and its tributaries) provided geographic focus
- Forest and denser vegetation in some areas limited cavalry effectiveness
- Greater distance from the Manden heartland complicated logistics

**The goldfields:**

The gold-producing regions were typically in forested or semi-forested river valleys where alluvial gold could be extracted. This terrain was not favorable to large-scale military operations; control was exercised through the trading towns rather than through occupation of the mining areas.

### Lessons

The Unification Campaigns illustrate several principles of West African state-building:

**1. Coalition warfare requires political as well as military leadership.** Sundiata's achievement was holding together an alliance of diverse clans with different interests. Military victory at Kirina was necessary but not sufficient; the post-war settlement (represented by the Kouroukan Fouga) was equally important.

**2. Economic control matters more than territorial occupation.** Mali's power rested on controlling the gold trade, not on governing every village. The empire was a commercial hegemony organized to extract and protect wealth flows.

**3. Legitimation is essential for durable rule.** Military conquest created the empire; the ideological and institutional framework attributed to the Kouroukan Fouga gave it stability. The Mande world remembered Sundiata as a founder of social order, not merely as a conqueror.

**4. Delegated command enables expansion but risks fragmentation.** Trusting generals like Tiramaghan with independent operations allowed Mali to expand rapidly, but the same autonomy that made expansion possible could become centrifugal force if loyalty weakened.

**5. Cavalry superiority was decisive in the savanna.** Mali's mounted warriors dominated the Sahelian landscape; opponents without equivalent cavalry were at severe disadvantage.

### The Telescoping Problem

A significant methodological challenge in studying the Unification Campaigns is the tendency of oral tradition to "telescope" — to attribute the achievements of a dynasty to its founder. Sundiata is the culture hero of the Mande world; attributing all of Mali's expansion to him enhances his glory regardless of historical accuracy.

Some of the expansion credited to Sundiata may actually have occurred under his successors — Mansa Uli (his son), Mansa Wati, Sakura (the slave-usurper), or others. The tradition does not carefully distinguish between what Sundiata personally accomplished and what his dynasty accomplished in his name.

This does not mean the tradition is worthless as history. It means that claims about the specific scope and timing of Sundiata's personal campaigns carry the confidence level [Traditional/Oral] and cannot be verified independently. [D]

## For Fiction Writing

### Dramatic Potential

**Fiction Potential: HIGH**

The Unification Campaigns offer rich material for fiction — not the concentrated drama of Kirina itself, but the expansive canvas of empire-building with its blend of military action, political intrigue, and social transformation. A writer working with this material has access to:

**The epic of consolidation:**
Following a climactic victory, the real work begins. The Unification Campaigns are the story of building something durable from military success — a narrative of organization, negotiation, and vision that can be as compelling as battle if handled well.

**Multiple protagonists:**
Unlike the focused Sundiata-vs-Sumanguru conflict of Kirina, the Unification period offers multiple compelling figures: Tiramaghan conducting independent conquests in the west, Fakoli adapting to his new role after defection, Sundiata himself transitioning from warrior-prince to emperor-administrator, unnamed local leaders facing the choice of resistance or submission.

**The western campaigns:**
Tiramaghan's conquest of the Gambia region is a complete narrative arc: the mission, the journey, the battles, the settlement. It can be told as adventure, as meditation on conquest's human costs, or as exploration of the hunter-warrior ethos.

**The Kouroukan Fouga:**
A constitutional convention — the moment when the victors decide what kind of society they will build. This is not typical action-adventure material, but it offers dramatic opportunities for debate, negotiation, and the articulation of values. Characters with different visions for the new empire clash over principles that will shape their world for centuries.

**The moral complexity of empire:**
The Mande world that emerged from the Unification Campaigns was built on conquest. Populations were subjected, tribute extracted, autonomy limited. A sophisticated treatment can explore both the achievements (peace, trade, cultural florescence) and the costs (violence, subordination, loss of independence for the conquered).

### The Generals and Their Conquests

**Tiramaghan Traore's Western Campaigns:**

*Narrative arc:* Tiramaghan receives his mission from Sundiata, marches west with an army, conquers the Gambia region through a series of battles and negotiations, establishes settlements, and returns in triumph.

*Character elements:*
- The loyal general executing his lord's vision
- The hunter-warrior whose spiritual mastery enables military success
- The founder of lineages, transitioning from conqueror to ancestor
- The man who sees the ocean (if following traditions of Atlantic-reaching conquest)

*Scenes:*
- **The commissioning:** Sundiata entrusts Tiramaghan with the western campaigns, articulating the mission and his faith in his general
- **The march:** Weeks on campaign through the savanna, the rhythm of march and camp, the gradual westward progress
- **First major engagement:** A significant local polity resists; Tiramaghan must decide how to handle resistance
- **The river crossing:** The Gambia River as geographic and symbolic boundary; the challenges of moving an army across water
- **Victory and its costs:** Triumph over resistance, contemplation of what conquest means, the aftermath of battle
- **The founding:** Establishing a settlement, leaving warriors to become settlers, creating the structures that will persist after the army moves on
- **Reaching the sea:** The moment when a Sahel warrior sees the Atlantic, the edge of the known world
- **The return:** Journey back to Sundiata, reunion, recognition of achievement

**Fakoli Koroma's Transition:**

*Narrative arc:* Having defected from Sumanguru and fought at Kirina, Fakoli must find his place in the new order. A man who betrayed his blood for justice now serves a new lord, but the weight of what he did does not simply vanish.

*Character elements:*
- The defector who must prove his loyalty is genuine
- The blacksmith-sorcerer whose powers serve the coalition's enemies-turned-allies
- The man haunted by his uncle's memory (or survival? — Sumanguru's fate is ambiguous)
- The warrior carving out a place in the post-war hierarchy

*Scenes:*
- **Uncertain welcome:** Other coalition leaders may view Fakoli with suspicion; he must demonstrate his commitment
- **Campaign service:** Fighting alongside his new allies, proving himself in battle
- **Sumanguru's shadow:** Dreams, omens, or rumors that the sorcerer-king survived; Fakoli's psychological response
- **The Kouroukan Fouga:** Fakoli and the Koroma clan receiving their place in the new social order; recognition and legitimation
- **Personal costs:** Keleya (his wife whom Sumanguru stole) — what happened to her? Reunion, loss, or silence?

**Sundiata's Transformation:**

*Narrative arc:* The warrior-prince who won Kirina must become the emperor who builds an enduring state. This is a different kind of challenge than defeating Sumanguru in battle.

*Character elements:*
- The founder transitioning from war-maker to law-giver
- The charismatic leader managing a coalition of powerful subordinates
- The man who holds authority through personal presence, knowing that his successors will lack the same legitimacy
- The visionary who sees beyond conquest to the shape of what he is building

*Scenes:*
- **Return to Niani:** Establishing the capital, the physical base of the new empire
- **Managing allies:** Coalition partners who expect reward and influence; balancing competing claims
- **Sending Tiramaghan west:** Delegating authority, trusting a subordinate with a critical mission
- **Receiving submissions:** Former enemies who now seek accommodation; decisions about who to trust
- **The assembly at Kurukan Fuga:** The constitutional moment — addressing the gathered clans, articulating the principles that will govern their world
- **Private moments:** The weight of leadership; consultation with Sogolon (his mother, if she survives) or Bala Faseke (his griot); contemplation of what he has wrought
- **Awareness of mortality:** Sundiata knows he will die; he is building for those who come after

### The Assembly at Kurukan Fuga

The Kouroukan Fouga assembly offers a distinctive fictional opportunity — a scene of political creation that can be as dramatic as battle in its own way.

**Setting:**
A great clearing (Kurukan Fuga — "the clearing under the high trees") in the Manden heartland. Representatives of all the clans gathered. The visual spectacle of assembled contingents, each with their distinctive dress, banners, and retinue. The central space where Sundiata will address the assembly.

**Characters:**
- Sundiata, presiding — articulating the principles, managing debate, rendering judgment
- The principal generals: Tiramaghan, Fakoli, Kamandjan, others — as witnesses and participants
- Clan representatives with competing interests and visions
- Bala Faseke, the griot — recording, perhaps performing, certainly present as the voice of history
- Prominent women (the tradition mentions Sundiata's mother Sogolon, his sister Nana Triban, and other female figures)

**Dramatic possibilities:**
- Debate over the social order: should the nyamakala (occupational castes) have their traditional status confirmed? Should the san ankuya (joking relationships) be codified? What rights do women have?
- Tension between clans: the Keita claim supreme authority; other clans have their own pride and interests
- The question of the defeated: what status do former Sosso supporters receive? Can they be integrated, or are they permanently suspect?
- Personal confrontations: old rivalries, competing claims, the settling (or not settling) of scores
- The griot's role: Bala Faseke performing history even as it happens, his praise and blame shaping how events will be remembered
- The final proclamation: Sundiata's speech articulating the new order, the moment when the charter becomes law

**Tone:**
The Kouroukan Fouga can be treated as triumph (the crowning achievement of the liberation), as political drama (competing interests negotiating their futures), or as meditation (what kind of society do the victors want to build, and what compromises does that require?). The best treatments might combine all three.

### Sensory World

**The Campaign:**

*Sight:*
- The savanna landscape: golden grass in the dry season, green in the rains, scattered trees breaking the horizon
- Dust raised by marching feet and horses, coating everything
- Camp at evening: fires in the darkness, tents and lean-tos, silhouettes of warriors
- Battle: the chaos of close combat, dust and blood, weapons catching light
- Victory and defeat: bodies on the field, the wounded being tended or abandoned

*Sound:*
- War drums: communicating across distance, building morale before battle
- The balafon (wooden xylophone): music at camp, performance by griots
- Horses: the thunder of a cavalry charge, the snorting and whinnying of mounts
- Battle: clash of weapons, war cries, screams of the wounded, the sounds that haunt survivors
- Silence after battle: the absence of noise that is itself a sound

*Smell:*
- Wood smoke from campfires, constant in camp life
- Horses and their leavings
- Blood and death on the battlefield
- The dry Sahelian air, the harmattan dust
- Cooking food: millet porridge, roasted meat, the spices of Mande cuisine

*Touch:*
- The weight of weapons: spear, sword, shield, bow
- The discomfort of the march: sore feet, aching muscles, the burden of equipment
- Heat: the Sahelian sun beating down, the relief of shade
- The horse beneath a cavalryman: the connection between rider and mount
- Victory or defeat: the adrenaline ebbing, exhaustion setting in

*Taste:*
- Campaign rations: simple food, not the elaborate cooking of home
- Thirst: water is precious; the relief of reaching a river or well
- Kola nuts: bitter, stimulating, shared among warriors
- Blood: for those who taste it in battle, the metallic tang

**The Kouroukan Fouga:**

*Sight:*
- The great clearing: space enough for hundreds of representatives
- Clan delegations in their distinctive attire: the diversity of the assembled peoples
- Sundiata at the center: the founder in his moment of legislative authority
- The griots: visual performers as well as verbal ones
- The Manden landscape: trees surrounding the clearing, sky above

*Sound:*
- Oratory: Sundiata speaking, clan representatives responding
- The griots: praise songs, historical recitations, the verbal artistry that frames the proceedings
- Debate: voices raised in argument (politely or not)
- Affirmation: the collective response when decisions are accepted
- Music: the balafon, drums, the formal musical elements of Mande assembly

*Smell:*
- Gathered humanity: hundreds of people in the tropical heat
- Ceremonial incense and aromatics
- Food being prepared for the feasting that will follow
- The natural environment: trees, grass, earth

### Speech and Voice

**Modes of address:**

- **Titles:** Sundiata as Mansa; generals as Faama or Keletigui; clan chiefs by their appropriate honorifics
- **Praise names:** Important figures accumulate names that celebrate their deeds; these are used in formal address
- **Griot formulae:** The griots have their own specialized vocabulary for praise, genealogy, and historical recitation
- **Joking relationships:** Between clans with sanankuya, speech can be startlingly informal — insults exchanged where others would require politeness

**Rhetorical styles:**

- **Proverbs:** Mande speech is rich in proverbial wisdom; characters demonstrate eloquence through apt proverbs
- **Genealogical invocation:** Naming ancestors, connecting present to past, grounding claims in lineage
- **Formal oratory:** The Kouroukan Fouga speeches would use elevated, formal language
- **Military directness:** Warriors among themselves may speak more bluntly
- **Griot eloquence:** The professional historians are wordsmiths; their speech is elaborate, musical, performative

**Dialogue opportunities:**

- **Sundiata commissioning Tiramaghan:** "The west is yours to take. I give you armies, I give you trust, I give you Mali's name to carry. Return to me with victory, or do not return."

- **Tiramaghan addressing his army:** "We march where Mali has never marched. The lands ahead do not know our name. They will learn it."

- **A local chief deciding whether to resist:** "They come with horses and iron, these men from the east. My father's father never bowed to strangers. But my father's father never saw an army like this..."

- **Fakoli contemplating his position:** "I betrayed my uncle's blood. The griots will sing of it for generations. But they will also sing that I chose right. Let them weigh the two."

- **Sundiata at Kurukan Fuga:** "We were scattered clans, each alone against the sorcerer's power. Now we are Manden — one people, under one law. What we build today, our children's children will inherit."

### Key Relationships

**Sundiata and his generals:**

The relationship between Sundiata and his commanders — Tiramaghan, Fakoli, Kamandjan, and others — is the structural framework of the Unification period. These are not subordinates in a modern military hierarchy but allies bound by oaths, shared history, and mutual interest. Elements to explore:

- **Trust and delegation:** Sundiata gives Tiramaghan enormous responsibility; that trust is itself a relationship of profound significance
- **Managing ambition:** Each general is powerful in his own right; Sundiata must ensure their energies serve the coalition rather than personal aggrandizement
- **Recognition and reward:** How does Sundiata honor his generals? The Kouroukan Fouga settlements presumably included provisions for them
- **Succession anxiety:** What happens when Sundiata dies? His generals will outlive him; their relationship with his heirs is not guaranteed

**Conquerors and conquered:**

The Unification Campaigns created relationships of domination that would last for centuries. Fiction can explore these from multiple perspectives:

- **Local leaders facing conquest:** The moment when resistance becomes clearly futile; the decision to submit; the negotiation of terms
- **Warriors on both sides:** Men who fight against Mali, lose, and must find their place in the new order
- **Women:** Conquest affects families; wives and daughters of the defeated, marriages that seal political alliances
- **Children:** Growing up in a world shaped by conquest they did not experience but whose consequences define their lives

**Tiramaghan and the western peoples:**

The western campaigns created a specific set of relationships:

- **Resistance leaders:** Chiefs and warriors who fought against Tiramaghan; their perspective on the conqueror
- **Accommodators:** Those who submitted quickly and found advantage in the new order
- **Settlers:** Warriors who stayed behind to become the ancestors of western Traore lineages
- **The conquered peoples:** Mandinka identity spread through these campaigns; those who adopted it, those who resisted cultural as well as political integration

### Scene Opportunities

**The Return from Kirina:**

Sundiata and his army returning to the Manden heartland after the victory. Elements:
- Triumph and exhaustion: the war is won, but the work is beginning
- Reception at Niani (or wherever the capital is established)
- The transition from war footing to governance
- First decisions about what comes next

**The Commissioning of Tiramaghan:**

Sundiata entrusts the western campaigns to his general. Elements:
- The formal setting: court, witnesses, ritual
- The weight of the mission: this is not a raid but the creation of imperial reach
- Tiramaghan's acceptance: the oaths reaffirmed, the commitment made
- The personal dimension: what do these men feel about each other?

**A Western Battle:**

Tiramaghan's forces engage a local polity that has chosen to resist. Elements:
- Pre-battle: reconnaissance, deployment, Tiramaghan's commands
- The engagement: cavalry charges, infantry clash, the chaos of combat
- Crisis moment: when the battle could go either way
- Resolution: victory (or perhaps near-defeat overcome)
- Aftermath: the treatment of defeated enemies, the cost in Mande lives

**The Founding of a Settlement:**

After conquest, Tiramaghan establishes a permanent presence. Elements:
- Choosing the site: strategic, agricultural, or other considerations
- The ritual of foundation: sacrifices, blessings, the establishment of sacred spaces
- Selecting who stays: warriors becoming settlers, leaving the campaign for a new life
- The general moving on: Tiramaghan continues the campaign while his settlement remains

**Submission of Ghana:**

The incorporation of the old Wagadu polity. Elements:
- The negotiation: Soninke leaders facing Mande power
- What they retain: their merchant networks, their cultural identity, their elite status (within limits)
- What they lose: independence, the memory of having once ruled the region themselves
- The Wangara: the merchant communities whose commercial role transcends political boundaries

**The Kouroukan Fouga:**

The constitutional assembly. Elements:
- The gathering: hundreds of representatives, the visual spectacle
- Opening ceremonies: invocations, performances, the establishment of the sacred context
- Debate: competing visions, clan interests, the hard work of negotiation
- Sundiata's role: presiding, judging, articulating the principles
- The final proclamation: the moment when the charter is declared
- Celebration: feasting, music, the release after formal proceedings

**Sundiata's Death:**

The founder's end, however the tradition presents it. Elements:
- The manner: drowning, assassination, peaceful death — the tradition offers multiple versions
- The succession question: which son inherits? What roles do the generals play?
- Grief: the response of those who built the empire with him
- Legacy: the immediate sense that something irreplaceable has been lost
- Continuity: the structures survive the man; Mali persists

### Worldview and Values

**Building the lasting thing:**

The Unification Campaigns are about creating something that will endure. This is a different challenge from winning a battle; it requires vision, patience, and the ability to think beyond immediate victory. Characters operating in this worldview:

- Think in terms of generations, not just moments
- Value institution-building alongside military achievement
- Recognize that conquest without consolidation is hollow
- Accept that compromise may be necessary for durability

**The weight of foundation:**

Sundiata is not just conquering; he is founding a civilization. The decisions made in this period will shape his people for centuries. Characters who understand this weight:

- Feel the responsibility intensely
- Debate principles as seriously as tactics
- Consider how their choices will be remembered
- Accept that they are laying down obligations for their descendants

**The hunter-warrior ethos:**

The *donso* tradition that shapes figures like Tiramaghan involves:

- Mastery of *nyama*: spiritual power accumulated through hunting and warfare
- The liminal position: operating between the human and spirit worlds
- The code of the hunter: courage, endurance, skill, spiritual discipline
- The transformation of dangerous power into social good

**The clan as fundamental:**

Identity in this world is collective as much as individual. Characters:

- Carry their ancestors with them
- Act in the name of their lineages, not just themselves
- Think about how their deeds will affect their clan's standing
- Navigate the web of clan relationships (alliance, rivalry, sanankuya) that structures society

**Justice and power:**

The Unification Campaigns are justified, in the tradition's telling, by justice. Sundiata fought to liberate his people from tyranny; his victory established a just order. But this framework also raises questions:

- What justice is offered to those who were conquered?
- Does liberation for one people justify domination of others?
- The Kouroukan Fouga establishes social categories including slavery — is this just?
- Power legitimates itself through narratives of justice; how much can those narratives be trusted?

Fiction that engages with these questions can achieve moral complexity beyond simple heroic narrative.

## Sources

### Primary Sources

**Sundiata Epic (Oral Tradition):**

The primary source for the Unification Campaigns is the Sundiata epic, transmitted orally by griots for centuries and recorded in written form in the 20th century. Key editions:

- **D.T. Niane, *Sundiata: An Epic of Old Mali*** (1960; English trans. G.D. Pickett, 1965): The most widely read version, a literary rendering based on griot Djeli Mamadou Kouyate's telling. Accessible but smooths over variations between traditions. Provides the core narrative of Kirina and its aftermath, including the Kouroukan Fouga.

- **David C. Conrad, *Sunjata: A West African Epic of the Mande Peoples*** (2004): A scholarly edition presenting multiple versions with extensive notes. Better for understanding how the tradition varies across griots and regions. Essential for serious study.

- **John William Johnson, *The Epic of Son-Jara: A West African Tradition*** (1986, revised 2003): Another scholarly edition with detailed commentary.

**Ibn Khaldun (c. 1377):**

The Tunisian historian mentions Mali's dynastic history in his *Kitab al-'Ibar*, providing external confirmation of the empire's existence and basic chronology. He does not describe the Unification Campaigns in detail but attests to the general pattern of expansion. [E]

**Later Arabic Sources (al-Umari, Ibn Battuta):**

These sources describe Mali in its maturity (14th century), not its founding. However, they confirm that the empire Sundiata founded was real and powerful, validating the tradition's basic claims. [E]

### Secondary Sources

- **Levtzion, Nehemia, *Ancient Ghana and Mali*** (1973): The standard scholarly treatment of the Sahel empires. Careful use of Arabic sources and oral tradition. Essential for historical context.

- **Gomez, Michael A., *African Dominion: A New History of Empire in Early and Medieval West Africa*** (2018): The most recent major synthesis. Centers African perspectives and provides fresh analysis.

- **Conrad, David C., *Empires of Medieval West Africa*** (2005): Accessible overview including substantial treatment of Mali's formation.

- **Austen, Ralph A., ed., *In Search of Sunjata: The Mande Oral Epic as History, Literature, and Performance*** (1999): Scholarly essays examining the tradition from multiple angles.

- **Jansen, Jan, *Epopee, histoire, societe: Le cas de Soundjata*** (2001): French-language scholarly treatment of the epic tradition with attention to its social context.

- **UNESCO Documentation of the Kouroukan Fouga** (2009): The inscription of the Manden Charter on the Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity includes documentation of the tradition.

### Source Assessment

The Unification Campaigns are documented almost entirely through oral tradition. The Arabic sources confirm that Mali existed and expanded, but they do not describe the campaigns themselves. The Sundiata epic provides narrative detail but carries all the limitations of oral transmission: telescoping of events, heroic elaboration, political shaping of memory.

For historical purposes:
- The general fact of Mali's expansion in the mid-13th century is [Probable]
- The existence of generals like Tiramaghan and Fakoli who conducted campaigns is [Traditional/Oral]
- Specific details of campaigns, battles, and settlements are [Traditional/Oral]
- The Kouroukan Fouga as a foundational moment is [Debated]; its content as recorded is likely accumulated tradition

For fiction purposes, this is an asset. The tradition provides the framework (scope of expansion, key figures, constitutional assembly) while leaving abundant room for creative development of specific events, characters, and scenes.
