## Overview

The Battle of Anfao in April 1493 was one of the most consequential political events in medieval West African history — a military confrontation that ended one dynasty, founded another, and transformed the ideological basis of the Songhai Empire. When the general Muhammad Ture defeated and overthrew Sunni Baru, he was not merely seizing a throne; he was executing a religious revolution. The Sonni dynasty that had ruled Songhai for centuries and had expanded it into the largest empire West Africa had yet seen was replaced by a new dynasty committed to Islamic legitimacy, administrative reform, and integration into the wider Muslim world. The battle itself may have lasted only hours; its consequences shaped the next century of Sahelian history. [E]

The conflict's origins lay in the death of Sunni Ali, the brilliant warrior-king who had built Songhai's territorial dominance through relentless campaigning from 1464 to 1492. Sunni Ali was a military genius but a religious problematic: the Islamic scholarly establishment of Timbuktu considered him at best insufficiently pious and at worst an active enemy of their faith. When Sunni Ali drowned in November 1492, his son Sunni Baru inherited both his throne and his religious ambiguity. The Timbuktu ulama (Islamic scholars) demanded that Sunni Baru make a public profession of faith — effectively repudiating his father's religious stance and committing himself to rule according to Islamic law. Sunni Baru refused. [E]

Muhammad Ture, a senior military commander of Soninke origin who had served Sunni Ali loyally but whose Islamic piety placed him closer to the scholarly establishment, positioned himself as the champion of true religion against an apostate ruler. The language of religious obligation — the duty of Muslims to overthrow an impious king — legitimized what might otherwise have been mere ambition. Muhammad rallied supporters, including military commanders who shared his religious orientation, and marched against his new sovereign. At Anfao, the forces met, Muhammad triumphed, and Sunni Baru fled into exile and obscurity. Muhammad Ture adopted the new title "Askia," marking a complete break with the past, and embarked on a reign that would transform Songhai into the most sophisticated Islamic state sub-Saharan Africa had yet produced. [E]

## Background and Causes

### Political Context: The Legacy of Sunni Ali

To understand the coup of 1493, one must first understand what Sunni Ali had built and what tensions he had created. Sunni Ali Ber (r. 1464-1492) was arguably the greatest military commander in medieval West African history. In twenty-eight years of nearly continuous warfare, he transformed the small Songhai state centered on Gao into a vast empire controlling the entire Middle Niger — from Timbuktu to Djenne to Hausaland's western approaches. He captured Timbuktu from its Tuareg occupiers (1468), conquered the wealthy trading city of Djenne after a legendary siege (c. 1468-1473), and pushed back the Mossi raiders who had terrorized the Sahel. By the time of his death, Songhai was the dominant power of the Western Sudan. [E]

But Sunni Ali's relationship with Islam was, by the standards of the Timbuktu scholarly community, deeply inadequate. The *Tarikh al-Sudan* and *Tarikh al-Fattash* — the primary sources for this period, both written by scholars affiliated with Timbuktu's intellectual establishment — portray Sunni Ali as a nominal Muslim who secretly practiced indigenous Songhai religion, who showed contempt for Islamic learning, and who persecuted the ulama of Timbuktu after capturing the city. These accounts are certainly biased: the scholars had reasons to demonize a ruler who had marginalized them and to celebrate his successor who patronized them. But the underlying tension between Sunni Ali's warrior tradition and the scholars' religious expectations was real. [E/D]

The nature of Sunni Ali's religious failing, from the ulama's perspective, was not simply personal impiety. It was a matter of governance. Sunni Ali did not submit his rule to Islamic law as interpreted by the scholarly establishment. He maintained traditional Songhai royal rituals, patronized indigenous religious practitioners, and — most critically — did not grant the qadis (Islamic judges) and scholars the political authority they believed proper Islamic governance required. Whether this made him an apostate, a syncretist, or merely a pragmatic ruler navigating religious diversity is a matter of interpretation. What matters for understanding the coup is that powerful religious interests saw him as a threat to proper Islamic order. [D]

### Sunni Ali's Death: November 1492

Sunni Ali died in November 1492 while returning from a military campaign. The chronicles report that he drowned while crossing a body of water called Koni — possibly the Niger River or a tributary. The circumstances are mysterious: medieval drownings sometimes concealed less natural deaths, and the timing (at the height of his power, with succession unsettled) is suspicious. The sources, hostile to Sunni Ali, present his death as divine punishment for his impieties, but this is conventional religious interpretation rather than evidence. [E for the death; D for circumstances]

The death of such a dominant figure inevitably created a succession crisis. Sunni Ali had many sons, but the Sonni dynasty did not have clear primogeniture rules. His son Sunni Baru (also spelled Sunni Baro, Sonni Bakori) succeeded him — the sources suggest this was the expected succession — but Sunni Baru inherited a volatile situation. His father's military achievements gave him a powerful state; his father's religious controversies gave him powerful enemies. [E]

### The Religious Confrontation: Sunni Baru's Refusal

The decisive moment came when the Islamic scholarly establishment, likely through intermediaries and formal religious channels, demanded that Sunni Baru make a clear public profession of faith (*shahada*) — the Islamic declaration that "there is no god but God, and Muhammad is His messenger." This demand was not merely ceremonial. It was a test of whether the new ruler would commit himself to Islamic governance and repudiate the religious ambiguity of his father's reign. [E]

The *Tarikh al-Fattash* records Sunni Baru's response:

> "I will not make the profession of faith, for it would mean I was abandoning the religion of my father and my grandfather."

Whether Sunni Baru was expressing genuine religious conviction (loyalty to traditional Songhai practices), political defiance (refusing to submit to scholarly interference), or simply misjudging the political situation cannot be determined. The statement may even be a literary construction by hostile chroniclers. But the effect was clear: by this refusal, Sunni Baru placed himself outside the Islamic community as the scholars defined it. He made himself vulnerable to religious challenge. [E/T]

The refusal gave Muhammad Ture the theological justification he needed. In Islamic political theory, a ruler who was not a proper Muslim — or who had apostatized from Islam — could legitimately be deposed. Indeed, Muslims might have a *duty* to replace such a ruler with one who would govern according to Islamic law. What might otherwise have been a mere power grab could now be framed as a religious obligation. [P]

### Strategic Objectives

**Muhammad Ture's Objectives:**
- Overthrow Sunni Baru and seize the throne of Songhai
- Establish a new dynasty, breaking completely with the discredited Sonni line
- Secure religious legitimacy by positioning himself as the champion of Islam
- Gain the support of the Timbuktu ulama and their networks
- Create a new political order based on Islamic principles of governance [P]

**Sunni Baru's Objectives:**
- Maintain his father's throne and the Sonni dynasty's rule
- Suppress the rebellion before it could gather strength
- Preserve his father's legacy and governing approach
- Prevent the scholarly establishment from gaining political power over the crown [P]

## The Belligerents

### Muhammad Ture's Rebel Coalition

**Commander:** Muhammad Ture (later Askia Muhammad I)

Muhammad Ture was a senior military commander, one of the generals who had served Sunni Ali throughout his campaigns of conquest. His family background is significant: he was a Soninke, a member of the ethnic group that had once ruled the Ghana Empire centuries earlier and remained prominent in Sahelian commerce and military life. His clan name, Ture (also Touré), indicates Soninke origins, not Songhai royal blood. He was, in terms of the Sonni dynasty, an outsider — a professional soldier, not a prince. [E]

The *Tarikh al-Fattash* provides genealogical information, stating that Muhammad's father was Abu Bakr and that the family had connections to earlier Sahelian Muslim communities. His Islamic piety, whether reflecting sincere conviction or political calculation (the sources favor the former), distinguished him from Sunni Ali's more ambivalent religious stance. He appears in the chronicles as a devout Muslim who maintained the practices the scholars valued. [P]

**Forces:**

The sources do not provide detailed information about the composition of Muhammad Ture's forces. What can be inferred:

- **Military defectors:** Some portion of the Songhai army defected to Muhammad's cause. His position as a senior commander meant he had personal loyalty from troops he had led, and his religious positioning attracted officers and soldiers who shared his Islamic orientation. [P]
- **Scholarly networks:** While the ulama themselves were not warriors, their social networks extended throughout Songhai society. Merchants, urban elites, and Islamic legal scholars had families, clients, and dependents who might be mobilized. The scholars' support gave Muhammad legitimacy that translated into material support. [P]
- **Disaffected elements:** Any regime transition creates winners and losers. Those who had been marginalized under Sunni Ali or who feared marginalization under Sunni Baru might have rallied to Muhammad's banner. [S]

**Strengths:**
- Religious legitimacy: Muhammad's cause was framed as a jihad (religious struggle) against an impious ruler, giving it moral authority
- Scholarly support: The Timbuktu ulama and their networks provided ideological backing and material resources
- Military experience: Muhammad was a proven commander who knew Songhai military capabilities and the officers who led them
- Initiative: As the challenger, Muhammad controlled the timing and circumstances of the confrontation [P]

**Weaknesses:**
- Outsider status: Muhammad was not of the Songhai royal lineage; he was a Soninke usurper
- Untested legitimacy: His claim to rule rested entirely on religious grounds and military victory
- Unknown loyalty: It was uncertain how much of the Songhai military establishment would defect versus remain loyal to the dynasty [P]

### Sunni Baru's Loyalist Forces

**Commander:** Sunni Baru (son of Sunni Ali)

Sunni Baru had inherited the throne by dynastic succession — he was the son of the previous ruler, following normal patterns of succession. But he had also inherited his father's religious controversies without his father's military reputation. He had not led the great campaigns that built the empire; he was merely the heir to someone who had. [P]

The sources say almost nothing about Sunni Baru's personality, capabilities, or experience before the confrontation with Muhammad Ture. He appears as a figure defined by what he refused (the Islamic profession of faith) rather than by what he achieved. This silence may reflect the reality that his reign was brief and his character unremarkable, or it may reflect the chroniclers' lack of interest in humanizing a ruler they considered illegitimate. [P]

**Forces:**

Sunni Baru nominally commanded the regular Songhai military establishment — the cavalry, infantry, and river forces that had conquered half of West Africa under his father. This was one of the most formidable military machines in African history.

- **Songhai cavalry:** The core of Songhai military power, drawn from the warrior aristocracy, mounted on horses acquired through trans-Saharan trade [E]
- **Infantry forces:** Foot soldiers armed with iron weapons, including archers [E]
- **Provincial troops:** Forces from conquered territories that owed loyalty to the Songhai throne [P]
- **Traditional supporters:** Those whose interests were tied to the existing dynasty and who had reason to fear a new regime [S]

**Strengths:**
- Legitimate succession: Sunni Baru was the son of the previous ruler, following established patterns
- Institutional control: He nominally commanded the state apparatus and military establishment
- Territorial base: The Sonni dynasty had ruled from Gao for centuries; the capital was the center of their power [P]

**Weaknesses:**
- Religious vulnerability: His refusal to profess Islam made him a legitimate target for religiously-motivated opposition
- Unearned reputation: He had not built the empire his father conquered; he only inherited it
- Factional opposition: Elements within the military and court who favored Islamic reform might defect
- Speed of challenge: Muhammad Ture moved quickly, giving Sunni Baru little time to consolidate [P]

## The Battle / Campaign

### Approach and Deployment

The sources provide frustratingly little tactical detail about the Battle of Anfao. The *Tarikh al-Sudan* and *Tarikh al-Fattash* mention the battle as a decisive event but do not describe the military operations in the detail they provide for, say, Sunni Ali's siege of Djenne or the later Battle of Tondibi. This silence likely reflects the chroniclers' interests: they cared about the religious and political significance of Muhammad's victory, not the tactical details of how it was achieved.

What can be reconstructed:

**Timing:** The battle occurred in April 1493, approximately five months after Sunni Ali's death in November 1492. This interval suggests a period of political maneuvering: Sunni Baru establishing his rule, the ulama issuing their demand, Sunni Baru's refusal, Muhammad Ture organizing his rebellion, and the gathering of forces. [E]

**Location:** Anfao (variant spellings: Angoo, Ankoo) was likely located northeast of Gao, in the Songhai heartland. The precise location has not been archaeologically identified, and the sources do not provide geographic coordinates. That the battle occurred in this region suggests Muhammad Ture moved toward the capital to confront Sunni Baru rather than waiting for the king to suppress his rebellion. [P]

**Muhammad's advance:** As the challenger, Muhammad Ture had to take the initiative. He would have needed to gather his forces, likely at some location outside the immediate reach of royal authority, and then march toward Gao. His advance forced Sunni Baru to respond. [P]

**Sunni Baru's response:** The king could have chosen to defend behind fortifications, to await reinforcements from distant provinces, or to meet Muhammad in the field. He apparently chose the latter — marching out to confront the rebellion directly. Whether this was strategic confidence, political necessity (needing to demonstrate strength), or simply the standard Songhai military approach cannot be determined. [S]

### Course of the Battle

The actual fighting at Anfao is not described in the sources. What we know:

#### Phase 1: Engagement

The two armies met at Anfao. Both forces would have consisted primarily of cavalry — the standard arm of Sahelian warfare — with supporting infantry. Songhai battles typically involved cavalry charges, archery exchanges, and close combat with iron weapons. The warrior aristocracy on both sides would have been the decisive element. [P]

#### Phase 2: The Turning Point

At some point, the battle turned in Muhammad Ture's favor. The sources do not explain how. Several possibilities suggest themselves:

- **Defections:** Some of Sunni Baru's forces may have defected to Muhammad during the battle itself, recognizing the religious legitimacy of his cause or calculating that his victory was inevitable. Such battlefield switches were not uncommon in succession conflicts. [S]
- **Superior leadership:** Muhammad Ture was a veteran commander; Sunni Baru may have been less experienced in battlefield command. The skill gap could have been decisive. [S]
- **Morale collapse:** Sunni Baru's refusal to profess Islam may have undermined his army's willingness to fight for him. Soldiers who believed they were fighting for an impious ruler might have broken more readily. [S]
- **Tactical superiority:** Muhammad may simply have deployed his forces better or executed superior battlefield tactics. [S]

The lack of detail is itself significant: had there been a dramatic tactical moment, the chroniclers might have recorded it. The silence suggests either that the battle was unremarkable in its conduct (a straightforward victory) or that the chroniclers simply did not know or care about the military specifics.

#### Phase 3: Sunni Baru's Flight

The outcome was decisive. Sunni Baru's forces were defeated, and Sunni Baru himself fled the battlefield. He did not die in the fighting but retreated, gathering what loyalists he could. [E]

### Decisive Moment

Without detailed accounts, identifying a single decisive moment is impossible. But the *Tarikh al-Sudan*'s presentation suggests that the outcome was clear-cut: Muhammad won, Sunni Baru lost, and the Sonni dynasty ended. There are no references to prolonged campaigning, sieges of Gao, or continued resistance by Sunni Baru's forces. The battle appears to have been militarily and politically decisive in a single engagement. [P]

This decisiveness suggests several possibilities:
- The Songhai military establishment largely accepted Muhammad's victory and did not resist
- Sunni Baru's support was shallower than his father's, making resistance impossible
- The religious framing of the conflict made continued loyalty to an "apostate" king difficult to justify
- Muhammad moved quickly to consolidate control before opposition could form [P]

## Aftermath

### Immediate Consequences

**Sunni Baru's exile and death:**

The *Tarikh al-Sudan* reports that Sunni Baru fled to the region of Ayorou after his defeat. He did not attempt to rally supporters for a counterattack or establish a rival power center. The sources indicate he eventually died in exile, his dynasty extinguished. [E]

Whether Sunni Baru was killed, died of natural causes, or simply faded into obscurity is not recorded. The brevity of the chronicle account suggests the latter: had there been a dramatic pursuit and execution, or a notable subsequent conflict, the sources would likely mention it. Sunni Baru seems to have simply disappeared from history — the last of a dynasty that had ruled for centuries. [P]

**Muhammad Ture's accession:**

Muhammad Ture took power, but he faced an immediate problem: his legitimacy. He was not of the royal lineage. He had seized the throne by force. His religious justification was powerful among the scholarly class but might not convince everyone. He needed to establish a new basis for his rule.

His solution was to adopt an entirely new title: **Askia**. Rather than claiming to be "Sunni" (the dynastic title of the line he had overthrown), he broke with the past entirely. "Askia" marked a fresh start, a new dynasty, a different kind of rule. [E]

**The meaning of "Askia":**

The etymology and meaning of the title "Askia" have been debated by scholars:

- **A dignity title:** Muhammad may have adopted or invented a title that signified his new status without claiming continuity with the Sonni line. [P]
- **Linguistic origin:** Some analyses suggest the word may have Songhai or Soninke roots meaning "forceful" or "one who uses force." [S]
- **Folk etymology:** A popular tradition, recorded in later oral histories, claims that "Askia" derived from the Songhai phrase "A si kiya" — meaning "He is not it" or "He will not be" — allegedly shouted by Sunni Ali's daughter or widow, lamenting that the usurper could never be a true king. This etymology is almost certainly folk-etymology rather than accurate linguistics, but it is culturally significant: it preserves the memory that Muhammad's claim to the throne was contested, that some did not accept his right to rule. [T]

Regardless of etymology, the title stuck. Muhammad became Askia Muhammad, his successors were all "Askia," and the dynasty founded at Anfao would rule Songhai until its destruction by Moroccan invasion in 1591. [E]

### Long-Term Impact

The coup of 1493 had consequences that extended far beyond a mere change of rulers:

**Transformation of Songhai's ideological basis:**

Under the Sonni dynasty, Songhai had been a military empire. Its legitimacy derived from the power of its warriors, the personal authority of rulers like Sunni Ali, and traditional Songhai notions of kingship that incorporated indigenous religious elements alongside nominal Islam. The relationship between the ruling house and the Islamic scholarly establishment was tense; Sunni Ali had allegedly persecuted the ulama.

Under the Askia dynasty, Songhai became explicitly an *Islamic* state. Its legitimacy derived from religious sanction, scholarly approval, and integration into the wider Muslim world. The Askia rulers sought guidance from Islamic jurists, established qadi courts to administer Sharia law, patronized scholarship, and performed public acts of Islamic piety. The contrast was deliberate and politically calculated. [E]

**The Askia's hajj (1496-1497):**

The most dramatic act of Muhammad's legitimation project came just three years after seizing power. In 1496, he departed on the *hajj* — the pilgrimage to Mecca that is one of the Five Pillars of Islam. The journey took approximately eighteen months.

In Cairo, Muhammad sought and obtained an audience with the Abbasid caliph — the shadow caliph maintained by the Mamluk sultans, who held no real political power but retained enormous symbolic authority as the nominal spiritual leader of Sunni Islam. The caliph formally invested Muhammad with authority over the Sudan (the Sahel region), granting him the title "Caliph of Takrur" or "Caliph of the Sudan." [E]

This investiture was precisely what Muhammad's usurper status required. He could now claim that his authority derived not merely from military victory but from the highest religious sanction available in Sunni Islam. He had walked to Mecca, fulfilled his religious obligation, received the caliph's blessing, and returned as God's appointed ruler. The general who had seized power by force was now a caliph in his own right. [E]

**Administrative reforms:**

Muhammad Ture's reign (1493-1528) was characterized by extensive administrative reforms designed to create durable institutions. He formalized a provincial system with appointed governors, established Islamic courts throughout the empire, systematized taxation and bureaucracy, and sought written guidance from Islamic jurists (notably Muhammad al-Maghili of Tlemcen) on proper governance. The Songhai Empire became the most administratively sophisticated state sub-Saharan Africa had yet seen. [E]

**Timbuktu's golden age:**

Under Muhammad's patronage, Timbuktu reached its zenith as a center of Islamic learning. The city's mosques and schools attracted scholars from across the Muslim world. Libraries accumulated tens of thousands of manuscripts. The intellectual tradition that would produce figures like Ahmad Baba al-Timbukti flourished under Askia protection. The scholarly establishment that had supported Muhammad's coup was repaid with patronage, protection, and influence. [E]

### Treatment of the Defeated

The sources say relatively little about what happened to the supporters of the Sonni dynasty after Muhammad's victory:

- **Sunni Baru:** Fled into exile and died in obscurity
- **The Sonni family:** The dynasty's fate is not detailed; they presumably lost their positions but may not have been systematically eliminated
- **Military loyalists:** Many likely transferred their allegiance to the new regime; those who resisted are not described

The relative silence suggests that Muhammad's victory was followed by consolidation rather than purge. A new ruler who wanted to govern an empire needed the people who knew how to run it. Mass executions of the defeated would have created enemies and disrupted administration. The practical approach was to allow former loyalists to switch sides. [P/S]

## Military Analysis

### Tactics and Strategy

**Strategic level:**

Muhammad Ture's strategy was primarily political, using military force to execute a plan that had been prepared through religious and diplomatic groundwork. He understood that:

- Religious legitimacy mattered in Songhai society, and Sunni Baru's refusal to profess Islam made him vulnerable
- The scholarly establishment could be mobilized as an alliance partner
- Speed was essential: challenging the new king before he could consolidate
- A decisive battlefield victory would end the conflict quickly, preventing prolonged civil war [P]

**Operational level:**

Muhammad's campaign was brief — the interval from Sunni Ali's death (November 1492) to the Battle of Anfao (April 1493) was approximately five months. This suggests efficient organization: recruiting forces, coordinating with allies, and moving to confront Sunni Baru before he could prepare. [P]

**Tactical level:**

Nothing is known about the tactics employed at Anfao specifically. Songhai warfare generally relied on cavalry — mounted warriors armed with swords, lances, and bows — supported by infantry. Battles were typically decided by cavalry engagements and by the morale of the forces involved. [E for general Songhai warfare; S for Anfao specifically]

### Technology

Both sides would have employed similar military technology:

- **Horses:** Acquired through trans-Saharan trade, essential for the cavalry that dominated Sahelian warfare. Quality horses were prestigious and militarily valuable. [E]
- **Iron weapons:** Swords, spears, lances, arrowheads. West African ironworking was highly developed. [E]
- **Armor:** Quilted cotton armor, leather armor, and possibly some chain mail for elite warriors. [E]
- **Bows:** Both composite bows for cavalry and larger bows for infantry archers. [E]
- **No firearms:** Gunpowder weapons did not feature in West African warfare until the late sixteenth century, after the Moroccan invasion. [E]

### Terrain and Environment

**The Middle Niger environment:**

- The battle took place in the Sahel, the semi-arid belt south of the Sahara
- Terrain: flat or gently rolling savanna, scattered trees, seasonal watercourses
- Season: April falls in the late dry season / early hot season, before the rains
- Conditions would have favored cavalry operations on open ground [P]

**Logistical considerations:**

- Both armies operated in familiar territory near the Songhai heartland
- Supply lines would have been short
- Water sources were a consideration in dry season campaigning [P]

### Lessons

The Battle of Anfao demonstrates several patterns relevant to understanding medieval African military-political history:

1. **Religious legitimacy was a military asset:** Muhammad's framing of the conflict as a religious struggle attracted supporters and may have undermined Sunni Baru's forces' willingness to fight

2. **Succession crises were vulnerable moments:** The death of a strong ruler created opportunities for challengers; Sunni Baru's inability to match his father's reputation made him vulnerable

3. **Military commanders could become kings:** Muhammad was a general, not a prince; his career shows that military success could create political opportunities outside normal succession

4. **Speed mattered:** Muhammad's quick action prevented Sunni Baru from consolidating; decisive early action shaped outcomes

5. **Victory required consolidation:** Muhammad's post-battle actions — the new title, the hajj, the administrative reforms — were as important as the battle itself [P]

### The Sunni Ali Controversy and Its Relevance to the Coup

Much scholarly debate has focused on whether the *Tarikh al-Sudan* and *Tarikh al-Fattash* accurately portray Sunni Ali as an enemy of Islam or whether they represent biased accounts by scholars whom Ali had marginalized. This debate directly affects how we understand the coup:

**If Sunni Ali was genuinely hostile to Islam:** Then Muhammad Ture's religious revolution was addressing a real problem, and Sunni Baru's continuation of his father's policies genuinely threatened Islamic interests in Songhai.

**If Sunni Ali's impiety has been exaggerated:** Then the coup was more about political power than religious reform, and Muhammad Ture exploited religious rhetoric to legitimize a power grab.

The truth likely lies between these positions. Sunni Ali probably practiced some form of religious syncretism and probably did marginalize the scholarly establishment — but the chroniclers also had reasons to exaggerate. The coup was both religiously and politically motivated, with the two dimensions reinforcing each other. [D]

### The Question of Muhammad's Origins

Some oral traditions suggest that Muhammad Ture himself may have had royal or quasi-royal connections that the written chronicles do not acknowledge. Certain genealogies link his family to earlier Sahelian Muslim dynasties or suggest higher status than "mere" general. If true, this would complicate the narrative of an outsider usurper seizing power purely through military force and religious legitimacy.

The mainstream scholarly view treats Muhammad as a Soninke military commander without royal blood, whose claim to the throne rested on his religious positioning and his victory at Anfao. But the question of his precise background and any claims he may have advanced remains open. [D]

## For Fiction Writing

**Fiction Potential: HIGH**

The coup of 1493 offers exceptional material for fiction: a religious revolution that transforms an empire, compressed into a single dramatic arc from a king's death to a usurper's triumph. The conflict embodies timeless themes — ambition versus duty, faith versus tradition, the new order overthrowing the old — while being grounded in specific historical circumstances that provide rich worldbuilding material.

### Dramatic Potential

**The Religious Revolution Narrative:**

At its core, this is a story about ideological transformation. One world — the Songhai of Sunni Ali, where indigenous traditions coexisted with Islam, where warrior prowess mattered more than scholarly approval, where the king answered to no cleric — is violently replaced by another world. Muhammad Ture's Songhai would be different: explicitly Islamic, deferential to scholars, integrated into the international Muslim community. The battle at Anfao was the hinge between these two visions of what Songhai could be.

**The Usurper's Dilemma:**

Muhammad Ture faces a fundamental problem: he has no right to the throne by any traditional measure. He is not of the royal lineage. He is seizing what belongs to another. How does a usurper justify himself? How does he sleep at night? The religious framing provides justification — God's will, the duty to overthrow impiety — but does it satisfy the man himself? The tension between ambition and piety, between self-serving action and genuine belief, drives character complexity.

**The Fallen Dynasty:**

Sunni Baru is a tragic figure: inheriting an empire built by his extraordinary father, confronted immediately by demands he cannot or will not meet, defeated in a single battle, fleeing into exile to die in obscurity. He represents the old order, but he never had the chance to prove himself. Fiction can explore what the last days of a dynasty feel like — the confusion, the desperation, the moment when you realize everything your family built is ending.

**The Transformation of a Society:**

Beyond the personal drama, the coup changed how an entire civilization understood itself. The merchants of Timbuktu, the scholars in their libraries, the cavalry officers on their horses, the farmers along the Niger — all found themselves in a new political reality. How do people experience revolutionary change? How do they adapt?

### Sensory Details

**The Battlefield at Anfao:**

- **Sight:** Two cavalry armies facing each other across the dry-season savanna. Dust rising from horse hooves. Iron weapons glinting in the tropical sun. Banners — perhaps religious banners on Muhammad's side, royal standards on Sunni Baru's. The flat landscape stretching to the horizon, scattered baobabs and acacias casting sharp shadows.
- **Sound:** The thunder of cavalry charges. War drums communicating across the battlefield. The clash of iron on iron, the cries of the wounded. Horses screaming. On Muhammad's side, perhaps, religious invocations — "Allahu akbar" — marking the cause as holy. On Sunni Baru's side, the traditional Songhai battle cries his father's warriors had used.
- **Smell:** Dust and sand, horse sweat, the metallic scent of blood. Smoke if there was any burning. The earthy smell of the Sahel in dry season.
- **Touch:** The bone-jarring impact of cavalry collision. The weight of iron weapons swung until arms ached. The heat of April — dry, intense, unrelenting. Thirst as the battle extended.
- **Taste:** Dust in the mouth. The taste of fear — dry, metallic. For the wounded, the taste of blood.

**The Court at Gao:**

- **Sight:** The royal compound, mud-brick walls plastered smooth. The great mosque with its pyramidal tower. Courtiers in flowing robes, warriors in leather and quilted armor. The Niger River visible beyond, brown water moving slowly northward.
- **Sound:** Griots (praise-singers) performing for the ruler, their words weaving together genealogy, history, and flattery. The call to prayer echoing from the mosque. The bustle of the capital — merchants, petitioners, soldiers, slaves.
- **Smell:** The river — fish, mud, the green scent of water in dry country. Food cooking in the compounds — millet porridge, grilled fish, stews flavored with pepper and locust bean. Incense burned to perfume the royal presence.
- **Touch:** The textures of fine cloth — cotton from the looms of Djenne, wool brought across the Sahara. The smooth polish of leather goods. The cooling evening breeze coming off the water.

**The Moment of Confrontation:**

The scene where Sunni Baru refuses to profess Islam could be one of the most powerful in any fictional treatment:

- The formal setting: the audience hall or mosque courtyard, religious authorities present
- The demand: spoken by a scholar or delivered through an intermediary, formal and weighty with theological consequence
- Sunni Baru's refusal: the words that sealed his fate
- The reactions: shock, satisfaction, calculation — each observer responding according to their interests
- The aftermath: the scramble as factions reassess, Muhammad Ture's supporters recognizing their moment has come

### Character Opportunities

**Muhammad Ture:**

- **Before the coup:** A general serving a king he found religiously problematic. How did he navigate this? Did he ever confront Sunni Ali? Did he mask his disapproval? Or did he serve loyally, keeping his reservations private?
- **The decision:** What tipped him toward rebellion? Was there a specific moment? A conversation with a scholar who convinced him it was his duty? A calculation that the opportunity was too good to miss? Or had he been planning for years?
- **The battle:** Leading men against their former comrades. The psychological weight of civil war. The moment of victory — triumph or relief?
- **After:** The immediate challenges of ruling. The need to be more than a general. The awareness that his own claim could be challenged by the same logic he used against Sunni Baru.

**Sunni Baru:**

- **The inheritance:** Taking over from a legendary father. The impossible expectations. People comparing him unfavorably to Sunni Ali at every turn.
- **The confrontation:** Why did he refuse? Pride? Genuine religious conviction? Political miscalculation? The scene of refusal offers deep character exploration.
- **The battle:** Watching his forces waver, defect, break. The realization that he has lost everything. The decision to flee rather than die.
- **The exile:** The long journey to nowhere. What does a fallen king think about? Does he rage? Despair? Plan impossible comebacks? Find peace?

**The Timbuktu Ulama:**

Not a single character but a collective with individual voices:
- **The senior qadi:** An elderly scholar who suffered under Sunni Ali, who has been waiting for this moment, who sees Muhammad as the answer to prayers
- **The young intellectual:** A scholar who supported the coup but begins to wonder if Muhammad's Islam is genuine or merely political
- **The skeptic:** Someone who suspects the religious framing is a cover for mere ambition
- **The triumphalist:** A scholar so convinced of his side's righteousness that he cannot see any complexity

**The Soldiers:**

Men caught between loyalties:
- **The defector:** A cavalry officer who served Sunni Ali and initially served Sunni Baru, but who cannot fight against a cause he believes is righteous
- **The loyalist:** Someone who stayed with Sunni Baru to the end, not because he believed Sunni Baru was right but because loyalty was his code
- **The opportunist:** A soldier who calculates which side is likely to win and switches accordingly
- **The believer:** A warrior who fights for Muhammad because he genuinely believes this is jihad

### Scene Opportunities

1. **The death of Sunni Ali:** The news arriving at Gao. Sunni Baru learning he is now king. Muhammad Ture learning the man who made him a general is gone. The immediate political maneuvering.

2. **The demand and refusal:** The formal confrontation where the ulama demand Sunni Baru profess his faith. This scene could be staged many ways — public or private, directly or through intermediaries — each choice creating different dramatic effects.

3. **Muhammad's decision:** The moment Muhammad commits to rebellion. A secret meeting with supporters? A private prayer asking for divine guidance? A confrontation with a family member who urges caution?

4. **Raising the standard:** Muhammad gathering his forces, making his cause public. The declaration that frames the conflict as religious war. The speeches, the oaths, the commitment.

5. **The march to Anfao:** Days of movement across the Sahel. The tension of approaching battle. Conversations between Muhammad and his lieutenants. Scouts reporting on Sunni Baru's movements.

6. **The battle:** The engagement itself — cavalry charges, the tide of combat, the moment it becomes clear that Muhammad is winning. The chaos of battle rendered in sensory detail.

7. **Sunni Baru's flight:** The decision to run rather than die. Gathering what followers remain. The journey into exile. The realization that there will be no return.

8. **The new Askia:** Muhammad's first acts as ruler. Entering Gao as king. The ceremonies of accession. The beginning of a new era.

9. **Years later — the hajj:** A flash-forward to 1496-1497, Muhammad in Cairo receiving the caliph's blessing. The general who became king now recognized by the highest religious authority. The legitimation complete.

10. **The historian's pen:** A meta-scene: the scholar writing the *Tarikh al-Sudan* decades later, shaping the story we have received. How does he portray Muhammad? How does he portray Sunni Baru? What does he include, what does he leave out, and why?

### Worldview and Values

**Muhammad Ture's perspective:**

- **Islam as political order:** Religion is not private belief but the foundation of proper governance. A state should be organized according to God's law, interpreted by those trained to understand it.
- **Legitimacy requires sanction:** Power alone is not enough. A ruler needs religious approval, scholarly support, recognition from the wider Muslim community. This is why the hajj matters — it transforms force into authority.
- **The scholar as advisor:** Religious scholars are not threats but essential partners. Their knowledge guides the ruler; their approval legitimates his rule.
- **The burden of righteous action:** Overthrowing Sunni Baru was not a choice but a duty. Allowing an impious ruler to continue would have been complicity in his sin.

**Sunni Baru's perspective (reconstructed):**

- **The warrior tradition:** Kingship is about strength, about the ability to protect your people and defeat your enemies. His father built an empire through war, not through pleasing scholars.
- **Independence from clerics:** The king rules by his own authority. He does not need scholars' permission to govern. Their attempts to dictate terms are an encroachment on royal prerogative.
- **Loyalty to heritage:** His father and grandfather ruled a certain way. To repudiate that heritage at the scholars' command would be betrayal.

**The scholars' perspective:**

- **The primacy of Sharia:** God's law is supreme. A ruler who does not govern according to Islamic law is not a legitimate Muslim ruler.
- **The duty of consultation:** Rulers should seek guidance from those who know the law. Scholars have a legitimate role in governance.
- **Historical grievance:** Sunni Ali persecuted them; Sunni Baru threatened more of the same. Muhammad offers a better future.

### Themes for Exploration

- **Faith versus Tradition:** The tension between Islam (a universal religion with transcendent claims) and indigenous Songhai tradition (local, ancestral, tied to specific places and peoples)
- **Legitimacy and Power:** What makes a ruler legitimate? Birth? Victory? Religious sanction? Popular acceptance? Muhammad's career raises all these questions.
- **The Uses of Religion:** Is religious framing ever neutral? Muhammad's coup shows how religious language can serve political ends — but this does not necessarily mean it is insincere.
- **Revolutionary Transformation:** What happens to a society when its fundamental principles change? The Songhai of 1494 was organized differently than the Songhai of 1492.
- **The Writing of History:** Our only accounts come from scholars whose interests Muhammad served. Fiction can explore how the story might look different from other perspectives.

## Sources

### Primary Sources

**Tarikh al-Sudan (Abd al-Rahman al-Sadi, c. 1650):**

The most detailed narrative account of the Songhai Empire, including the coup of 1493. Written approximately 160 years after the events, it draws on oral tradition, earlier chronicles (now lost), and the institutional memory of Timbuktu's scholarly families. Al-Sadi was a scholar in the Timbuktu tradition — the very community that supported Muhammad's coup and benefited from his patronage.

- **Key content on the coup:** Sunni Ali's death, Sunni Baru's succession and religious refusal, Muhammad Ture's rebellion, the Battle of Anfao, the new Askia title
- **Translation:** John O. Hunwick, *Timbuktu and the Songhay Empire* (Brill, 1999)
- **Reliability:** [E] for the events described; [D] for characterizations (scholarly bias)

**Tarikh al-Fattash (Mahmud Kati family, c. 1519-1665):**

A family chronicle begun by Mahmud Kati, who may have been a contemporary witness to the coup, and continued by his descendants. Shares the Timbuktu perspective and the positive view of Muhammad Ture.

- **Key content:** Details of Sunni Baru's refusal to profess Islam; Muhammad's religious motivations
- **Translation:** Partial; older French translation (Houdas & Delafosse, 1913)
- **Reliability:** [E] for events; [D] for characterizations; [T] for specific quoted speech

### Secondary Sources

**Hunwick, John O., *Timbuktu and the Songhay Empire* (1999):**
The definitive English translation and analysis of the *Tarikh al-Sudan*. Hunwick's introduction and extensive notes provide the most authoritative scholarly treatment of Songhai history, including the 1493 coup. Essential for any serious research.

**Hunwick, John O., *Shari'a in Songhay* (1985):**
Provides translation and analysis of Muhammad al-Maghili's legal treatises addressed to Askia Muhammad, illuminating the Islamic governance project that the coup initiated.

**Gomez, Michael A., *African Dominion* (2018):**
The most recent major synthesis of West African imperial history. Provides fresh analysis of the coup within the broader context of Sahelian politics and religious transformation.

**Levtzion, Nehemia, *Ancient Ghana and Mali* (1973):**
Essential context for understanding the Songhai's rise and the religious dynamics of the Western Sudan.

**Cissoko, Sekene Mody, *Tombouctou et l'Empire Songhay* (1975):**
French-language study providing a Malian scholarly perspective on the coup and its consequences.

### Source Assessment

The evidence for the coup of 1493 is relatively abundant but filtered entirely through sources sympathetic to Muhammad Ture. The *Tarikh al-Sudan* and *Tarikh al-Fattash* were written by scholars connected to the Timbuktu intellectual establishment that Muhammad patronized. They had every reason to portray him positively and Sunni Baru negatively.

**What is well-established [E]:**
- The coup occurred in April 1493
- Muhammad Ture defeated Sunni Baru at the Battle of Anfao
- Sunni Baru fled into exile and the Sonni dynasty ended
- Muhammad adopted the title "Askia" and founded a new dynasty

**What is probable [P]:**
- Some kind of religious confrontation preceded the coup
- Muhammad's Islamic orientation distinguished him from the Sonni rulers
- The scholarly establishment supported Muhammad

**What is debated [D]:**
- The exact nature and extent of Sunni Baru's religious failing
- Whether Muhammad was primarily motivated by piety or ambition
- The reliability of quoted speeches and specific incidents

**What is speculative [S]:**
- Tactical details of the battle
- Force sizes and compositions
- Casualty figures
