# Major Conflicts Index
## Sub-Saharan African Kingdoms — Military Conflicts Directory (~300-1600 CE)

*This index lists the most significant military conflicts across Sub-Saharan Africa during the project period. Each entry includes dates, participants, brief significance, and fiction potential rating.*

**Fiction Potential Ratings:**
- **HIGH** — Dramatic stakes, well-documented, strong characters, rich sensory possibilities
- **MEDIUM** — Significant historically but thinner sources or less inherently dramatic narrative
- **LOW** — Important for context but limited fiction material

## Military Technology and Organization Notes

### Key Military Traditions by Region

**West African Sahel (Ghana, Mali, Songhai):**
- Cavalry-based armies (horses from trans-Saharan trade)
- Iron-tipped weapons, quilted armor
- Professional warrior classes (*ton-den* in Mande tradition)
- Asymmetric warfare: cavalry vs. infantry, desert raiders vs. settled populations
- Post-1500: introduction of firearms via Morocco

**West African Forest Zone (Benin, Yoruba):**
- Infantry-dominated warfare (tsetse fly limits horses)
- Fortified cities with elaborate wall systems
- Crossbow and poison-arrow technology
- Dense forest as defensive advantage

**East Africa (Swahili Coast):**
- Naval/maritime defense
- City walls (coral stone)
- Limited military tradition compared to hinterland peoples
- Vulnerable to Portuguese naval power and mainland raiders

**Horn of Africa (Ethiopia, Aksum, Adal):**
- Highland warfare with mountain fortresses
- Mixed cavalry and infantry
- Ethiopian heavy infantry; Aksumite war elephants (early period)
- Post-1500: Ottoman-supplied firearms for Muslim forces, Portuguese firearms for Christians

**Southern Africa (Great Zimbabwe, Mutapa, Rozvi):**
- Cattle-wealth-based military power
- Stone fortifications
- Iron weapons, limited cavalry
- Later: conflict with Portuguese musketeers

**Nubia:**
- Famous archery tradition ("eye-smiters")
- River-based defense (Nile cataracts as barriers)
- Fortified monasteries and cathedrals as defensive positions
- Cavalry and camel-mounted warriors

## Scholarly Debates on Major Conflicts

### Kirina (c. 1235)
- **Date:** Usually given as c. 1235 but ranges from 1230s-1240s in different sources
- **Location:** Debated; traditionally placed near Koulikoro (modern Mali)
- **Sumanguru's fate:** Oral tradition says he was killed by a magic arrow or disappeared; historical reality unknown
- **Historicity:** The battle's existence is [Probable] but details are [Traditional/Oral]

### Almoravid "Conquest" of Ghana (c. 1076)
- **Major debate:** Did the Almoravids actually conquer Ghana militarily? Older scholarship (based on al-Bakri) assumed yes; recent work (Masonen, Conrad) questions whether there was a conquest at all, suggesting instead a gradual political transition.
- **Confidence:** [Debated]

### Tondibi (1591)
- **Well-documented** by both Moroccan and Songhai sources
- **Army sizes:** Moroccan force of ~4,000 (including musketeers) vs. Songhai army of ~30,000-40,000. The precise numbers are [Debated] but the asymmetry is [Established].
- **Outcome:** Moroccan firearms routed Songhai cavalry. Cattle stampede tactic by Songhai failed. Decisive technological mismatch.
- **Confidence:** [Established] for the event; [Debated] for precise numbers
