# Sub-Saharan African Kingdoms Academic Research Database

**Status: COMPLETE** | 166 files | ~1.1 million words | February 2026

A comprehensive academic research database documenting the major kingdoms, empires, and city-states of Sub-Saharan Africa from approximately 300 CE to 1600 CE. This repository provides historically accurate, fiction-ready research covering over a millennium of African political, cultural, military, and economic achievement across the continent.

## Purpose and Rationale

This research hub supports commercial fiction writing by providing:
- **Character profiles** with dramatizable moments and story potential
- **Battle documentation** with tactical details and sensory information
- **Thematic research** exploring African culture, religion, trade, and society
- **Fiction resources** including story seeds, character archetypes, and period details

All research is structured with a "fiction-first" approach — every document emphasizes what matters for storytelling while maintaining academic rigor.

### Why This Project Exists: Filling a Major Lacuna

This project was identified as a **critical gap** in the broader historical research vault. Sibling projects cover Babylon, Persia, Greece and Rome, the Mongol Empire, the Crusades, China, Russia, and other civilizations — but Sub-Saharan Africa was entirely absent. This is precisely the kind of omission that perpetuates the marginalization of African history in popular culture and historical fiction.

Sub-Saharan Africa between 300 and 1600 CE contained some of the world's wealthiest individuals (Mansa Musa), largest cities (Benin City, Timbuktu, Kilwa), most sophisticated trade networks (trans-Saharan, Indian Ocean), and most dramatic political narratives — yet this period is drastically underrepresented in English-language historical fiction. This project aims to make that history accessible and usable for storytelling.

## Priority Periods for Fiction

The following periods and events have been identified as having the **highest potential** for historical fiction:

### Tier 1: Outstanding Fiction Potential

1. **Sundiata's Exile and Return (c. 1217-1235)** — The founding epic of the Mali Empire. A disabled prince overcomes exile, builds alliances, and defeats the sorcerer-king Sumanguru at the Battle of Kirina. An African "Hero's Journey" with deep oral tradition support.

2. **Mansa Musa's Hajj to Mecca (1324-1325)** — The richest man in the world crosses the Sahara with tons of gold, crashes Egypt's economy, and puts Mali on European maps. Epic travelogue, culture clash, diplomacy, and spectacle.

3. **Swahili Coast Golden Age (c. 1200-1400)** — Kilwa and other city-states as cosmopolitan Indian Ocean trading powers. Coral-stone palaces, monsoon-driven commerce, Afro-Arab-Persian cultural fusion. Maritime adventure setting.

4. **Kingdom of Kongo Meets Portugal (1483-1506)** — First sustained contact between a Central African kingdom and European power. Afonso I's remarkable attempt to modernize through Christian conversion while maintaining sovereignty. Tragic diplomatic drama.

### Tier 2: Excellent Fiction Potential

5. **Great Zimbabwe at its Height (c. 1300-1400)** — Massive stone city on the Zimbabwe Plateau, center of gold trade, cattle wealth, and Shona civilization. Mystery and grandeur; archaeological evidence rich but written records absent.

6. **Fall of Songhai (1591)** — Moroccan army with firearms crosses the Sahara and defeats the last great Sahelian empire at Tondibi. End of an era, clash of military technologies, and the destruction of Timbuktu's scholarly culture.

7. **Aksum's Conversion to Christianity (c. 330 CE)** — King Ezana's adoption of Christianity makes Aksum one of the earliest Christian states. Religious transformation, diplomatic realignment, monumental architecture.

8. **Queen Amina of Zazzau (c. 1533-1610)** — Warrior queen of a Hausa city-state who expanded territory through military campaigns and fortification-building. Draws on rich oral tradition; warrior-woman archetype with historical grounding.

## Naming Conventions

### File Naming
- **Historical Figures:** `HF-Name.md` (e.g., `HF-Mansa-Musa.md`, `HF-Sundiata-Keita.md`)
- **Battles/Campaigns:** `BC-Name-Year.md` (e.g., `BC-Kirina-1235.md`, `BC-Tondibi-1591.md`)
- **Geography:** `GE-Location.md` (e.g., `GE-Timbuktu.md`, `GE-Great-Zimbabwe.md`)

- **Events:** `EV-Name-Year.md` (e.g., `EV-Mansa-Musa-Hajj-1324.md`)

### Person Naming
- Use the most widely recognized English-language form as primary identifier
- Document all variant spellings and transliterations in the entry
- Include names in original languages where known
- Royal titles: Include the relevant title (Mansa, Mai, Oba, Negus, Sultan)

## Research Standards

### Source Hierarchy
1. **Archaeological evidence** — Physical remains, radiocarbon dating, material culture
2. **Contemporary written sources** — Arabic chronicles, Ethiopian Ge'ez texts, inscriptions
3. **Near-contemporary sources** — Written within 1-2 generations of events
4. **Oral tradition** — Professional historical recitation (griots, court historians)
5. **Later written accounts** — European chronicles, later Arabic compilations
6. **Modern scholarship** — Peer-reviewed Africanist historiography (prefer post-1960)

### Confidence Levels
- **[Established]** — Multiple independent sources; scholarly consensus
- **[Probable]** — Good evidence; majority view
- **[Debated]** — Active scholarly disagreement
- **[Speculative]** — Inference without direct evidence
- **[Traditional/Oral]** — From oral tradition; not independently confirmed

### Status Markers
- `[E]` Enumerated (listed, no document yet)
- `[S]` Stubbed (basic document exists)
- `[X]` Expanded (fully researched)
- `[V]` Verified (cross-referenced and fact-checked)

## Project Philosophy

Sub-Saharan African history offers extraordinary fiction opportunities:

1. **Untold Stories** — These are among the most underrepresented major civilizations in English-language fiction. Novelty is a creative advantage.
2. **Epic Scale** — Empires spanning millions of square kilometers, cities rivaling contemporary Europe, trade networks connecting three continents.
3. **Dramatic Characters** — Exiled princes, warrior queens, scholar-kings, world-changing diplomats, merchant adventurers.
4. **Rich Oral Tradition** — The Sundiata epic, the Dausi, and countless other oral narratives provide ready-made dramatic structures.
5. **Cultural Depth** — Sophisticated religious traditions, artistic achievement (Benin bronzes, Lalibela churches, Great Zimbabwe), intellectual life (Timbuktu manuscripts).
6. **Global Connections** — These were not isolated societies. They traded with China, India, Persia, Arabia, and Europe. Stories set here naturally become world stories.
