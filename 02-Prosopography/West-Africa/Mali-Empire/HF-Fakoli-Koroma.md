## Overview

Fakoli Koroma stands as one of the most dramatically compelling figures in Mande oral tradition — a warrior of immense power who made the fateful choice to betray his own blood and join the enemy. His story encapsulates one of the fundamental tensions in Mande political thought: the conflict between kinship obligation and justice, between loyalty to family and loyalty to what is right. In the great narrative of Sundiata's rise and Sumanguru's fall, Fakoli's defection marks the turning point — the moment when the sorcerer-king's own coalition began to crumble from within.

According to the oral tradition preserved by griots across the Mande-speaking world, Fakoli was the nephew (sister's son) of Sumanguru Kante, the powerful ruler of Sosso who had conquered and oppressed the Mande peoples. Fakoli served as one of Sumanguru's most formidable generals, commanding armies and wielding significant spiritual power in his own right. But when Sumanguru committed an unforgivable personal injury — stealing Fakoli's wife Keleya — the general faced an impossible choice. Family loyalty demanded that he swallow the insult and continue serving his uncle. Justice and personal honor demanded retribution. Fakoli chose honor, defected to Sundiata's coalition, and brought with him military strength, strategic intelligence, and the devastating symbolic message that Sumanguru's own kin had abandoned him.

The historical Fakoli, if he existed, cannot be separated from the legendary figure. No Arabic sources mention him; he appears only in oral tradition. But this does not diminish his significance. The Mande peoples chose to remember this story for eight centuries because it speaks to something profound about their values: that there are limits to kinship obligation, that a ruler who violates basic moral principles forfeits the loyalty of even his closest relatives, and that choosing the right side — even against one's own family — can be an act of courage rather than betrayal. Fakoli's story is preserved not as a cautionary tale about disloyalty but as an exemplum of moral clarity in an impossible situation.

## Early Life and Background

### Family and Lineage

The oral tradition identifies Fakoli as closely related to Sumanguru Kante, most commonly as his nephew through his sister. This matrilineal connection is significant: in many West African societies, the sister's son occupied a special position, often as potential heir or trusted subordinate. The relationship between maternal uncle and nephew (the *denke* relationship in some Mande societies) was typically one of affection and obligation, making Fakoli's eventual betrayal all the more dramatic. [T]

Fakoli belonged to the Koroma (also spelled Kourouma, Kuruma) clan, which was associated with blacksmithing — the same craft-caste connection that defined Sumanguru's own power base. The blacksmith (*numu*) in Mande society was not merely a metalworker but a figure of spiritual potency. Blacksmiths worked with fire and iron, transforming raw materials into tools and weapons. This mastery of transformation gave them access to *nyama* — spiritual power, life force — that ordinary people could not wield. The forge was a sacred space, and the blacksmith was both respected and feared. [T]

The Koroma clan's blacksmith heritage meant that Fakoli, like his uncle, came from a lineage steeped in occult power. The epic tradition attributes formidable magical abilities to both men. When Fakoli defected, he did not merely bring warriors to Sundiata's side; he brought spiritual weapons from the same arsenal Sumanguru wielded. The battle between Sundiata and Sumanguru was thus not merely a contest between two armies but between two systems of spiritual power — and Fakoli's defection shifted the balance. [T]

Some versions of the oral tradition provide additional genealogical details, naming Fakoli's mother as Kosiya Kante (Sumanguru's sister) and his father as Mansa Bele or other figures. The details vary between griots, reflecting the decentralized nature of oral transmission. What remains consistent is the fundamental structure: Fakoli was close kin to Sumanguru, close enough that his defection represented a profound rupture. [T]

### The Warrior's Rise

Before the crisis that drove him to Sundiata, Fakoli had established himself as one of the foremost warriors in Sumanguru's kingdom. The tradition emphasizes his military prowess and the forces he commanded. He was not a marginal figure who defected from desperation; he was a pillar of Sosso power whose departure shook the foundation. [T]

Fakoli's epithet "Daaba" (or similar variations) in some traditions refers to his baldness — a physical characteristic that made him visually distinctive and that the griots incorporated into their praise songs. Other traditions give him different epithets emphasizing his strength, his magical power, or his ferocity in battle. The accumulation of praise names was a mark of status in Mande society; that Fakoli has multiple names preserved across traditions indicates his importance in the cultural memory. [T]

As a general under Sumanguru, Fakoli would have participated in the campaigns that brought the Mande peoples under Sosso domination. He would have helped enforce the tribute system, suppressed resistance, and maintained the military apparatus that kept Sumanguru in power. This history makes his later choice more complex — he was not an innocent joining a righteous cause but a complicit actor choosing to change sides. The tradition does not shy away from this complexity; it presents Fakoli's defection as a transformation, not a revelation of hidden virtue. [T]

### Keleya — The Wife

The immediate cause of Fakoli's break with Sumanguru was, according to nearly all versions of the tradition, the theft of his wife Keleya (also spelled Keleya Konkon, Keliya, or similar variants). This was not a minor domestic dispute; it struck at fundamental questions of honor, property, and the proper behavior of rulers toward their kin. [T]

Keleya is described in the tradition as a woman of exceptional qualities — beautiful, accomplished in domestic arts, and beloved by Fakoli. Some versions emphasize her cooking abilities, particularly her skill at preparing the staple dish *tô* (thick millet or sorghum porridge). This detail is not mere culinary trivia; food preparation was a fundamental aspect of a wife's role, and a woman who excelled at it brought honor to her household. That Sumanguru desired Keleya despite her being his nephew's wife demonstrates the depth of his transgression against proper kinship behavior. [T]

In the logic of the narrative, Sumanguru's theft of Keleya is of a piece with his tyrannical rule. A king who oppresses his people, extracts excessive tribute, and rules through fear and sorcery is the same kind of man who would violate the most basic obligations to his own family. The personal injury to Fakoli mirrors the collective injury to the Mande peoples. When Fakoli rebels, he does so for personal reasons that align with the broader cause of liberation. [T]

Some scholars have noted that the "stolen wife" motif appears in oral traditions across West Africa and beyond, serving as a narrative catalyst for political ruptures. Whether Keleya was a historical person or a literary device cannot be determined. What matters for understanding the tradition is that Mande society found this explanation compelling — that a man might break with his own blood over such an injury was plausible and, in a sense, admirable. Fakoli did not forgive the unforgivable. [T]

## The Crisis and Defection

### Sumanguru's Transgression

The tradition presents Sumanguru's seizure of Keleya not as an impulsive act but as a manifestation of his fundamental character flaw — the abuse of power. Sumanguru already possessed many wives (the epic sometimes mentions that his harem numbered in the hundreds, a conventional marker of royal excess). He did not need Keleya; he took her because he could, because his power had grown so absolute that he no longer recognized limits. [T]

This is the moral logic of tyranny as understood in Mande political thought. A legitimate ruler exercises restraint, respects the rights of subordinates, and maintains the social bonds that hold society together. Sumanguru violated all these principles. His sorcerous power, which should have been used to protect his people, was turned toward domination. His position as maternal uncle, which should have entailed protection of his nephew's interests, was perverted into predation. The theft of Keleya was the final demonstration that Sumanguru recognized no obligations, only appetites. [T]

The tradition records Fakoli's initial response in different ways. Some versions suggest he first appealed to Sumanguru, reminding him of their kinship and requesting the return of his wife. This appeal was refused — in some versions mockingly, in others with threats. The refusal transformed a family dispute into a political rupture. Sumanguru had shown that kinship meant nothing to him; Fakoli was free to respond in kind. [T]

### The Impossible Choice

Fakoli's dilemma was genuine, not merely a rhetorical device. Mande society placed enormous weight on kinship obligations. The bond between maternal uncle and nephew was particularly significant; to break it was to tear at the fabric of social order. Fakoli's ancestors would judge him. His descendants would inherit his reputation. He was not merely making a tactical decision; he was choosing who he would be remembered as for centuries. [T]

The alternative, however, was acceptance of injustice. To remain loyal to Sumanguru after such treatment would mark Fakoli as a man without honor, a man who could be abused with impunity. It would also make him complicit in Sumanguru's broader tyranny. The tradition presents this as a genuine moral choice, not an obvious one. Fakoli weighed family against justice and chose justice. [T]

This choice resonates with Mande political philosophy more broadly. Kingship in the Mande world was not absolute; it was conditional on the ruler's fulfillment of obligations. A king who violated his covenant with his people — or with his own kinsmen — could legitimately be opposed. Fakoli's defection was not betrayal of a rightful lord but rejection of a lord who had forfeited his claim to loyalty. The tradition affirms this interpretation by making Fakoli a hero rather than a villain. [T]

### The Defection

When Fakoli decided to break with Sumanguru, he did not merely slip away. He defected with his forces — the warriors who followed him personally, who owed their allegiance to him as clan chief and general. This was a substantial military asset, but it was also a political declaration. Fakoli announced, through his actions, that Sumanguru's legitimacy was hollow, that even his own family would not stand with him. [T]

The timing of the defection varies in different tellings. Some versions place it before Sundiata's coalition had fully formed, making Fakoli an early and important recruit. Others place it closer to the decisive confrontation at Kirina, making it a turning point in a campaign already underway. The narrative logic favors the latter — the defection as the crack in Sumanguru's armor that presaged his defeat — but historical sequence cannot be established. [T]

Fakoli sought out Sundiata and offered his service. The tradition presents this encounter as a meeting of two great warriors: Sundiata, the legitimate prince returned from exile to liberate his people; Fakoli, the honorable general who had rejected an unjust kinsman. Their alliance was not merely strategic but moral — two men who had chosen the right side finding each other. [T]

## Role in Sundiata's Coalition

### Military Contribution

Fakoli brought to Sundiata's coalition military capability that the prince desperately needed. Sundiata had spent years in exile building alliances, but many of his supporters were from distant kingdoms who had their own priorities. Fakoli brought Mande warriors — men who knew the terrain, understood Sosso tactics, and carried the authority of Sumanguru's own military apparatus. [T]

The tradition emphasizes that Fakoli's forces were formidable. He commanded loyal warriors, controlled significant resources, and possessed the tactical knowledge that came from serving at the center of Sosso power. When he joined Sundiata, he did not come as a supplicant but as a partner. The coalition that faced Sumanguru at Kirina was strengthened immeasurably by his presence. [T]

More than the raw military numbers, Fakoli's defection had psychological impact. Other subordinates who had remained loyal to Sumanguru out of fear or habit now saw that defection was possible. If the king's own nephew had abandoned him, perhaps his cause was truly lost. The tradition suggests that Fakoli's example encouraged other defections, that Sumanguru's coalition began to crumble once the first break appeared. [T]

### Spiritual Power

Fakoli's contribution was not merely military; he also brought spiritual power. As a member of a blacksmith lineage and a warrior of renown, Fakoli possessed mastery of *nyama* that could be deployed against Sumanguru. The epic tradition portrays the struggle between Sundiata and Sumanguru as partly a magical contest, and Fakoli added to Sundiata's arsenal of spiritual weapons. [T]

Some versions of the tradition describe Fakoli performing his own sorcerous feats, demonstrating that he commanded powers comparable to those of his uncle. The balance of mystical force, which had favored Sumanguru, shifted when Fakoli changed sides. This was not mere superstition from the tradition's perspective; it was recognition that power in the Mande world operated on multiple levels, and that spiritual resources mattered as much as spears and horses. [T]

The connection between Fakoli and Sundiata also operated on the level of legitimacy. Sundiata's claim to rule rested on justice, prophecy, and his own spiritual capacities. Fakoli's endorsement reinforced that claim. If Sumanguru's own kinsman recognized Sundiata as the rightful leader, then Sumanguru's pretensions were exposed as mere tyranny. [T]

### Intelligence and Knowledge

Fakoli knew Sumanguru's strengths and weaknesses. He knew the disposition of Sosso forces, the state of Sumanguru's alliances, the vulnerabilities of his defensive positions. This intelligence was invaluable to Sundiata's campaign planning. The tradition does not emphasize this practical contribution as much as the dramatic and spiritual elements, but it underlies the narrative logic: Fakoli's defection changed the strategic calculus. [T]

In some versions, Fakoli provides specific knowledge about Sumanguru's magical protections. The secret of Sumanguru's vulnerability — that he could be harmed by a weapon incorporating a white cock's spur — may have been transmitted through Fakoli or through other channels (often attributed to Sundiata's sister Nana Triban, who may have obtained it from within Sumanguru's household). Whether Fakoli contributed this crucial intelligence varies by version, but his insider knowledge of Sumanguru's court would logically have included such details. [T]

### Relationship with Other Coalition Leaders

Fakoli joined a coalition that already included other major figures, notably [[HF-Tiramaghan-Traore]] (who would lead the western conquests after Kirina) and various princes and chiefs who had allied with Sundiata. The tradition presents this coalition as generally harmonious under Sundiata's leadership, but the dynamics must have been complex. Fakoli was a newcomer whose past included service to the enemy; other coalition members might have viewed him with suspicion. [T]

The epic tradition generally smooths over such tensions, presenting the alliance as unified in purpose. When tensions appear, they typically reinforce the narrative's moral lessons — Sundiata's wisdom in managing diverse allies, the shared commitment to overthrowing tyranny. Whatever actual frictions existed between Fakoli and other coalition leaders, the tradition chose not to emphasize them. [T]

## The Battle of Kirina (c. 1235)

### Fakoli's Role in the Campaign

As Sundiata's forces advanced toward the decisive confrontation with Sumanguru, Fakoli fought alongside them. His role in the campaign's preliminary stages is not detailed in most versions, but his presence would have been significant — a constant reminder to Sumanguru's remaining supporters that the coalition they faced included the king's own kin. [T]

The Battle of Kirina itself was the climax of the campaign. The exact location is debated, but it was somewhere in the Manden heartland, probably in what is now southern Mali or northern Guinea. The two armies — Sundiata's liberation coalition and Sumanguru's Sosso forces — faced each other for the battle that would determine the future of the Western Sahel. [T]

### The Battle

The tradition focuses the battle narrative on the confrontation between Sundiata and Sumanguru, portraying it as a duel between champions as much as a clash of armies. The magical dimension dominates: Sumanguru's supernatural protections, the enchanted weapon with the cock's spur, the moment of vulnerability when the sorcerer-king was struck. Fakoli's specific actions during the battle are less emphasized, but his presence on the field — fighting against his uncle, his own blood — added to the drama. [T]

Some versions of the tradition give Fakoli notable feats during the battle. His warriors would have formed an important contingent, possibly facing Sosso units whose commanders they had once served alongside. The psychological warfare was as important as the physical: Sumanguru's army knew that Fakoli fought against them, knew that the coalition included warriors who had inside knowledge of their tactics and vulnerabilities. [T]

When Sundiata struck Sumanguru with the enchanted weapon and the sorcerer-king's power shattered, Fakoli was among those who witnessed the transformation. Some versions describe Sumanguru fleeing the battlefield, pursued to the mountains where he disappeared or was transformed into stone. Others describe his power simply collapsing, his army dissolving into chaos. Fakoli had helped make this happen — had chosen the right side and seen that choice vindicated. [T]

### The Aftermath

With Sumanguru's defeat, the Sosso kingdom ceased to exist as an independent power. Sundiata's coalition swept up the remaining resistance and established control over the Mande heartland. The question of what happened to Sumanguru's former domains and supporters now had to be resolved, and Fakoli's position became complicated. [T]

He had betrayed his uncle, and however justified that betrayal might have been, it created awkwardness. Some of Sumanguru's other kinsmen and former supporters would have viewed Fakoli as a traitor. Others might have seen him as a model — a man who had recognized the inevitable and positioned himself accordingly. Fakoli's post-war situation depended on how Sundiata chose to reward and integrate him. [T]

## Post-Kirina Career and Legacy

### Place in the New Order

After Sundiata's victory, the traditions indicate that Fakoli received recognition and position in the new Mali Empire. The precise nature of his post-war status varies by version. Some traditions suggest he received territorial authority over certain regions; others indicate he remained a military commander; still others suggest he retired from active participation in affairs of state. [T]

The Kouroukan Fouga — the constitutional assembly that Sundiata convened after Kirina — would have been the occasion for establishing Fakoli's place in the new order. The assembly organized Mande society, establishing clan relationships, social categories, and political structures. Fakoli and the Koroma clan would have received their designated position in this system, their rights and obligations codified along with those of other clans. [T]

The tradition's relative silence about Fakoli's later career may reflect several factors. The epic is primarily concerned with the struggle against Sumanguru; once that story concludes, characters who were important to it may fade from the narrative. Alternatively, Fakoli may have lived a relatively quiet life after Kirina, his dramatic moment having passed. Or the details of his later life may simply not have been preserved with the same intensity as the defection narrative. [T]

### The Koroma Clan

Fakoli's legacy extended beyond his individual story to the Koroma (Kourouma) clan that claimed descent from him or association with him. The Koroma remain a significant clan among Mande peoples today, found across Mali, Guinea, and surrounding regions. Clan members maintain traditions about their connection to Fakoli and take pride in his role in the founding of Mali. [T]

The clan's association with blacksmithing — the *numu* caste — continued through the centuries. Blacksmiths in Mande society retained their special status, their spiritual power, their role as transformers of material and mediators with the spirit world. The Koroma clan's memory of Fakoli connected this ongoing social position to the heroic age of Mali's founding. [T]

Joking relationships (*sanankuya*) between clans, established or reinforced at the Kouroukan Fouga, may have connected the Koroma to other clans through bonds of ritual mockery and mutual obligation. These relationships, which require certain clans to engage in stylized insults while also providing hospitality and aid, created cross-cutting ties that helped maintain social cohesion. The Koroma's relationships with other clans would have been shaped by the events of the founding period, including Fakoli's defection. [T]

### Memory and Commemoration

Fakoli's story has been preserved and transmitted for approximately eight hundred years. Griots across the Mande-speaking world know his name, his deed, and his significance. This preservation reflects a cultural judgment: the story of the man who betrayed his uncle for justice was worth remembering, worth teaching to subsequent generations, worth singing at celebrations and ceremonies. [T]

The Fakoli narrative appears in multiple versions of the Sundiata epic, including those recorded by D.T. Niane and David Conrad. It also circulates as a more independent tradition, particularly among the Koroma clan and in regions where Fakoli's post-war authority may have been concentrated. The variations between versions are typical of oral transmission — details change, emphasis shifts, local traditions add or subtract elements — but the core narrative remains stable. [T]

In modern times, Fakoli has become a cultural symbol in Mali and the broader Mande diaspora. His name appears in popular culture, in political rhetoric, and in discussions of Mande identity. Like other figures from the epic tradition, he has been adapted to new contexts while retaining his essential meaning: the man who chose justice over blood. [T]

## Personality and Character

### The Warrior

The tradition portrays Fakoli as a formidable warrior — physically powerful, skilled in combat, fearless in battle. His epithets emphasize strength and ferocity. He was not a diplomat or a schemer but a fighter, a man whose primary mode of action was direct confrontation. This characterization makes his defection more dramatic: a warrior's honor, not political calculation, drove his choice. [T]

The baldness attributed to Fakoli in some traditions (the "Daaba" epithet) adds a visual distinctiveness. In a society where elaborate hairstyles could mark status and identity, baldness set Fakoli apart. Whether this was an actual physical characteristic or a literary/symbolic element cannot be determined, but it contributes to his memorable portrayal. [T]

### The Loyal Man Betrayed

Fakoli is not portrayed as naturally treacherous or quick to switch allegiances. Before Sumanguru's transgression, he served loyally and effectively. The tradition emphasizes that he was a *good* nephew, that he fulfilled his kinship obligations until Sumanguru made them impossible. His betrayal was reactive, not proactive — a response to injury, not an expression of ambition. [T]

This characterization serves the narrative's moral logic. If Fakoli had been portrayed as disloyal by nature, his defection to Sundiata would carry less weight. The fact that he was loyal until pushed beyond endurance makes his eventual choice a statement about the limits of loyalty rather than its absence. [T]

### The Man of Honor

Honor — *horoya* in some Mande languages — was central to Fakoli's self-understanding as the tradition presents him. A man without honor was scarcely a man at all; to accept dishonor was to forfeit one's place in society. When Sumanguru stole Keleya, he attacked Fakoli's honor. The only honorable response was resistance. [T]

The tradition validates this understanding. Fakoli is remembered as honorable precisely because he refused to accept dishonor. His defection was not betrayal but self-defense on the plane of reputation and social standing. The griots who sing his story affirm that he did the right thing — that his honor was worth more than his kinship, that some injuries cannot be forgiven. [T]

### Spiritual Power

As a member of a blacksmith lineage, Fakoli possessed spiritual capacities that the tradition takes for granted. He could wield *nyama*, work with the powers associated with fire and iron, and participate in the mystical dimensions of warfare. This was not "magic" in a fantasy sense but the normal expectation of what such a man could do. [T]

The tradition does not make Fakoli's spiritual power the center of his story — that role belongs to the Sundiata/Sumanguru confrontation. But his power underlies his importance. He was not merely a general but a man of force on multiple levels, whose defection shifted spiritual as well as military balances. [T]

### The "Stolen Wife" Motif

Scholars have noted that the narrative of political rupture caused by the theft of a woman appears across many cultures and historical periods. The Trojan War, in Greek tradition, began with the abduction of Helen. Similar motifs appear in various African oral traditions. This raises the question of whether the Keleya story reflects a historical event or a conventional explanation for Fakoli's defection.

The question may be unanswerable, but it is also somewhat beside the point. Even if the Keleya narrative is a literary device rather than a historical report, it encodes real cultural values. The Mande peoples who preserved the tradition found it believable that a man would break with his kinsman over such an injury. The story functions as cultural data regardless of its historicity. [D]

### Oral Tradition as Evidence

Fakoli exemplifies the challenges of studying figures who appear only in oral tradition. He cannot be verified through Arabic chronicles, archaeological evidence, or other independent sources. His entire existence as a historical figure depends on accepting that oral tradition preserves historical memory — a premise that this project affirms while recognizing its limitations.

The Sundiata epic has been transmitted for eight centuries by professional historians (griots) whose role was precisely to preserve accurate memory. This does not mean every detail is accurate, but it suggests that the core narratives reflect genuine historical events and persons. Fakoli's story is too integrated into the epic structure to be a late addition; he appears in early recorded versions and across multiple griot lineages. The probability that such a figure existed, and that his defection was a significant event in the war against Sumanguru, is high even if specific details cannot be verified. [T]

## For Fiction Writing

### Dramatic Potential

**Fiction Potential: EXCEPTIONAL**

Fakoli's story contains one of the most powerful dramatic conflicts available to fiction: the choice between family loyalty and justice. This is not an abstract ethical dilemma but a concrete, embodied crisis with personal stakes and world-historical consequences. A fiction writer working with Fakoli has access to:

- **The torn loyalty narrative:** Fakoli must choose between his uncle — his mother's brother, his protector, his lord — and his own honor. This is not a choice between good and evil but between two goods (or two evils) that cannot be reconciled. The dramatic tension is immense.

- **The personal injury:** The theft of Keleya is simultaneously intimate (a man's wife) and political (a king's abuse of power). It reduces vast historical forces to human scale. The audience can understand why Fakoli cannot simply accept this, even if "accepting it" would be the politically prudent course.

- **The defection scene:** The moment when Fakoli leaves Sumanguru's camp and seeks out Sundiata is a potential masterpiece of dramatic writing. The physical departure, the psychological transformation, the risks involved, the irrevocability of the choice — all these elements offer rich material.

- **The battle as vindication:** Kirina provides the climax where Fakoli's choice is validated. He fights against his uncle, watches the tyrant's power shatter, and knows that he chose correctly. The emotional payoff is enormous.

- **The aftermath:** What happens to a man who has betrayed his blood, even for the best reasons? The post-war period offers opportunities for exploring guilt, justification, integration into a new order, and the long-term consequences of impossible choices.

### The Betrayal Drama

**Before the Break:**
Fiction can explore Fakoli's life under Sumanguru before the crisis. What was it like to serve a tyrant who was also your uncle? Did Fakoli suppress doubts? Did he witness injustices he could not prevent? The pre-defection period offers opportunities for:
- Scenes showing Sumanguru's tyranny in concrete detail
- Fakoli's internal conflict as he serves a cause he may not believe in
- The relationship with Keleya before she was taken
- Moments of loyalty and affection between uncle and nephew that make the break more painful

**The Taking of Keleya:**
The moment when Sumanguru seizes Keleya can be written in multiple ways:
- Fakoli is present and witnesses the taking, forced to choose between immediate resistance (suicidal) and retreat (humiliating)
- Fakoli returns from campaign to find Keleya already gone, learning of the theft from others
- A gradual process: Sumanguru's interest, his demands, Keleya's resistance, Fakoli's attempts to intervene, final failure
- The scene from Keleya's perspective: her terror, her appeals to Fakoli, her fate in Sumanguru's harem

**The Appeal and Refusal:**
If Fakoli first appealed to Sumanguru for Keleya's return, this scene offers dramatic potential:
- Fakoli humbling himself, invoking kinship, making his case
- Sumanguru's response: mockery, threats, indifference, or perhaps genuine puzzlement that his nephew would object
- The moment when Fakoli realizes that appeal is futile and another course must be chosen
- Witnesses to the exchange who will carry the story to others

**The Decision:**
The internal process of deciding to defect can be dramatized through:
- Sleepless nights weighing options
- Consultation with trusted advisors or family members
- The voice of ancestors (literal or metaphorical) demanding that he act
- The crystallizing moment when the decision becomes final

**The Departure:**
Leaving Sumanguru's camp to join the enemy:
- Gathering loyal followers who will accompany him
- The physical act of departure: at night? Openly? With a declaration or in silence?
- Crossing the boundary between the two camps
- The first meeting with Sundiata's forces

### Sensory World

**The Forge:**
Fakoli comes from a blacksmith lineage. The forge is the center of his world's spiritual and material power:
- The heat: the bellows roaring, the charcoal glowing white, the shimmer of superheated air
- The sounds: hammer on anvil, the hiss of quenching, the rasp of filing, the low chants of the smith at work
- The smells: charcoal smoke, heated metal, the acrid tang of flux, sweat
- The light: the darkness of the forge interior contrasted with the glowing metal, sparks flying
- The transformation: raw iron becoming blade, tool, weapon — the metallurgical miracle that gives blacksmiths their spiritual authority

**The Military Camp:**
As a general, Fakoli inhabited military spaces:
- Tents or temporary structures, the layout of a war camp
- Horses picketed in lines, their smell and sound
- Warriors sharpening weapons, mending equipment, boasting of past exploits
- The evening fires where strategy is discussed and morale maintained
- The pre-battle tension: checking equipment, final prayers, the silent grimness of men facing death

**The Savanna Landscape:**
The Western Sahel provides the backdrop:
- Flat or rolling grassland, scattered trees (baobabs rising like sentinels, acacias providing precious shade)
- The seasonal rhythm: green in the rains, gold and brown in the dry season
- The sky: enormous, dominating the landscape, dramatic cloud formations in the wet season, the pale harmattan haze in the dry
- Wildlife: antelope in the distance, birds circling, the tracks of lions and leopards reminding humans they are not the only predators
- Villages and towns: mud-brick compounds, granaries raised on stilts, central meeting spaces

**The Battle:**
Kirina provides opportunity for visceral combat description:
- The dust raised by thousands of feet, hooves, and bodies
- The noise: war drums, shouted commands, the clash of weapons, the screams of wounded men and horses
- The smell: blood, sweat, fear, the bowel-loosening terror of combat
- The visual chaos: banners, weapons, bodies falling, the press of combat where one can barely see beyond arm's reach
- The moment of Sumanguru's fall: the supernatural element, whatever form it takes in a given narrative treatment

### Speech and Voice

**Fakoli's Voice:**
How might Fakoli speak? The tradition suggests:
- A warrior's directness: not flowery court speech but blunt statements
- Pride in his lineage and his deeds: the habit of naming ancestors, invoking past glories
- The vocabulary of honor: words for shame, reputation, obligation
- When angry: cold fury rather than hot rage (the controlled anger of a man planning action, not venting emotion)
- When addressing Sundiata: respect for a fellow warrior and legitimate prince, but not subservience — Fakoli came as an ally, not a supplicant

**Dialogue Opportunities:**
- Fakoli confronting Sumanguru about Keleya: "Uncle, you have taken what is mine. What kinship remains between us?"
- Fakoli addressing his followers before defection: "I go to join Sundiata. Who among you will follow a man against his own blood? Know what you choose."
- Fakoli presenting himself to Sundiata: "I am Fakoli Koroma, nephew of Sumanguru. I bring you warriors, knowledge, and a heart that will not forgive."
- Fakoli during battle, facing former comrades: "Today we learn which of us chose rightly."
- Fakoli after victory, contemplating what he has done: [Silence can be as effective as speech]

### Key Relationships

**Sumanguru Kante (Uncle/Antagonist):**
The relationship between Fakoli and Sumanguru is the dramatic core. Before the break:
- Genuine affection? Or always tinged with fear of the sorcerer-king?
- Did Sumanguru favor Fakoli among his kinsmen, making the betrayal more personal?
- Shared experiences: battles fought together, enemies defeated, the bond of warriors
- The power imbalance: Sumanguru as king and uncle, Fakoli as subordinate and nephew

After the break:
- Does Sumanguru feel betrayed, or merely annoyed?
- Is there a moment when they face each other at Kirina?
- The absence of reconciliation: Sumanguru dies (or vanishes) without any healing of the breach

**Keleya (Wife):**
The tradition tells us little about Keleya beyond her role as the object of dispute, but fiction can develop her:
- Her perspective on being taken by Sumanguru
- Her feelings about Fakoli's response (or initial non-response)
- What happens to her after Fakoli defects? Is she killed? Kept by Sumanguru? Returned after Kirina?
- The reunion (if any) with Fakoli after the war
- A relationship under strain: gratitude for being fought for, perhaps also resentment at having been unable to prevent the taking

**Sundiata Keita (New Lord):**
The alliance between Fakoli and Sundiata offers:
- Initial wariness: Sundiata might reasonably wonder if Fakoli can be trusted, given his history of switching sides
- Growing respect: Fakoli proves himself through action
- The contrast between Sundiata's legitimate rule and Sumanguru's tyranny
- Possible friendship, or at least the mutual regard of warriors who have fought together
- The post-war settlement: how Sundiata rewards and positions Fakoli

**Fakoli's Mother (Sumanguru's Sister):**
If fiction develops this character:
- Her impossible position: her brother versus her son
- Did she support Fakoli's defection? Oppose it? Accept it with grief?
- Her fate after Sosso's defeat: does she survive? How do survivors of the losing side adapt?

### Scene Opportunities

**The Theft of Keleya:**
Multiple possible treatments:
- Sumanguru arriving at Fakoli's compound with warriors, taking Keleya by force while Fakoli watches helplessly
- Fakoli returning from campaign to find his household devastated, Keleya gone
- A court scene where Sumanguru publicly claims Keleya, humiliating Fakoli before witnesses
- Intimate scene: Keleya's last night with Fakoli before she is taken, their private farewell

**The Decision to Defect:**
- Fakoli alone, wrestling with the choice
- Fakoli consulting with trusted warriors: "What would you do?"
- A ritual or spiritual experience that clarifies his path
- The moment of commitment: a gesture, a word, an action that makes the choice irrevocable

**The Night Departure:**
- Gathering followers in darkness
- Moving through Sumanguru's camp for the last time
- The border between camps: a stream to cross, a hill to climb, a symbolic threshold
- First contact with Sundiata's sentries: tense identification, gradual acceptance

**The Audience with Sundiata:**
- Fakoli presented to Sundiata's council
- The other leaders' reactions: suspicion, welcome, calculation
- Sundiata's judgment of Fakoli's character
- The formal acceptance into the coalition

**Facing Former Comrades at Kirina:**
- Recognizing enemies who were once allies
- The psychological strain of fighting against men he once commanded
- Possibly killing someone he once called friend
- The horror and the necessity

**Victory and Its Costs:**
- The aftermath of battle: walking among the dead
- Confirming Sumanguru's defeat (or disappearance)
- The emotional complexity: triumph and grief intermingled
- Reunion with Keleya (if she survives) — or learning of her fate

### Worldview and Values

**Kinship and Its Limits:**
Mande society placed enormous weight on kinship obligations, but Fakoli's story shows that these obligations were not absolute. A kinsman who violated fundamental norms forfeited his claim to loyalty. Fiction set in this world should convey:
- The default expectation of kinship solidarity
- The weight of breaking kinship bonds
- The recognition that some wrongs cannot be tolerated even from family
- The tension between individual conscience and collective obligation

**Honor as Survival:**
In this world, honor was not merely a matter of feeling good about oneself. A man without honor lost his place in society, his ability to make alliances, his standing with ancestors and descendants. Fakoli's choice was not between comfort and honor but between two kinds of survival:
- Remain loyal, keep material position, but be known as a man who could be abused with impunity
- Defect, risk everything, but preserve the honor that made life meaningful

**Power and Responsibility:**
The tradition's judgment of Sumanguru reflects a political philosophy: power must be exercised with restraint and for the benefit of those under it. A ruler who treats his position as license for predation is not a king but a tyrant. Fakoli's defection is the enactment of this judgment — the withdrawal of allegiance from one who no longer deserves it.

**Spiritual Reality:**
The world of the Sundiata epic is one where spiritual power is real and consequential. Blacksmiths wield *nyama*; sorcerers work transformations; certain objects have intrinsic power. Fiction in this world should neither dismiss these elements as superstition nor treat them as fantasy magic. They are the way the world works from the perspective of the tradition. Fakoli's spiritual power, like Sumanguru's and Sundiata's, is part of his identity as a figure of consequence.

## Sources

### Primary Sources

**Sundiata Epic (Oral Tradition):**
Fakoli appears in all major recorded versions of the Sundiata epic. His story is one of the central episodes, though the details vary between griots and regions.

- **D.T. Niane, *Sundiata: An Epic of Old Mali*** (1960; English trans. 1965): Includes Fakoli's defection as a key moment in the narrative. Niane's version, based on griot Djeli Mamadou Kouyate's telling, presents the Keleya story and emphasizes Fakoli's importance to Sundiata's victory.

- **David C. Conrad, *Sunjata: A West African Epic of the Mande Peoples*** (2004): A scholarly edition presenting multiple versions, including variant treatments of Fakoli's story. Useful for seeing how the narrative varies across different griot traditions.

- **Gordon Innes, *Sunjata: Three Mandinka Versions*** (1974): Earlier scholarly collection showing variant treatments, including differences in how Fakoli's story is told.

**Koroma Clan Traditions:**
Beyond the Sundiata epic proper, the Koroma clan maintains traditions about Fakoli as an ancestor or eponymous figure. These traditions, while less accessible to outside researchers, provide additional perspectives on Fakoli's significance. [T]

### Secondary Sources

- **Levtzion, Nehemia, *Ancient Ghana and Mali*** (1973): Standard scholarly treatment of the Sahel empires; discusses the figures of the Sundiata tradition with appropriate caution about historicity.

- **Conrad, David C., *Empires of Medieval West Africa*** (2005): Accessible overview including treatment of the Sundiata tradition and its major figures.

- **Austen, Ralph A., ed., *In Search of Sunjata*** (1999): Scholarly essays examining the Sundiata tradition from historical, literary, and performance perspectives; includes discussion of supporting characters like Fakoli.

- **Jansen, Jan, *Epopee, histoire, societe: Le cas de Soundjata*** (2001): French-language scholarly treatment of the epic tradition with attention to its social context.

### Source Assessment

Fakoli is known exclusively from oral tradition. No Arabic sources mention him, and there is no archaeological evidence that could be specifically connected to his career. His historicity cannot be established through external verification; we must evaluate the oral tradition itself.

The oral tradition is consistent about Fakoli's basic story: kinsman of Sumanguru, defected to Sundiata over a personal grievance involving a wife, fought at Kirina, received position in the new order. This consistency across versions and griot lineages suggests that the core narrative is stable and (by the standards of oral tradition) reliable.

The specific details — the name Keleya, the exact kinship relationship, the circumstances of the theft, the post-war career — vary between versions. These variations are normal in oral transmission and do not necessarily indicate that any particular detail is false, only that precision cannot be expected.

For fiction purposes, the tradition provides abundant material. The dramatic structure is clear, the character is vivid, and the moral significance is profound. Whether Fakoli was "real" in a narrow historical sense matters less than the fact that the Mande peoples have remembered him for eight centuries as a man who made a hard choice rightly.
