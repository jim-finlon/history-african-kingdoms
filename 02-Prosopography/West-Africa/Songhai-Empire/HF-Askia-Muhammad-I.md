## Overview

Askia Muhammad I stands as one of the most consequential rulers in West African history — a soldier who seized power in a religious coup, a pilgrim who walked to Mecca and returned with the title "Caliph of the Sudan," and an administrator who transformed the Songhai Empire into the most sophisticated Islamic state in sub-Saharan Africa. His reign (1493-1528) marked the height of Songhai's territorial extent, commercial prosperity, and intellectual prestige. Under his rule, Timbuktu flourished as a center of Islamic scholarship, rivaling any in the Muslim world. Yet his story ends in tragedy: deposed by his own son, stripped of power, blind and elderly, he died a forgotten man in the empire he had built. [E]

Muhammad Ture was not born to the throne. He was a general, a Soninke from the Ture clan, who had served Sunni Ali — the brilliant but religiously ambivalent conqueror who forged the Songhai Empire through military genius. When Sunni Ali died in 1492, his son Sunni Baru inherited the throne but refused to make the public declaration of Islam that the Muslim scholarly community of Timbuktu demanded. Muhammad Ture saw his opportunity. In the name of Islam, he raised a rebellion, defeated Sunni Baru at the Battle of Anfao in April 1493, and proclaimed himself ruler with the new title "Askia" — a word whose meaning remains debated but which marked a clean break from the Sunni dynasty. [E]

What followed was a reign of calculated statecraft. Muhammad understood that his legitimacy, unlike that of the natural-born Sunni dynasty, required religious sanction. His pilgrimage to Mecca (1496-1497) was as much a political act as a spiritual journey — he returned with the blessing of the Abbasid caliph in Cairo, the title "Caliph of the Sudan," and the authority to speak for Islam in West Africa. He corresponded with the North African jurist Muhammad al-Maghili, receiving detailed treatises on Islamic governance that he used to reshape Songhai's legal and administrative systems. He replaced traditional Songhai offices with Islamic-style bureaucracy, established qadi courts to administer Sharia, and patronized the scholarly community of Timbuktu that Sunni Ali had allegedly persecuted. The Askia's Songhai was an empire designed to be legible to the wider Islamic world. [E]

## Early Life and Rise to Power

### Origins and Background

Muhammad Ture's origins place him outside the royal Songhai lineage. He was a Soninke, a member of the ethnic group that had once ruled the Ghana Empire centuries earlier and remained prominent in the commercial and military networks of the Sahel. His clan name, Ture (also spelled Turé or Touré), would later become one of the most significant lineages in West African history, and modern descendants trace their ancestry to him. [E]

The *Tarikh al-Fattash* provides genealogical information, stating that Muhammad's father was Abu Bakr and that the family had connections to earlier Sahelian Muslim communities. The sources agree that he was not of the Songhai royal lineage — the Za or Sunni dynasty that had ruled Songhai before and during Sunni Ali's reign. This outsider status would shape his entire reign, forcing him to seek legitimacy through religious and institutional means rather than hereditary right. [P]

Little is known of Muhammad's early life or education. By the time he appears in the historical record, he is already a senior military commander serving Sunni Ali. The *Tarikh al-Sudan* describes him as one of Ali's generals, trusted with significant military responsibilities. His Islamic piety, whether sincere or politically calculated (the sources suggest the former), distinguished him from Sunni Ali's more ambivalent religious stance. [P]

### The Religious Crisis: Sunni Ali's Legacy

To understand Muhammad Ture's seizure of power, one must first understand the tensions within the Songhai state that Sunni Ali had created. Sunni Ali (r. 1464-1492) was unquestionably a military genius who had transformed the small Songhai state centered on Gao into a vast empire stretching across the Middle Niger. He captured Timbuktu (1468), conquered Djenne after a legendary siege (traditionally said to have lasted seven years), and broke the power of the Mossi kingdoms to the south. By the time of his death, Songhai controlled the entire arc of the Niger River and the lucrative trade routes that crossed it. [E]

But Sunni Ali's relationship with Islam was, by the standards of the Timbuktu scholarly community, dangerously inadequate. The *Tarikh al-Sudan* and *Tarikh al-Fattash* — written by scholars affiliated with Timbuktu's intellectual establishment — portray him as a Muslim in name only, a ruler who paid lip service to Islam while secretly practicing indigenous Songhai religion, who persecuted the ulama (Islamic scholars), and who was guilty of terrible cruelties against the learned men of Timbuktu. Modern historians debate how much of this portrayal reflects actual religious failing and how much represents the biased perspective of scholars whom Ali may have politically marginalized. [D]

What is clear is that Sunni Ali did not fully embrace the legal and cultural framework that the Timbuktu ulama considered essential to proper Islamic governance. He maintained traditional Songhai royal rituals, patronized indigenous religious practitioners, and — most critically — did not submit his rule to the oversight of Islamic law as interpreted by the scholarly establishment. Whether this made him a bad Muslim, a pragmatic ruler navigating a religiously diverse empire, or simply a king who refused to share power with clerics is a matter of interpretation. [D]

### Sunni Baru and the Break

When Sunni Ali died in November 1492 (the chronicles say he drowned crossing a river, possibly the Niger), his son Sunni Baru succeeded him. The critical moment came when the ulama of Timbuktu, likely through intermediaries, demanded that Sunni Baru make a clear public profession of Islam — effectively repudiating his father's religious ambiguity and committing himself to rule according to Islamic law. [E]

Sunni Baru refused. The *Tarikh al-Fattash* records that he declared: "I will not make the profession of faith, for it would mean I was abandoning the religion of my father and my grandfather." Whether this represented genuine religious conviction, defiance of clerical interference, or simply poor political judgment, it gave Muhammad Ture the opening he needed. [E]

Muhammad positioned himself as the champion of Islam against a pagan usurper. He rallied military forces loyal to him and to the Islamic cause, framing the coming conflict as a religious war rather than a mere power grab. The stage was set for a confrontation that would reshape the Songhai state. [P]

### The Battle of Anfao (April 1493)

The decisive battle took place at Anfao (also spelled Angoo or Ankoo), a location whose precise identification remains uncertain but was likely in the region northeast of Gao. Muhammad Ture led a coalition of forces — his own loyal troops, defectors from Sunni Baru's army who supported the Islamic cause, and possibly some scholarly and merchant factions who saw their interests aligned with Muhammad's religious program. [E]

The battle itself is not described in great tactical detail in the sources, but its outcome was decisive. Sunni Baru's forces were defeated, and Sunni Baru himself fled. The *Tarikh al-Sudan* reports that he retreated to the region of Ayorou and eventually died in exile, his dynasty ended. [E]

Muhammad Ture was now master of the Songhai Empire. But he faced an immediate legitimacy problem: he was a usurper, not a hereditary king. His solution would be to build a new kind of legitimacy — religious, institutional, and international.

### The Title "Askia"

Upon taking power, Muhammad adopted the title "Askia" rather than continuing the "Sunni" (or "Sonni") title of the previous dynasty. The meaning and origin of "Askia" have been debated by scholars. Several theories exist:

1. **A new title of dignity:** Muhammad may have invented or adopted a title that marked a clean break from the dynasty he had overthrown, signaling that this was a new beginning rather than a mere change of personnel. [P]

2. **Meaning "forceful one" or similar:** Some linguistic analyses suggest the word may have Songhai or Soninke roots meaning "powerful" or "one who uses force." [S]

3. **Derived from a phrase of lamentation:** A popular tradition recorded in later oral histories claims that "Askia" derived from the phrase "A si kiya" — "He is not it" or "He will not be" — allegedly shouted by Sunni Ali's daughter or widow, lamenting that the usurper would never be a true king. This etymology is almost certainly folk-etymology rather than accurate linguistic history, but it is culturally significant as a recognition that Muhammad's claim to the throne was contested. [T]

Regardless of etymology, the title "Askia" would become the defining mark of the dynasty Muhammad founded, and all his successors would bear it. The break with the past was complete. [E]

## The Hajj (1496-1497) and Islamic Legitimation

### Motivations and Preparations

Only three years after seizing power, Muhammad Ture undertook the pilgrimage to Mecca — one of the Five Pillars of Islam and an obligation for every Muslim with the means to perform it. But for Muhammad, the hajj was far more than a personal religious duty. It was a calculated act of legitimation designed to secure his position both at home and across the Islamic world. [E]

The precedent of Mansa Musa's spectacular 1324 hajj loomed large. Musa had used his pilgrimage to announce Mali's wealth and piety to the wider world, returning with religious prestige and international recognition. Muhammad Ture sought similar benefits, though his approach was less focused on ostentatious display of wealth and more on securing formal religious and political sanction. [P]

The preparations were substantial. The empire's administration was organized to function during the Askia's extended absence — a journey that would take approximately eighteen months. A regent (the sources suggest his brother Umar) was left in charge. Resources were gathered for the journey: gold, trade goods, supplies for the desert crossing, and gifts for the holy cities and the rulers Muhammad would encounter along the way. [P]

### The Journey

Muhammad Ture's caravan departed from Gao in 1496, following the trans-Saharan routes northeast through the Sahara toward Egypt and ultimately Arabia. The journey was long, arduous, and dangerous — the same brutal desert crossing that had challenged Mansa Musa's much larger caravan nearly two centuries earlier. [E]

The caravan's size was substantial but apparently not on the legendary scale of Mansa Musa's. The *Tarikh al-Fattash* provides the figure of 1,500 soldiers accompanying the Askia, along with merchants, scholars, and servants. Muhammad also brought 300,000 gold pieces (mithqals) — an enormous sum that demonstrated Songhai's wealth while serving the practical purposes of financing the journey, making charitable donations (zakat), and distributing gifts. [P]

The route took the caravan through the central Saharan oases to Egypt, where Muhammad would have his most politically significant encounter.

### The Meeting with the Abbasid Caliph

In Cairo, Muhammad Ture sought and obtained an audience with the Abbasid caliph — not the original Baghdad-based caliphate (which had been destroyed by the Mongols in 1258), but the shadow caliphate maintained by the Mamluk sultans of Egypt. The Abbasid caliphs in Cairo held no real political power, but they retained enormous symbolic authority as the nominal spiritual leaders of Sunni Islam. [E]

The meeting was a triumph for Muhammad's legitimation project. The caliph formally invested him with authority over the Sudan (the Sahel region of West Africa), granting him the title "Caliph of Takrur" or "Caliph of the Sudan" — effectively recognizing him as the representative of Islamic authority in his region. This was precisely the religious sanction that Muhammad's usurper status required. [E]

The *Tarikh al-Fattash* records details of the encounter:

> "When Muhammad Ture arrived in Egypt, he sought audience with the Commander of the Faithful [the Abbasid caliph]. The caliph received him with great honor and invested him with the turban and the sword of authority, naming him Caliph of the Sudan and charging him with the duty of upholding Islam and Islamic law in his lands." [E]

Whether this investiture carried real weight in Islamic legal theory (the Abbasid caliphs' authority was itself contested) mattered less than its practical effect: Muhammad could now claim that his authority derived not merely from military victory but from the highest religious sanction available in Sunni Islam. [P]

### Mecca and the Holy Cities

From Egypt, Muhammad continued to the Hijaz — the Arabian region containing the holy cities of Mecca and Medina. He performed the hajj rituals at Mecca, fulfilling the religious obligation that every Muslim with means is required to perform at least once in their lifetime. [E]

The sources describe Muhammad's conduct in the holy cities as pious and generous. He distributed substantial charitable donations, purchased property in Mecca (reportedly establishing a hostel for West African pilgrims), and engaged with scholars in theological discussions. [P]

The hajj was not merely ritual but also an occasion for political and commercial networking. Muhammad encountered scholars, merchants, and pilgrims from across the Islamic world, establishing connections that would benefit Songhai's position in international trade and intellectual exchange. The Askia's questions about Islamic governance, which he would later pose in writing to the North African jurist al-Maghili, may have been partly formulated during conversations with scholars encountered on this journey. [P]

### Return and Impact

Muhammad Ture returned to Songhai in 1497, his position immeasurably strengthened. He was no longer merely a victorious general who had overthrown the legitimate dynasty; he was now a hajji (one who has completed the pilgrimage), a recipient of caliphal investiture, and the acknowledged Islamic leader of the Sudan. [E]

The hajj had accomplished what military victory alone could not: it had transformed a usurper into a legitimate Islamic sovereign. For the remainder of his reign, Muhammad would build on this religious foundation, reshaping Songhai's institutions to align with Islamic legal and administrative norms. [P]

## Administrative Reforms

### The Provincial System

Upon his return from hajj, Muhammad Ture embarked on a systematic reorganization of Songhai's administration. The empire he had inherited from Sunni Ali was vast but loosely organized, held together primarily by military force and the personal authority of the ruler. Muhammad sought to create more durable institutional structures that would outlast any individual and align with Islamic principles of governance. [E]

The territorial organization of the empire was formalized through a provincial system. The *Tarikh al-Sudan* describes a hierarchy of governors appointed by the Askia to administer the empire's various regions:

- **Kurmina-fari:** The governor of the western provinces, including the region around Timbuktu
- **Dendi-fari:** The governor of the Dendi region in the southeast, the Songhai heartland
- **Balama:** The military commander of the eastern frontiers
- **Hi-koy:** The "river master," responsible for the Niger River fleet and navigation
- **Fari-mondyo:** Various provincial governors with specific territorial responsibilities [E]

These officials were appointed by the Askia and served at his pleasure, creating a bureaucratic hierarchy answerable to the central government rather than a system of hereditary nobles with independent power bases. While not all of these offices were necessarily Muhammad's inventions — some may have existed in earlier forms — he systematized and formalized them into a coherent administrative structure. [P]

### Islamic Courts and Legal Reform

Central to Muhammad's reform program was the establishment of Islamic legal authority throughout the empire. He appointed qadis (Islamic judges) to administer Sharia law in major cities and established a more formal relationship between the scholarly establishment and the state. [E]

The chief qadi of Timbuktu — typically drawn from the scholarly families that dominated the city's intellectual life — gained substantial authority to adjudicate cases according to Islamic law. The Maliki school of jurisprudence, already dominant in the western Islamic world, became the official legal standard. Muhammad sought guidance on the proper implementation of Islamic law through his famous correspondence with the jurist al-Maghili. [E]

This legal reform represented a significant shift from the Sunni dynasty's practice. Under Sunni Ali, traditional Songhai customary law had coexisted alongside Islamic law, with the ruler maintaining considerable discretion. Under Muhammad, Islamic law was to be the explicit foundation of the state's legal system — at least in principle and in the major urban centers. [P]

### Bureaucracy and Offices

The Askia's administration employed a more elaborate bureaucracy than previous Songhai rulers. Specialized offices handled different functions of government:

- **Treasury officials** (often drawn from scholarly families) managed the empire's finances
- **Market inspectors** (muhtasib) supervised commercial activity and enforced standards
- **Scribes** (often trained in Arabic and Islamic law) documented the administration's decisions
- **Tax collectors** gathered revenues from trade and agriculture [P]

The sources suggest that literacy and Islamic education became increasingly important for advancement in the Askia's administration, though this should not be overstated — military prowess and traditional markers of status remained significant. The tension between meritocratic Islamic ideals and the practical realities of kinship and patronage networks was never fully resolved. [P]

### The al-Maghili Correspondence

One of the most significant sources for understanding Muhammad's vision of Islamic governance is his correspondence with Muhammad ibn Abd al-Karim al-Maghili (d. 1505), a prominent jurist from Tlemcen (in modern Algeria). Al-Maghili had traveled through the Sahel, advising various rulers on the implementation of Islamic law, and Muhammad Ture sought his counsel on fundamental questions of governance. [E]

The questions Muhammad posed to al-Maghili, and al-Maghili's responses (collected in treatises such as *Taj al-Din* and *Answers to the Questions*), cover topics including:

- **The obligation to establish proper Islamic governance:** What duties does a Muslim ruler bear?
- **The treatment of those who mix Islam with non-Islamic practices:** How should syncretic religious practices be handled?
- **The status of indigenous religious practitioners:** What policy toward non-Muslims and religious syncretists?
- **The enforcement of Sharia:** How to establish proper Islamic courts and legal procedure?
- **The conduct of jihad:** When is religious war justified, and against whom? [E]

Al-Maghili's responses were rigorous and sometimes uncompromising. He urged Muhammad to enforce Islamic law strictly, to suppress syncretic practices, and to distinguish sharply between true Muslims and those whose Islam was merely nominal. Some of al-Maghili's positions — particularly regarding the treatment of religious minorities — reflected his own controversial history; he had incited violence against the Jewish community in Tuat (Saharan Algeria) before his Sahelian travels. [E]

How fully Muhammad implemented al-Maghili's more extreme recommendations is uncertain. The correspondence represents ideals that the Askia aspired to; the realities of governing a religiously and ethnically diverse empire likely required more flexibility than the jurist's treatises envisioned. [P]

## Military Campaigns and Empire at its Height

### Strategic Vision

While Muhammad Ture is remembered primarily as an administrator and religious reformer, he was also a successful military commander who expanded Songhai's territorial extent to its greatest boundaries. The empire he led at its height stretched from the Atlantic approaches (the Senegal River valley) to the Hausa city-states of northern Nigeria, and from the Saharan salt mines to the fringes of the forest zone. [E]

Muhammad's military campaigns served multiple purposes: they extended Songhai's commercial reach, brought additional populations under its tribute system, and — from the perspective of the Askia's Islamic legitimation — spread the domain of Islam. The *Tarikh al-Sudan* and *Tarikh al-Fattash* record numerous campaigns, though the details of tactics and battles are often sparse. [E]

### Campaigns to the West

Muhammad conducted military operations in the western Sahel, securing Songhai's control over the regions that had once been the heartland of the Mali Empire. By the late 15th century, Mali was a much-diminished power, and Songhai absorbed portions of its former territory. [E]

Campaigns in the Senegal River valley and the approaches to the Atlantic extended Songhai's influence to its westernmost extent. The Jolof (Wolof) and other states of the Senegambia region maintained their independence but acknowledged Songhai's power. Control over these regions secured Songhai's access to Atlantic trade goods and additional sources of gold. [P]

### The Conquest of Hausaland

The most significant expansion under Muhammad's reign was the conquest of the Hausa city-states to the east. The Hausa cities — Kano, Katsina, Zaria, and others — were prosperous commercial centers that controlled the eastern termini of the trans-Saharan trade routes connecting the Sahel to Tripoli and the Mediterranean. [E]

Beginning around 1512-1515, Muhammad launched a series of campaigns against the Hausa states. The *Tarikh al-Sudan* describes the conquest of Katsina and the imposition of Songhai suzerainty over the region. While the Hausa cities retained their local rulers and internal administration, they became tributaries of Songhai, and Songhai officials oversaw their affairs. [E]

This eastern expansion had significant economic implications. By controlling both the western and eastern ends of the Sahelian trade network, Songhai dominated trans-Saharan commerce to an unprecedented degree. Gold, salt, textiles, and enslaved people flowed through an empire that stretched nearly 2,000 miles from west to east. [P]

### The Air Expedition

Muhammad also conducted campaigns in the Saharan north, including expeditions against the Tuareg confederations that controlled the desert routes and the salt-producing regions of Taghaza and Air. Securing these routes was essential for Songhai's commercial prosperity. [E]

The Air region (in modern Niger) came under Songhai influence, and the sultanate of Agadez acknowledged Songhai suzerainty. Control over Air secured access to trans-Saharan routes leading to the Fezzan and ultimately to North Africa. [P]

### Limits of Expansion

Muhammad's campaigns were not uniformly successful. Attempts to extend Songhai power into the forest zone to the south met with limited success — the ecological and military challenges of forest warfare differed fundamentally from the savanna and Sahel warfare at which Songhai excelled. The Mossi kingdoms, though raided repeatedly, maintained their independence. [P]

The empire's vast extent also created administrative challenges. Controlling distant provinces required constant military vigilance, and rebellions in outlying regions were recurrent problems. The very success of Muhammad's expansion strained the empire's ability to maintain coherent governance. [P]

## Patronage of Timbuktu Scholarship

### The Scholarly Community

If military conquest built Songhai's territorial extent, intellectual patronage secured its cultural prestige. Under Muhammad Ture's rule, Timbuktu reached its zenith as a center of Islamic learning, rivaling established scholarly centers in the Middle East and North Africa. The city's mosques — Djinguereber, Sankore, and Sidi Yahia — functioned as universities where scholars taught theology, law, grammar, rhetoric, logic, and other sciences. [E]

The scholarly community of Timbuktu was composed of several interconnected elements:

- **Scholarly families:** Certain lineages, such as the Aqit family and the ancestors of Ahmad Baba, held hereditary positions of intellectual leadership, passing knowledge and library collections from generation to generation
- **Traders and scholars:** The line between merchant and scholar was often blurred, with wealthy trading families patronizing scholarship and scholars themselves engaging in commerce
- **Students:** Young men came from across West Africa and beyond to study with Timbuktu's masters
- **Sufi orders:** Mystical brotherhoods provided another dimension of religious life [E]

### Muhammad's Relationship with the Ulama

Muhammad Ture's relationship with Timbuktu's scholarly establishment was far warmer than Sunni Ali's had been. Where Ali had allegedly persecuted the ulama and marginalized their influence, Muhammad cultivated them as essential allies and sources of legitimacy. [E]

The *Tarikh al-Sudan* and *Tarikh al-Fattash* — both written by scholars connected to this community — portray Muhammad in glowing terms as a pious ruler who sought scholarly guidance, endowed mosques and schools, and protected the intellectual freedom of the learned. This portrayal should be read critically (these scholars had obvious reasons to praise a ruler who favored them), but the underlying pattern of alliance between the Askia and the ulama is well established. [P]

Muhammad's patronage took multiple forms:

- **Financial support:** Endowments (waqf) for mosques, schools, and scholars' salaries
- **Legal authority:** Appointment of qadis and recognition of scholarly expertise in legal matters
- **Protection:** Defense of scholarly privileges and immunities
- **Personal engagement:** The Askia reportedly consulted scholars on matters of governance and attended their lectures [P]

### Contrast with Sunni Ali

The contrast between Muhammad's treatment of the scholarly community and Sunni Ali's is a recurring theme in the Timbuktu chronicles. The *Tarikh al-Sudan* accuses Sunni Ali of persecuting the ulama, executing scholars, and showing contempt for Islamic learning. Whether this portrayal is accurate or represents the partisan perspective of scholars whom Ali marginalized for political reasons is debated by modern historians. [D]

Regardless of historical accuracy, the contrast served Muhammad's legitimation purposes perfectly. By positioning himself as the defender of scholarship against a tyrannical predecessor, Muhammad aligned his seizure of power with the interests of the influential scholarly class. His reign became, in the scholarly memory preserved in the chronicles, a golden age of Islamic learning after a period of oppression. [P]

### The Manuscript Tradition

Under Muhammad's patronage and that of his successors, Timbuktu's manuscript production flourished. Scholars copied, studied, and composed works on every branch of Islamic learning. Libraries — both institutional and private — accumulated tens of thousands of manuscripts. [E]

This manuscript tradition would survive the fall of the Songhai Empire and continue for centuries. Today, hundreds of thousands of manuscripts from this tradition survive in Timbuktu and in family collections scattered across Mali and neighboring countries. They represent one of Africa's most important intellectual heritages — and they owe their existence, in part, to the patronage and protection that rulers like Askia Muhammad provided to the scholarly community. [E]

## Fall from Power

### The Problem of Succession

Muhammad Ture ruled for thirty-five years, and for most of that time his position seemed unassailable. He had built his legitimacy on multiple foundations: military victory, religious sanction, institutional reform, and the patronage of the scholarly establishment. But as he aged, the question of succession became increasingly fraught. [E]

Muhammad had many sons — the sources list varying numbers, but at least ten are named in the chronicles. In a polygynous royal house without clear primogeniture rules, this meant intense competition among potential heirs. Each son had his own mother, maternal kin, and faction of supporters. As the Askia's physical and mental faculties declined, these factions maneuvered for position. [E]

### Blindness and Decline

By the 1520s, Muhammad was elderly (by the standards of the era) and, according to the chronicles, had lost his sight. Blindness effectively incapacitated him as a ruler — he could no longer inspect troops, receive ambassadors with proper ceremony, or oversee the administration as he once had. Real power increasingly passed to those who controlled access to the aging ruler. [E]

The *Tarikh al-Sudan* describes the Askia's final years in poignant terms:

> "His sight failed him, and he became blind, and old age overcame him, and he was weakened in body and in authority. His sons saw his weakness and contended among themselves for power." [E]

### The Coup of Askia Musa (1528)

In 1528, Muhammad's son Musa organized a coup against his father. The details vary between sources, but the essential facts are clear: Musa gathered military support, confronted his father, and forced him to abdicate. Muhammad was deposed, stripped of power, and exiled to an island in the Niger River — probably near Gao, where he could be kept under surveillance but away from any remaining supporters. [E]

The manner of Muhammad's deposition was cruel. A man who had ruled one of Africa's greatest empires for thirty-five years, who had walked to Mecca and received the title of Caliph, was now a prisoner of his own son, blind and powerless. The *Tarikh al-Fattash* records that he was treated harshly, denied proper provisions, and left in humiliating circumstances. [E]

### The Fratricide That Followed

Muhammad's fall unleashed a period of instability and violence within the Askia dynasty. Musa's reign was brief; he was in turn overthrown and killed by Muhammad's other sons. The succession thereafter was marked by coups, assassinations, and fratricidal conflict:

- **Askia Musa** (r. 1528-1531) — deposed and killed
- **Askia Muhammad II Bunkan** (r. 1531-1537) — another of Muhammad's sons
- **Askia Ismail** (r. 1537-1539) — yet another son
- And subsequent successors, with the violence continuing for decades [E]

The stability that Muhammad had built through institutional reform and religious legitimacy could not survive the succession crisis that his many sons and the absence of clear succession rules guaranteed.

## Death and Legacy

### Final Years and Death (1538)

Muhammad Ture survived his deposition by a decade, living out his final years in exile on his Niger River island. The chronicles suggest that his later years were marked by poverty, neglect, and humiliation — a stark contrast to the splendor of his reign. [E]

He died in 1538, approximately forty-five years after seizing power at the Battle of Anfao. The *Tarikh al-Sudan* records his death simply, without the elaborate mourning that might be expected for so significant a ruler. By 1538, he was a figure from a previous era, his achievements overshadowed by the chaos of the succession wars that followed his deposition. [E]

### Assessment of His Reign

Despite the tragic ending, Muhammad Ture's reign is assessed positively by both the contemporary chronicles and modern historians. His achievements were substantial:

**Administrative innovation:** He created (or formalized) institutional structures that allowed the vast Songhai Empire to function as more than a mere tributary network. His provincial system, appointment of governors, and establishment of Islamic courts represented genuine state-building. [E]

**Religious legitimation:** He demonstrated how an African ruler could use the institutions and networks of the wider Islamic world to strengthen his domestic position. His hajj, his correspondence with al-Maghili, and his patronage of scholarship all contributed to an Islamic legitimation that transcended military power. [E]

**Territorial expansion:** The empire reached its greatest extent under his rule, controlling a vast arc from the Atlantic approaches to the Hausa states. [E]

**Intellectual patronage:** His support for Timbuktu's scholarly community laid the foundations for the city's continued intellectual prominence for centuries. [E]

**The tragic irony:** A ruler who had built his career on deposing an illegitimate predecessor was himself deposed by his own son. The very ambition and capability that produced so many potential heirs also produced the succession crisis that destroyed the stability he had created. [P]

### Long-term Legacy

Muhammad Ture is remembered today as one of West Africa's greatest rulers. In Mali, the name "Ture" (or "Touré") carries associations with his legacy — the first president of independent Mali, Modibo Keita, and other prominent figures have claimed descent from or connection to the Askia's lineage. [E]

The Songhai Empire he shaped survived for another sixty years after his death, though increasingly weakened by succession conflicts. It would eventually fall not to internal collapse but to external conquest — the Moroccan invasion of 1591, which destroyed Songhai's military power at the Battle of Tondibi and ended the great age of the Sahelian empires. [E]

| **Sunni Ali's portrayal** | Ali was a persecutor of Islam, as chronicles describe | Chronicles biased; Ali was pragmatic, not anti-Islamic | [D] Chronicle bias likely exaggerates, but real tensions existed |
| **Meaning of "Askia"** | New title marking dynastic break | Derived from lamentation phrase | [D] Etymology uncertain; function as new dynastic title is clear |
| **Extent of reforms** | Transformed Songhai's administration fundamentally | Built on existing structures more than innovated | [D] Both elements likely present |
| **Al-Maghili's influence** | Muhammad implemented al-Maghili's recommendations strictly | Recommendations were ideals, not fully implemented | [P] Ideals influenced policy but pragmatism modified implementation |

### The "Sunni Ali Problem"

Modern historians have increasingly questioned the chronicles' extremely negative portrayal of Sunni Ali. Some scholars suggest that Ali's "persecution" of the ulama may have been political marginalization of a potential rival power center rather than religious oppression, and that Muhammad Ture's partisans (who included the scholars who later wrote the chronicles) had obvious reasons to demonize the dynasty Muhammad had overthrown. [D]

This revisionism does not necessarily exonerate Sunni Ali or diminish Muhammad's achievements, but it complicates the simple narrative of "Islamic reformer overthrows pagan tyrant" that the chronicles present. The truth was likely more nuanced: both rulers were Muslims, but they had different visions of the relationship between royal power and religious authority. [D]

### The Limits of Islamic Reform

How thoroughly did Muhammad's reforms penetrate Songhai society? The major urban centers — Timbuktu, Gao, Djenne — were certainly affected, with Islamic courts, scholarly institutions, and formal religious practice. But the vast majority of Songhai's population lived in rural areas, maintained traditional religious practices alongside Islam, and had limited direct contact with the formal institutions of the Islamic state. [P]

Muhammad's Songhai was not a theocracy in the strict sense; it was a military empire whose ruler used Islamic legitimation and institutions as tools of governance, but which necessarily accommodated the religious diversity of its population. The "Islamization" of Songhai was a process, not an event, and it remained incomplete by the end of the Askia dynasty. [P]

## For Fiction Writing

**Fiction Potential: HIGH** — Askia Muhammad I's life contains all the elements of epic drama: a soldier's rise to power through religious revolution, a triumphant pilgrimage that legitimizes an usurper as caliph, the building of an empire at its height, and a tragic fall — blind, deposed, and forgotten by his own son. His story is a meditation on power, legitimacy, faith, and the violence that ambition breeds.

### Dramatic Arc

The trajectory of Muhammad Ture's life provides a natural three-act structure:

**Act I — Rise (1493-1497):**
The coup against Sunni Baru, framed as religious revolution. The usurper's problem: how does a general with no royal blood make himself a legitimate king? The solution: walk to Mecca, receive the caliph's blessing, return as God's appointed ruler of the Sudan.

**Act II — Reign (1497-1528):**
Empire-building at its height. Military campaigns extending Songhai's power to its greatest extent. The building of Timbuktu as an intellectual center. Administrative reform, legal innovation, the machinery of an Islamic state. But also: the accumulation of sons, each with his own ambitions, each a potential rival to his brothers.

**Act III — Fall (1528-1538):**
Blindness and decline. The old ruler's grip on power slipping. The coup organized by his own son. Exile on an island in the river he once ruled. Ten years of poverty and humiliation before death.

The irony that structures the whole: a man who seized power by overthrowing a dynasty is himself overthrown by his own blood.

### Personality and Character

The sources allow a tentative reconstruction of Muhammad's personality:

**Ambition:** He was not content to be a general serving others. He saw an opportunity in Sunni Baru's religious intransigence and seized it. The hajj was not mere piety — it was a calculated move to transform himself from usurper to caliph.

**Piety (or its performance):** Whether his religiosity was sincere or political is ultimately unknowable. But he enacted it consistently: the hajj, the correspondence with al-Maghili, the patronage of scholars, the establishment of Islamic courts. If it was performance, it was a lifetime's commitment to the role.

**Political intelligence:** He understood the sources of legitimacy in his world. Military force alone could not secure his position; he needed religious sanction, institutional structures, and the support of the scholarly class. His reign was a masterclass in building durable power.

**Paternal complexity:** He fathered many sons, creating the very competition that would destroy him. Did he fail to resolve the succession? Did he love his sons too much to choose among them? Did he simply assume his strength would last forever?

**In old age:** The blind, weakened ruler is a powerful image. What does a man who built an empire think about when he can no longer see? How does he experience his son's betrayal? What memories sustain him on his island exile?

### Sensory World

**The Niger River:**
- Brown water flowing between sandy banks, dotted with islands
- Hippos surfacing with explosive snorts; crocodiles sliding from mud banks
- Long boats (pirogues) carrying merchants, soldiers, tax collectors
- The smell of fish drying in the sun, of river mud, of woodsmoke from riverside villages
- At Gao, the river curves past the city, the royal court, the great mosque with its pyramidal tower

**Timbuktu:**
- Mud-brick buildings with organic curves, studded with wooden beams
- Narrow streets between high walls, creating shade and funneling breezes
- The great mosques: Djinguereber, Sankore, Sidi Yahia — architectural mountains of dried mud
- The markets: salt blocks from Taghaza, gold dust weighed in small scales, textiles from North Africa, leather goods, enslaved people

- The drums and horns that announce the ruler's movements

### Speech and Voice

Muhammad was literate in Arabic and corresponded with scholars in formal written Arabic. His questions to al-Maghili reveal a man who thought seriously about the nature of Islamic governance.

**In formal settings:** He might speak through an interpreter even when he understood the language (as Mansa Musa did), maintaining royal distance and giving himself time to consider responses.

**With scholars:** He reportedly treated them with great respect, seated them in positions of honor, sought their advice. His speech to ulama would be deferential toward their learning while maintaining his sovereign authority.

**With generals and governors:** The manner of a commander, direct and authoritative. He had been a soldier before he was a king.

**With family:** Unknowable, but the tragedy of his end suggests relationships that deteriorated. What did he say to Musa when his son came to depose him?

### Key Relationships

**Sunni Baru:** The man he overthrew. In fiction, their relationship before the break — two powerful men serving the same master, knowing they would eventually clash — offers dramatic potential.

**Al-Maghili:** The jurist whose correspondence shaped Muhammad's vision of Islamic governance. Their exchange (conducted in writing) was an intellectual partnership across distance. Did they ever meet? The sources are unclear.

**The Abbasid Caliph:** The shadow caliph who invested Muhammad with authority. Their meeting in Cairo was brief but transformative. What did they say to each other? What did each think of the other?

**The scholars of Timbuktu:** A collective relationship — Muhammad as patron, they as legitimizers. Individual scholars (ancestors of later luminaries like Ahmad Baba) might be developed as characters.

**His sons:** The tragedy of the ending. Musa organized the coup, but the other sons were also players in the brutal succession conflicts. What were these relationships like during Muhammad's reign? When did the sons begin to see their father as an obstacle?

**His wives:** The sources are largely silent, but a ruler with many sons had many mothers for those sons. The politics of the royal harem, the alliances and rivalries between different wives and their children, were surely significant.

### Scene Opportunities

1. **The decision to rebel:** Muhammad receiving word that Sunni Baru has refused to make the profession of faith. The moment of decision: will he act? What justifies rebellion against a king?

2. **The Battle of Anfao:** Combat, the uncertainty of the outcome, the moment when victory becomes clear. The aftermath: what does one do with a defeated king?

3. **The Cairo audience:** Muhammad and the Mamluk sultan, the Abbasid caliph, the performance of ceremony and the negotiation of status. Walking out with the title "Caliph of the Sudan."

4. **Circumambulating the Kaaba:** The spiritual center of the hajj. Muhammad as pilgrim, equal to every other pilgrim, before God. What does he pray for? What does he feel?

5. **Receiving al-Maghili's treatise:** Reading the scholar's responses to his questions. Some recommendations easy, others troubling. How literally to implement the jurist's vision?

6. **Watching Timbuktu flourish:** A quiet scene of satisfaction — the Askia visiting a mosque-school, observing students, conversing with scholars. The empire at its height.

7. **The first signs of blindness:** The ruler noticing his sight dimming. The realization of what this means. The concealment, the adaptation, the gradual acknowledgment.

8. **The coup:** Musa's soldiers entering the palace. The confrontation between father and son. The words spoken (or not spoken) at that moment.

9. **Exile:** The blind old man on his island, hearing the river, remembering. Visitors who bring news of the fratricide among his sons. Ten years of this.

10. **Death:** However it came — quietly, in his sleep, or in full consciousness — the end of a man who had walked to Mecca and ruled half of West Africa.

### Worldview and Values

Muhammad's worldview, as reconstructed from his actions and the writings he commissioned:

**Islam as political order:** Religion was not merely personal belief but the foundation of proper governance. An Islamic state meant Islamic law, Islamic courts, Islamic legitimacy.

**Legitimacy matters:** He could not be merely a successful warlord. He needed religious sanction, institutional authority, the recognition of the scholarly community. Power without legitimacy was unstable.

**Scholarship has value:** His patronage of learning was not mere political calculation; the sources suggest genuine respect for scholars and their knowledge.

**The universal community of Muslims:** The hajj placed Muhammad in a worldwide community, connected to Cairo, Mecca, North Africa, the entire Islamic world. He thought in these terms, not merely local ones.

**The burdens of rule:** Al-Maghili's treatises emphasize the moral responsibilities of the ruler — to enforce justice, to maintain proper religion, to protect the weak. Muhammad sought this guidance; presumably he took it seriously.

## Sources

### Primary Sources

**Al-Sa'di, Abd al-Rahman**
- *Tarikh al-Sudan* (*Chronicle of the Sudan*), completed c. 1656
- Written by a Timbuktu scholar approximately a century after Muhammad's death
- The fullest narrative account of the Askia dynasty and Songhai history
- **Key content:** Muhammad's reign, administrative structure, succession conflicts, fall
- **Translation:** John O. Hunwick, *Timbuktu and the Songhay Empire* (Brill, 1999)
- **Reliability: HIGH for the era** — Based on earlier sources and oral tradition; written by a scholarly insider [E]

**Kati, Mahmud (and successors)**
- *Tarikh al-Fattash* (*Chronicle of the Seeker after Knowledge*), begun c. 1519, completed later
- Mahmud Kati was a contemporary of Muhammad Ture and a witness to his reign
- Family chronicle continued by his descendants
- **Key content:** Muhammad's hajj, relationship with scholars, family history
- **Translation:** Partial in various works; cf. O. Houdas & M. Delafosse, *Tarikh el-Fettach* (1913, French)
- **Reliability: HIGH for eyewitness material** — Kati knew Muhammad personally [E]

**Al-Maghili, Muhammad ibn Abd al-Karim**
- *Taj al-Din* (*Crown of Religion*) and *Answers to the Questions of Askia al-hajj Muhammad*
- Written c. 1498-1502
- Direct correspondence with Muhammad Ture on Islamic governance
- **Key content:** Islamic legal theory, governance, treatment of non-Muslims
- **Translation:** John O. Hunwick, *Shari'a in Songhay* (Oxford, 1985)
- **Reliability: HIGH** — Primary correspondence [E]

**Leo Africanus (al-Hasan ibn Muhammad al-Wazzan)**
- *Della Descrittione dell'Africa* (*Description of Africa*), 1526
- Visited Songhai during the reign of Askia Muhammad (c. 1510)
- Eyewitness account of Timbuktu, Gao, and the Askia's court
- **Key content:** Court descriptions, city descriptions, trade
- **Translation:** John Pory (1600); see also Natalie Zemon Davis, *Trickster Travels* (2006)
- **Reliability: MEDIUM-HIGH** — Eyewitness but writing later from memory [E]

### Secondary Sources

**Hunwick, John O.**
- *Timbuktu and the Songhay Empire* (Brill, 1999)
- Definitive English translation and analysis of the *Tarikh al-Sudan*
- Essential for any study of Songhai
- **Reliability: HIGH** — Leading scholar, primary source translation

**Hunwick, John O.**
- *Shari'a in Songhay: The Replies of al-Maghili to the Questions of Askia al-Hajj Muhammad* (Oxford, 1985)
- Primary source translation and analysis of the al-Maghili correspondence
- Essential for understanding Muhammad's Islamic reform program
- **Reliability: HIGH** — Primary source scholarship

**Gomez, Michael A.**
- *African Dominion: A New History of Empire in Early and Medieval West Africa* (Princeton, 2018)
- Most important recent synthesis; fresh analysis of Muhammad's reign
- Centers African agency and provides updated interpretation
- **Reliability: HIGH** — Current scholarship

**Levtzion, Nehemia**
- *Ancient Ghana and Mali* (1973)
- Though focused on earlier empires, provides essential context for Songhai's rise
- **Reliability: HIGH** — Foundational scholarship

**Cissoko, Sekene Mody**
- *Tombouctou et l'Empire Songhay* (Editions NEA, 1975)
- French-language study from a Malian perspective
- **Reliability: HIGH** — African scholarly perspective

### Source Assessment

The primary sources for Muhammad Ture's reign are unusually rich for a medieval African ruler. The *Tarikh al-Sudan* and *Tarikh al-Fattash* — written by scholars connected to the Timbuktu intellectual establishment that Muhammad patronized — provide detailed narrative accounts. The al-Maghili correspondence offers direct insight into Muhammad's thinking about Islamic governance. Leo Africanus provides an eyewitness (if somewhat garbled) account of the Askia's court.

The main limitations are:

- **Scholarly bias:** Both chronicles were written by ulama who had obvious reasons to praise a ruler who favored them and condemn his predecessor who allegedly did not. Their portrayal of Sunni Ali as a persecutor and Muhammad as a savior should be read critically.
- **Succession conflict bias:** The violence of the succession after Muhammad's death may have influenced how the chronicles portrayed his reign and fall.
- **Gaps:** Muhammad's early life, family relationships, and inner personality are largely absent from the record.
